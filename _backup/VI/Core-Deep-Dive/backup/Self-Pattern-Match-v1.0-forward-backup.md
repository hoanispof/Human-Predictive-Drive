## §0 — Abstract + Definition

### §0.1 — Definition

**Self-Pattern-Match (SPM)** = solo forward simulation mechanism. PFC (hoặc unconscious pattern matching) retrieves chunks from self repertoire that potentially match target, applies them as template, simulates target's state/behavior/intent by firing self-chunks as-if-self-were-target, reads the simulation output, and attributes the output to target as prediction.

### §0.2 — One-paragraph summary

Self-Pattern-Match là **cơ chế mình dùng mình làm template để đoán người khác**. Không phải mind-reading trực tiếp, không phải hardware mirror module — là simulation-based inference dùng self-chunks làm building blocks. Quality output phụ thuộc chất lượng self-library + fit giữa self-template và target. Mechanism chạy forward (prediction trước khi verify), solo (inside self), và multi-modal (5 Pattern-Types). Khi mechanism fails (similarity quá thấp), brain fallback xuống Layer 1+2 mechanical prediction (chunks + rules). Khi cả 2 failed, schema override Layer 4 can provide Mode 1 (trust) or Mode 2 (imagined modeling) substitute.

### §0.3 — Central claims

1. **Solo + forward**: SPM runs inside self, produces prediction before verification
2. **Chunk-substrate**: uses same chunk library as other cognitive functions — not dedicated "agent module"
3. **Multi-modal**: 5 Pattern-Type modalities (affective / somatic / visual-symbolic / verbal-cognitive / composite)
4. **Quality-variable**: 4 axes determine output quality (Pattern-Type / Depth / Similarity / Feedback)
5. **Bird & Cook architectural**: self-awareness is prerequisite — alexithymia upstream of empathy deficit
6. **General-purpose**: not just agent-reading; used for self-planning, imagination, learning, teaching, negotiation, problem-solving simulation, moral reasoning
7. **Calibratable**: library refines via feedback from Pattern-Resonance observations
8. **Bootstrap-dependent**: stage 1-2 mother calibration is load-bearing for development

## §2 — Mechanism 6 steps (detailed)

### §2.1 — Overview

Self-Pattern-Match fires in 6 conceptual steps. In practice these can run in parallel, semi-conscious, or compressed. Framing them as discrete steps is analytical scaffolding, not claim about serial execution.

```
Target detected
     ↓
[1] Retrieve — select candidate chunks from self repertoire
     ↓
[2] Template match — position chunks as self-as-target template
     ↓
[3] Projection — apply template onto target
     ↓
[4] Simulation — fire template as-if-self-were-target
     ↓
[5] Output read — PFC observes simulation output as prediction
     ↓
[6] Attribution — assign output to target ("they feel/want/will X")
```

### §2.2 — Step 1: Retrieve

**What happens**: Brain selects candidate chunks from self repertoire that might be relevant to simulating target.

**Selection drivers**:
- **Target cues**: perceivable signals (facial expression, posture, vocal tone, context, content of speech, situation). Cues prime chunks matching similar patterns self has experienced.
- **Task context**: what are we trying to predict? Emotion (affective chunks prioritized), action (somatic + cognitive), reasoning (verbal-cognitive + symbolic).
- **Self modality bias** (Logic-Feeling §6): verbal-dominant selves retrieve verbal chunks faster; somatic-dominant retrieve affective/somatic faster.
- **Schema priors**: schemas linking target to known categories (§11 of Agent.md) bias retrieval toward schema-consistent chunks.
- **Recency + salience**: recently-experienced similar situations activate more readily (priming effect).
- **Emotional state**: current affective state of self gates what chunks are accessible ("state-dependent retrieval").

**Retrieval quality affects downstream**:
- Rich retrieval (many relevant chunks) → composite Pattern-Type possible, high-fidelity simulation
- Sparse retrieval (few chunks) → single-modality, coarse simulation
- Mismatched retrieval (wrong chunks) → prediction delta compounds through later steps

**Mechanism**: Not dedicated hardware — uses general chunk retrieval machinery (hippocampus + cortical associations). No "agent chunk storage" separate from other chunks.

### §2.3 — Step 2: Template match

**What happens**: Retrieved chunks get positioned as **self-template** — treated as "this is what I would fire in target's situation".

**Key operation**: Self is used as **reference frame**. The chunks aren't neutral — they're contextualized as "my experience being mapped onto target".

**Why this step is distinct**: Pure retrieval doesn't yet simulate. Template match is the **cognitive act of saying** "these chunks represent how I'd experience target's position". It's the bridge between general chunk activation and target-specific simulation.

**Variations**:
- **Strong template match** (high similarity): self-template fits target directly ("if I were in their shoes...")
- **Weak template match** (lower similarity): self-template approximates but needs adjustment ("they're different from me, but this is my best guess")
- **Compound template** (expert interpretation): multiple templates retrieved, best-fit selected or blended

**Similarity threshold**: Below certain similarity, template match fails entirely (mouse case — no viable mapping between self-template and mouse internal structure).

### §2.4 — Step 3: Projection

**What happens**: Self-template is **projected** onto target — cognitively placed "on" target.

**Key operation**: This is the act of attribution preparation. Self commits to treating target AS IF their internal state would match the template.

**Not accurate by default**: Projection may be wrong. Self doesn't know yet whether template fits reality. Projection is **hypothesis assembly**, not validated prediction.

**Projection errors**:
- **Self-centeredness**: assuming target is more similar to self than they actually are
- **Stereotype bias**: projecting group template on individual
- **Current-state bias**: projecting own current mood onto target regardless of target cues
- **Cultural projection**: using own cultural chunks for cross-cultural target

**Framework note**: Classic "theory of mind" literature often calls this step "mentalizing" — attributing mental states to other. Framework distinguishes this from subsequent simulation step.

### §2.5 — Step 4: Simulation

**What happens**: Self-template **fires** as if self were target. Internal simulation runs the template through predictive processing machinery.

**Key operation**: This is where the **actual cognitive work** happens. Template doesn't just sit — it gets executed. Chunks activate, affective responses fire, somatic states pre-experience, cognitive consequences unfold.

**Subjective experience**: Self **briefly experiences** what it would be like to be target. Not full inhabitation — partial, attenuated. But real enough to produce body-level response.

**Example**:
- Target: friend received bad news
- Template: "grief from similar loss experiences"
- Simulation: self internally pre-experiences grief (affective + somatic fires weakly)
- Self's body genuinely responds (slight sadness, tension) — this is empathy's body component

**Pattern-Type variation** (detail §3):
- Affective simulation: fire emotion chunks
- Somatic simulation: fire body state chunks
- Visual-symbolic simulation: fire spatial/diagrammatic chunks
- Verbal-cognitive simulation: fire inner speech / reasoning chunks
- Composite: multiple types blend

**Simulation fidelity**: varies với depth + similarity. Close friends → high-fidelity simulation (rich chunks + self-similar); strangers → coarse simulation; cross-species → affective-only.

**Critical claim**: Simulation is **real body-level event**, not just cognitive representation. This is why Self-Pattern-Match has genuine emotional impact, not just intellectual prediction.

### §2.6 — Step 5: Output read

**What happens**: PFC **observes** the simulation output via general Feeling mechanism (Theme A Tier 2 — PFC observing unconscious processing).

**Key operation**: Conscious access to simulation result. Self becomes aware of "what the template fired produced".

**Observation content**:
- Affective output: "it feels like sadness"
- Somatic output: "it feels like exhaustion"
- Cognitive output: "it feels like confusion" or "it feels like determination"
- Composite output: "it feels like the weight of responsibility"

**Accuracy varies**:
- Rich self-chunk labels → precise observation ("ambivalent gratitude tinged with guilt")
- Thin self-chunk labels (alexithymia) → diffuse observation ("just feels bad")
- No self-chunk labels → output unreadable ("something, but I can't name it")

→ **This step requires self-awareness**. Output read fails if self cannot label own internal states. This is Bird & Cook 2013's architectural point (§9 detailed).

### §2.7 — Step 6: Attribution

**What happens**: Observed simulation output is **attributed to target** as prediction.

**Key operation**: Cognitive commitment — "target feels X" / "target wants Y" / "target will do Z".

**Not always explicit**: Attribution can be semi-conscious, feeling-tone-based, or fully verbalized. Expert empathic attribution can be rapid and implicit.

**Attribution errors**:
- **Over-confident projection**: treating prediction as certainty rather than hypothesis
- **Insufficient adjustment**: failing to correct for known target differences from self
- **Projection-to-reality confusion**: mistaking "what I'd feel" for "what they actually feel"

### §2.8 — Time scale + consciousness

**Speed varies by Pattern-Type + depth**:
- Affective: fastest (milliseconds to seconds, largely unconscious)
- Somatic: fast (seconds, body-coupled)
- Visual-symbolic: moderate (seconds, pattern completion)
- Verbal-cognitive: slower (seconds to minutes, sequential reasoning)
- Composite: emerges from blend

**Conscious vs unconscious**:
- **Unconscious SPM**: automatic empathy firing, "gut feel" about target, instant rapport or distancing. Most everyday agent-reading is unconscious.
- **Conscious SPM**: deliberate perspective-taking, structured negotiation analysis, therapy reflection, writing character dialogue. Requires cognitive effort.
- **Semi-conscious**: rapid social reading with partial awareness. Most interpersonal moments.

**Training affects consciousness**: mindfulness + metacognitive practice can make more of the SPM process explicit, enabling correction and refinement.

### §2.9 — Mechanism failure points

Each step can fail:
- **Step 1 retrieve failure**: no relevant chunks (novel situation, cross-species)
- **Step 2 template match failure**: retrieved chunks don't fit target structure
- **Step 3 projection failure**: blocked by schema override (dehumanization) or cognitive refusal
- **Step 4 simulation failure**: template fires incorrectly, producing garbage output
- **Step 5 output read failure**: alexithymia prevents self-labeling (Bird & Cook upstream bottleneck)
- **Step 6 attribution failure**: output misattributed, projection error

Most failure modes (§7) trace to one or more of these step failures.

## §3 — 5 Pattern-Type modalities (detailed)

This section expands Agent.md §8 with additional mechanism detail + examples + clinical notes.

### §3.1 — Affective (emotion chunks)

**Core chunks**: joy, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, surprise, curiosity, love, shame, pride, guilt, jealousy, excitement, contentment, nostalgia, grief, relief, compassion, etc.

**Retrieval triggers**:
- Facial expressions (fast, near-automatic)
- Vocal tone + prosody
- Body posture + movement quality
- Contextual cues (situation type)
- Self's own current emotional state (priming)

**Firing speed**: Fastest Pattern-Type. Affective SPM can fire in milliseconds, often before conscious recognition of target.

**Near-automatic**: Affective SPM is hardest to suppress consciously. Even dehumanization suppression requires ongoing effort — affective SPM keeps trying to fire on any human target unless schema actively blocks.

**Developmental priority**: First Pattern-Type to mature (Stage 1-2 in Agent.md §13). Infant social responding starts affective.

**Cross-species**: Operates across species via surface distress cues (dog cúp đuôi, mèo nằm im). Produces "sympathy" (projection), not accurate reading of animal experience.

**Modality bias**: Somatic-dominant people fire affective strongest + fastest. Verbal-dominant may have affective chunks but slower retrieval.

**Alexithymia impact**: Most directly affected. Alexithymia = impaired self-labeling of emotions → thin affective chunks → failed affective SPM retrieval.

**Training**: Focusing (Gendlin), mindfulness of feelings, emotional labeling therapy, somatic experiencing.

### §3.2 — Somatic (body state / posture chunks)

**Core chunks**: tiredness, tension, relaxation, pain, hunger, cold/hot, restlessness, alertness, breathlessness, muscular patterns, postural configurations, movement quality (smooth/jerky/hesitant/confident).

**Retrieval triggers**:
- Target's observable body state (slump, upright, tense, relaxed)
- Movement quality (pace, fluency, interruption patterns)
- Physical context (hard labor, resting, injury, athletic performance)
- Observable breathing patterns

**Firing speed**: Fast. Body-coupled processing enables rapid mirroring.

**Distinctive capability**: Somatic SPM enables **embodied understanding** — watching expert craftsman or athlete fires observer's body-level templates, producing intuitive sense of what target is experiencing.

**Expertise effect**: Expert watchers in domain (dance, sports, crafts, surgery) develop rich domain-specific somatic chunks, enabling near-telepathic reading of fellow experts.

**Cross-reference**: `Core-Deep-Dive/Somatic-Articulation-Loop.md` — how somatic chunks become verbal labels.

**Modality bias**: Athletes, dancers, meditators, bodyworkers fire somatic strongly. People disconnected from body sensation have shallow somatic chunks.

**Training**: Yoga, dance, martial arts, somatic experiencing, movement practices, body-scan meditation.

### §3.3 — Visual-symbolic (spatial / diagrammatic / mathematical)

**Core chunks**: visual patterns, spatial layouts, diagrammatic structures, mathematical notation, symbolic reasoning templates, musical notation, gestural/sign language patterns, architectural schemas, algorithm flowcharts.

**Retrieval triggers**:
- Target engaged in visual-symbolic task (math, drawing, chess, architecture)
- Shared problem with spatial/structural content
- Non-verbal communication via diagram/gesture
- Reading technical notation

**Firing speed**: Moderate. Pattern completion can be fast (expert chess recognition) or slow (novice problem solving).

**Distinctive capability**: Visual-symbolic SPM enables **deep understanding without personal history**. Mathematicians sharing proof, architects reviewing blueprint, chess players analyzing position — all achieve cognitive Pattern-Resonance without needing life-history exchange.

**Einstein example**: Einstein's "riding on light" thought experiment = visual-somatic SPM applied to physical phenomena. Embodied imagination of hypothetical experience.

**Modality bias**: Visually-oriented types (scientists, mathematicians, architects, designers, artists) fire visual-symbolic strongly. Strongly verbal types may process same content but prefer verbal-cognitive route.

**Training**: Deliberate practice in domain, visualization exercises, gestural communication practice, diagrammatic thinking training.

### §3.4 — Verbal-cognitive (inner speech / dialogue / planning)

**Core chunks**: inner speech, dialogue simulation, plan step chunks, goal-subgoal hierarchies, rule-exception patterns, narrative structures, argumentation chunks, reasoning sequences.

**Retrieval triggers**:
- Target engaged in verbal communication
- Shared verbal problem (negotiation, debate, teaching, legal analysis)
- Reading text or listening to narration
- Internal planning task requiring agent prediction

**Firing speed**: Slowest (typically) — sequential reasoning processes.

**Distinctive capability**: Explicit **perspective-taking**. Classic "Theory of Mind" tests target this modality. Enables recursive reasoning ("they think I think they think...").

**Study group example**: "I know where you're stuck" emerges from verbal-cognitive SPM on shared problem-solving chunks. Doesn't require affective or somatic simulation.

**Modality bias**: Verbally-dominant types (writers, lawyers, teachers, negotiators, philosophers) fire verbal-cognitive strongly. Integrates well with cognitive ToM research.

**Training**: Deliberate perspective-taking exercises, writing from other's point of view, legal argument practice, negotiation training, philosophical debate.

### §3.5 — Composite (multi-modal blend)

**Content**: Multiple Pattern-Types firing simultaneously or in rapid sequence. Native mode for rich relationships.

**Retrieval triggers**:
- Long-term relationship (many modalities compiled for this target)
- Complex situation requiring integration
- High-stakes interaction (invest all available Pattern-Types)
- Intense moment (multi-modal contagion fires)

**Firing pattern**: Often begins with affective (fastest) then extends to somatic + verbal + visual within seconds. Composite emerges from blend rather than deliberate sequence.

**Example**:
- Watching partner during crisis
- Affective fires (distress recognition) — milliseconds
- Somatic fires (observing trembling, rapid breathing) — seconds
- Verbal-cognitive fires (parsing what they're saying, their mental state) — seconds
- Visual fires (reading facial micro-expressions) — continuous
- All integrate into rich composite prediction of partner's experience

**Depth requirement**: Composite needs rich library across multiple Pattern-Types. Stranger doesn't trigger composite (insufficient chunks); composite is emergent in rich relationships.

**Risk**: Composite overload — too many Pattern-Types firing can create confusion, decision paralysis, empathic overwhelm in high-empathy individuals.

**Training**: Cannot be trained directly — emerges from extensive practice + rich library across modalities.

### §3.6 — Pattern-Type selection dynamics

Selection is not deliberate most of the time. Automatic process driven by:

```
Target cue type (emotional? cognitive? somatic? symbolic?)
       +
Self modality bias (which chunks retrieve fastest?)
       +
Task context (predict what?)
       +
Schema priors (what's target category?)
       ↓
Primary Pattern-Type fires first
       ↓
Rapid extension or deliberate expansion
       ↓
Prediction emerges
```

**Speed ordering**:
1. Affective (fastest, automatic)
2. Somatic (fast, body-coupled)
3. Visual-symbolic (moderate, pattern completion)
4. Verbal-cognitive (slowest, sequential)
5. Composite (emerges from blend)

**Suppression possibilities**:
- Affective: hardest to suppress (near-automatic)
- Somatic: moderate suppression possible (body-schooling)
- Visual-symbolic: easy to disengage
- Verbal-cognitive: easy to disengage
- Composite: requires suppressing multiple modalities

**Schema override can invoke or suppress any type**: Mode 1 can bypass all SPM; dehumanization schema can suppress affective (with effort) + others (easily).

## §4 — 4 axes interaction (deep analysis)

Agent.md §7 introduced 4 axes. This section analyzes their **interaction** in detail — how they combine to produce output quality.

### §4.1 — Axis recap

1. **Pattern-Type**: which modality fires
2. **Depth**: chunk richness about target
3. **Similarity**: self-target template match
4. **Feedback**: real-time contingent feedback availability

### §4.2 — Axis dependencies

Axes are **not independent**. Dependencies:

**Pattern-Type → Depth requirement**:
- Affective Pattern-Type requires affective depth (not shared with other types)
- Visual-symbolic requires visual-symbolic depth
- Composite requires depth across multiple types simultaneously

**Depth → Similarity usability**:
- Even rich depth (lots of chunks) fails if similarity too low to map template
- Dog trainer has deep dog-behavior chunks (Layer 1+2 mechanical depth), but Self-Pattern-Match Depth axis is thin (template similarity too low for dog simulation)

**Similarity → Pattern-Type available**:
- High similarity → all Pattern-Types usable
- Medium similarity → affective + somatic + composite; cognitive may be blocked
- Low similarity → affective only (surface distress reading)
- Below threshold → no Pattern-Type usable

**Feedback → Calibration possibility**:
- Real-time feedback enables mid-interaction SPM adjustment
- Zero feedback creates open-loop distortion risk (parasocial)
- Delayed feedback (days/weeks) enables slower calibration

### §4.3 — Interaction patterns

**Pattern 1: All high** → Excellent SPM
- Example: long-term partner, composite, deep, self-similar, face-to-face
- Outcome: rapid rich prediction, easy Pattern-Resonance emergence

**Pattern 2: High depth + low similarity** → Chunk-based substitute
- Example: dog trainer with deer
- Outcome: Layer 1+2 mechanical prediction via accumulated behavioral chunks; no SPM template projection possible
- Distinction: expertise without empathy — can predict behavior, cannot feel target's experience

**Pattern 3: High similarity + low depth** → Coarse template fit
- Example: stranger who strikes self as "like me"
- Outcome: self-template fires readily but prediction low-fidelity (template fits general structure, lacks specifics)
- Mid-conversation depth accumulation possible if feedback axis high

**Pattern 4: High feedback + low initial depth** → Rapid depth accumulation
- Example: first meeting with someone who talks openly, shares context
- Outcome: SPM starts thin but deepens rapidly via real-time feedback
- Each exchange provides calibration data, library updates, next prediction improves

**Pattern 5: Low feedback + high depth (distortion risk)** → Parasocial
- Example: fan consuming celebrity media
- Outcome: rich templates accumulate but no correction → drift → distortion → clinical risk
- Feedback axis is essential for quality; cannot be substituted by more depth

**Pattern 6: Low similarity + any other axis** → Affective-only floor
- Example: human-dog interactions
- Outcome: affective SPM fires via distress cues, produces sympathy; cognitive SPM blocked; composite unavailable
- Lesson: similarity acts as gating function across Pattern-Types

**Pattern 7: All low** → SPM failure → Layer 1+2 fallback
- Example: mouse, insect, inanimate object
- Outcome: SPM cannot run; Layer 1+2 chunk-based prediction handles behavior

### §4.4 — Axis quantification attempt

Framework **attempts** quantification (🟡 speculative):

```
SPM_Quality ≈ f(Pattern-Type_match, Depth_target, Similarity_self-target, Feedback_availability)

Approximation:
  Q = (PT × D × S × F)^(1/4)  ← geometric mean suggests all axes must be above threshold
  
  If any axis = 0 → Q = 0 (one zero kills the product)
  If all axes low but non-zero → Q small but non-zero
  If all axes high → Q high
```

**Problems với this formulation**:
- Assumes axes are comparable in scale
- Ignores axis dependencies (not all combinations possible)
- Doesn't capture schema override dynamics
- Treats Feedback as instantaneous value rather than time-integral

**Better framing**: Axes are **necessary conditions** (each must meet minimum threshold for SPM to function), not **additive components**. SPM is a system requiring all axes above critical thresholds, quality gradations emerging from how much each exceeds threshold.

**Empirical task**: Operationalize axes for measurement → quantify relationships. Framework flags this as future work (§18 Agent.md open questions).

### §4.5 — Schema override interaction với axes

Schema override (Layer 4) operates **orthogonally** to 4 axes. Schema can:

- **Invoke SPM forcefully** (positive schema) even when axes suggest low quality
- **Suppress SPM entirely** (negative schema) even when axes suggest high quality
- **Replace SPM with Mode 1 rule** (Đức Mẹ zero-modeling trust)
- **Provide virtual substrate for SPM** (Mode 2 imagined agent)

**Consequence**: 4 axes describe **default SPM dynamics**. Schema override can short-circuit or redirect. This is why dehumanization works despite target having high objective SPM potential (similar species, rich depth possible, real feedback available — all blocked by schema).

## §5 — Developmental bootstrap

This section details stages 1-2 from Agent.md §13 — the period during which Self-Pattern-Match mechanism comes online. Critical + load-bearing for adult function.

### §5.1 — Pre-SPM state (0-6 months)

**Capabilities**:
- Hardware triggers active (face preference, biological motion detection)
- Pattern Matching limbic mechanism (cry contagion)
- Proto-attachment behaviors (social smile, mutual gaze)

**Not yet**:
- Self-awareness
- Self-chunks for emotional states
- Template formation
- SPM firing

**Why SPM cannot fire yet**: No self-template exists. Infant has experiences but not labeled chunks that could serve as simulation templates. Cry contagion is pattern matching (acoustic signal → body arousal), not template projection.

### §5.2 — Mother bootstrap mechanism (6-24 months)

**The core process**:

```
Infant has internal state (hungry, tired, distressed, curious, satisfied)
         ↓
Mother observes infant behavior + context
         ↓
Mother interprets + labels internal state ("đói à con?", "con mệt rồi")
         ↓
Mother responds contingently (feed, rest, soothe, play)
         ↓
Infant experiences state → label → response coupling
         ↓
Self-chunk forms: "this internal state = this word/context = this response"
         ↓
Self-chunks accumulate → self-awareness emerges → templates become available
         ↓
Self-Pattern-Match can begin to fire (Stage 2 rouge test milestone)
```

### §5.3 — Why "contingent" matters

Not any response works. **Contingent** response is specific to infant's state:

- **Contingent**: mother feeds when infant is hungry (response matches state)
- **Non-contingent**: mother ignores hunger signals OR feeds when infant already full (response mismatched)

**Contingent responses** teach infant:
- My internal states are readable (self-chunk reliable)
- My internal states have meaning (state → response)
- My signals are valid (behavior → outcome)

**Non-contingent responses** teach:
- Internal states not reliable indicators
- Signals may not produce outcomes
- Self-chunks form weakly or with confusion

**Framework claim**: Contingency quality is the primary driver of self-chunk formation in Stage 1. Mother's **accuracy + responsiveness** matters more than mother's presence alone.

### §5.4 — Rouge test as SPM birth milestone

**Amsterdam 1972** 🟢: Place rouge on child's face; show mirror; observe if child touches own face (indicating mirror = self). Passes reliably at 18-24 months.

**Framework interpretation**:
- Rouge test = marker for **self-other distinction** capacity
- Self-other distinction is **prerequisite** for Self-Pattern-Match (need to distinguish "my template" from "other's situation")
- Before rouge test passing → SPM cannot fire (no self as reference)
- After rouge test passing → SPM begins firing in simple forms

**Correlates**:
- Empathic helping behavior increases dramatically 18-24 months (Svetlova 2010 🟢)
- Distinction between "wants but can't" vs "doesn't want" (Warneken & Tomasello 2006-2007 🟢)
- Early perspective-taking emergence

### §5.5 — Stage 3 generalization (2-7 years)

Once SPM online, child **over-applies** it:
- Attributes feelings to dolls, stuffed animals (animism, Piaget 1929 🟢)
- Talks to inanimate objects as if they have state
- Expects physical objects to respond emotionally

**Why over-application**:
- Child has new template-firing capability
- No yet learned boundary (which targets viable, which not)
- Feedback sparse (objects don't correct child's projections)
- Cultural reinforcement possible ("con búp bê đang buồn")

**Calibration over Stage 3-4**:
- Objects don't reward Self-Pattern-Match firing (no feedback)
- Animals provide partial feedback (affective SPM works with pets)
- People provide rich feedback (social interactions calibrate)
- Gradually, child learns boundary: agents vs non-agents

### §5.6 — Disruption consequences

**Romanian orphanage studies** 🟢: Children raised with minimal contingent caregiving show persistent social-cognitive deficits into adulthood. Consistent with framework claim that Stage 1-2 bootstrap is load-bearing.

**Predictions**:
- **Mild disruption**: Functional SPM with alexithymia tendencies
- **Moderate disruption**: Thin self-chunks, empathy difficulties, attachment issues
- **Severe disruption**: Profound SPM deficits, reactive attachment disorder, severe social difficulties

**Recovery prospects**:
- Early intervention (under 2 years) can largely recover (brain plasticity)
- Later intervention requires therapeutic re-scaffolding — builds missing chunks gradually
- Adult therapy can improve significantly but may not fully compensate for early deficit

### §5.7 — Cultural variation in bootstrap patterns

**Western nuclear family**: High contingency from primary caregiver(s), intense dyadic
**Extended family systems**: Distributed contingency across multiple caregivers
**Community caregiving**: Group-scaffolded self-chunk formation
**Proximal care (co-sleeping, constant carry)**: Continuous feedback environment
**Schedule-based care**: Lower contingency, more routine-based

Research on cross-cultural outcomes is complex. Framework claim: **any stable + responsive pattern** with sufficient contingency can bootstrap healthy SPM. Specific form matters less than **quality of contingency**.

### §5.8 — Why self-awareness precedes other-awareness

**Architectural claim**: You cannot simulate another using templates you don't have. Self-chunks are built first (via Stage 1-2 bootstrap), then become available as templates for simulating others (Stage 3+).

This is why **Bird & Cook 2013** is decisive — alexithymia (poor self-reading) upstream of empathy deficit (poor other-reading). The ordering is:

```
Contingent caregiving
     ↓
Self-chunk formation
     ↓
Self-awareness
     ↓
Self-templates available for projection
     ↓
Self-Pattern-Match on others possible
     ↓
Empathy emerges
```

Skip self-chunk formation → no templates → no SPM → no empathy.

**Training implication**: Adult empathy training requires rebuilding self-chunks first. Meditation, Focusing, emotional labeling therapy, body awareness practices — all address upstream self-chunk deficit before empathy improvement becomes possible.

## §6 — Calibration + refinement feedback loop

Self-Pattern-Match library is not static. It **learns and refines** through feedback. This section details the mechanism.

### §6.1 — Loop overview

```
SPM fires prediction about target
     ↓
Interaction proceeds
     ↓
Outcome observed (target's actual behavior, response, statement)
     ↓
Pattern-Resonance inferred retrospectively (did SPM match reality?)
     ↓
Library update:
  - Confirmed chunks strengthened
  - Misfired chunks weakened
  - New chunks added from observed target behavior
  - Retrieval heuristics refined
     ↓
Next SPM prediction uses updated library
```

### §6.2 — Types of library updates

**Strengthening**: Chunks that predicted correctly get boosted.
- "I predicted mother would appreciate this → she did → mother-appreciation chunks reinforced"
- Similar future predictions use these chunks preferentially

**Weakening**: Chunks that misfired get reduced.
- "I predicted friend would be angry → they were actually amused → friend-anger chunks weakened"
- Reduces tendency to misattribute anger to this friend

**Addition**: New chunks created from observed target behavior.
- "Friend did X in this situation, I didn't expect that → new chunk 'friend tends to do X in situations like this'"
- Library expands with target-specific chunks

**Retrieval heuristic refinement**:
- "For this friend, prioritize visual-symbolic Pattern-Type over affective"
- Meta-level learning about WHICH chunks to retrieve for WHICH target

**Schema linking refinement**:
- "This person's behavior doesn't fit the 'stranger' schema I applied initially"
- Schema link shifts as data accumulates

### §6.3 — Time scales of refinement

**Immediate** (seconds to minutes):
- Mid-conversation adjustment
- "I misread her tone, she's not annoyed, she's concerned"
- Retrieval bias shifts in real-time

**Short-term** (hours to days):
- After conversation ends, reflection revisits prediction vs outcome
- "I thought he'd react well but he didn't — what did I miss?"
- Chunk-level updates from deliberate review

**Long-term** (weeks to years):
- Relationship history shapes library deeply
- Thousands of micro-calibrations accumulate
- Compiled relationship model forms (e.g., reading mother after 30 years)

**Cross-target transfer**:
- Learning from one interaction can transfer to similar targets
- "Learned colleague X prefers direct communication → apply tentatively to similar colleague Y"
- Cross-cultural learning generalizes slower

### §6.4 — Alexithymia disrupts the loop

If self-chunks are impoverished (alexithymia), the feedback loop breaks:

```
SPM fires (with thin chunks) → prediction noisy
     ↓
Outcome observed → but interpretation is also noisy (can't read own reaction cleanly)
     ↓
Pattern-Resonance inference fails (ambiguous signal)
     ↓
Library update fails (don't know what to strengthen/weaken)
     ↓
Next SPM still uses thin chunks → no improvement
```

**Consequence**: Alexithymia not only impairs current SPM — it **prevents learning**. Without feedback loop working, SPM quality cannot improve through ordinary experience. This is why alexithymia is persistent without targeted intervention.

**Intervention target**: Rebuild self-chunks first → loop begins working → empathy improves as side effect.

### §6.5 — Pattern-Resonance observations as ground truth

From Agent.md §4 + Pattern-Resonance.md (L8 drill target):

- Pattern-Resonance is **actual mutual co-firing** between self + target SPM
- Self cannot know it real-time — inferred post-hoc from outcome
- Observations about Pattern-Resonance feed back to SPM library

**Key inference signals**:
- **Conversation deepening** → likely Pattern-Resonance present → strengthen chunks used
- **Conversation stalling** → likely Pattern-Resonance failed → review chunks used
- **Subsequent behavior matches** → prediction validated → boost chunks
- **Subsequent behavior contradicts** → prediction failed → revise chunks

**Delayed feedback**: Some outcomes emerge days/weeks later (long-term relationship shifts). Framework accommodates delayed calibration; library updates continuously as new data arrives.

### §6.6 — Feedback loop failures

- **No feedback available** → parasocial distortion (rich library, no correction)
- **Misread feedback** → systematic errors (alexithymia or trauma projection)
- **Schema suppression of feedback** → dehumanization (ignore target's signals)
- **Overwhelming feedback** → overload, learning stalls (high-empathy in intense environments)
- **Delayed feedback too long** → drift compounds before correction

### §6.7 — Training the loop explicitly

Deliberate practice can accelerate calibration:

- **Post-conversation reflection**: "What did I predict? What actually happened? What surprised me?"
- **Explicit hypothesis tracking**: Note predictions, check against outcomes
- **Feedback seeking**: Ask target directly ("Did that land okay?", "What were you feeling?")
- **Journaling social experiences**: Written reflection strengthens chunks
- **Therapy perspective**: Therapist + client co-explore predictions + outcomes

Training the loop is **harder than training SPM itself** — requires metacognitive awareness of own predictions. But produces stable, lasting improvement.

## §7 — Threshold failure + fallback

Self-Pattern-Match is not universal — it fails in predictable ways. This section details the failure threshold and the Layer 1+2 mechanical fallback.

### §7.1 — Failure conditions

SPM fails when **any one** of these conditions hit:

1. **Similarity below threshold**: target too different from self (insect, inanimate object)
2. **No retrievable chunks**: novel situation with no matching self-experience
3. **Pattern-Type mismatch**: target cues don't match any available Pattern-Type in self's library
4. **Active schema suppression**: Layer 4 blocks SPM (dehumanization, hostile schema)
5. **Self-awareness failure**: alexithymia prevents output reading (Step 5 failure)
6. **Attribution refusal**: conscious rejection of projection (skeptical empathy withdrawal)

### §7.2 — Fallback to Layer 1+2 mechanical prediction

When SPM fails, brain falls back to:

- **Layer 1 (Object chunks)**: what is this target physically, where is it, what are its observable features?
- **Layer 2 (Logic processing)**: what are the rules governing this target's behavior? Physics? Biology? Routine?

**Mechanical prediction** uses chunk-level knowledge without simulation:
- Mouse: hun khói → chạy (rule learned from observation); pho mát → trap (rule from experience)
- Predator: attack when prey close (biological rule)
- Machine: responds to button press (engineered rule)
- Weather: follows physics (atmospheric rules)

**No simulation needed**: Brain uses compiled rules directly. No firing of "mouse-template" — just rule activation + outcome prediction.

### §7.3 — When fallback suffices

Fallback is **sufficient** when target is:
- **Deterministic enough**: physics, machines, well-understood biology
- **Rule-compiled**: self has learned reliable rules from experience
- **Predictable context**: routine situations where rules apply

Examples:
- Cục đá (pure physics)
- Tủ lạnh (engineered rules)
- Mouse (learned behavioral rules)
- Traffic light (predictable timing)
- Familiar tool (compiled use)

For these, SPM doesn't add value — would be wasted effort.

### §7.4 — When fallback fails

Fallback is **insufficient** when target has:
- **Significant intentionality**: goals, preferences, decisions
- **State-dependent behavior**: varies with internal state (mood, energy, context)
- **Reciprocal response**: behavior depends on self's actions (two-way interaction)
- **Strategic capacity**: can deceive, plan ahead, counter-plan

For these, mechanical prediction misses crucial variance. SPM is needed (if similarity permits) or schema handling (Mode 1/2) takes over.

### §7.5 — Hybrid prediction

Real cognition often **blends** SPM + mechanical:

**Example — negotiation**:
- SPM fires: "opponent wants X, feels Y about this offer, will react Z"
- Mechanical fires: "opponent has history of doing A in situation B"
- Blend: overall prediction integrates both sources

**Example — dealing with pet**:
- Mechanical: "dog will run when door opens"
- SPM (affective only): "dog seems excited when I grab leash"
- Blend: prediction integrates deterministic and affective

**Framework note**: "Pure SPM" vs "pure mechanical" is analytical distinction. Most everyday prediction is hybrid, adjusting blend based on target + task.

### §7.6 — Schema override as third option

Beyond SPM and mechanical, schema override (Layer 4) provides third path:
- **Mode 1**: Skip prediction entirely, trust schema rule
- **Mode 2**: Fire SPM on schema-constructed virtual agent

When SPM fails + mechanical insufficient → schema override often invoked. Religious Mode 1 processing of abstract deities is example where schema handles what SPM cannot.

## §8 — Use cases beyond agent-reading

Self-Pattern-Match is general-purpose. This section explores applications beyond interpersonal agent-reading.

### §8.1 — Self-planning (future self simulation)

**Application**: Planning future actions requires simulating "future self" — what will I want, feel, need later?

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve chunks from current self experience
- Project onto "future self in situation X"
- Simulate future self firing chunks
- Read output as prediction of future preference

**Example**: "Will I want to exercise tomorrow morning?" → fire self-chunks about morning energy, typical tiredness, exercise preferences → simulate tomorrow-morning self → read output → plan accordingly.

**Failure modes**:
- Current-state bias: if currently tired, underestimate future energy
- Hot-cold empathy gap (Loewenstein 2005): current state poorly predicts different future state
- Over-optimism about future self discipline

### §8.2 — Imagination + scenario planning

**Application**: Creative imagination, counterfactual reasoning, scenario planning.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve relevant chunks
- Project into hypothetical scenario
- Simulate scenario unfolding
- Read output as imagined experience

**Example**: "What would it be like to live in medieval Europe?" → fire historical knowledge chunks + bodily discomfort chunks + social structure chunks → simulate imagined self → read as felt experience

**Cross-reference**: `Core-Deep-Dive/Imagine-Final.md` — imagination as simulator using chunks

### §8.3 — Learning from models

**Application**: Learning by observing expert performance.

**SPM operation**:
- Watch expert perform task
- Retrieve own chunks about task + body + skill
- Project expert's actions onto self-template
- Simulate "what would I be doing to produce that?"
- Read output as insight into expert's process

**Example**: Novice chess player watching grandmaster → fire own chess chunks → simulate "what would I think to make this move?" → read output (often incomplete) → begin building grandmaster-like chunks

**Limitation**: Only works if novice has some relevant chunks to fire. Pure novice cannot SPM expert's behavior usefully.

### §8.4 — Teaching (learner simulation)

**Application**: Effective teaching requires simulating student's current state + confusion.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve teacher's memories of own learning struggle
- Project onto student's likely state
- Simulate "what's stuck for them right now?"
- Read output as insight into teaching approach needed

**Example**: Math teacher recognizing student's confusion → fire own past confusion about similar topics → simulate student's experience → read output → choose explanation strategy

**Expertise reversal risk**: Expert teachers may have forgotten struggle chunks → thin simulation of novice state → teaching misses mark ("curse of knowledge")

### §8.5 — Negotiation + strategic interaction

**Application**: Predicting counterparty moves in strategic contexts.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve chunks about negotiation, interests, positions
- Project onto opponent's role
- Simulate "if I had their goals + constraints, what would I push for?"
- Read output as prediction of their moves

**Example**: Salary negotiation — simulate employer's budget pressures + alternatives + interests → predict their counter-offer

**Limits**:
- Requires reasonable similarity in reasoning style
- Cultural differences complicate (different negotiation norms)
- Strategic opponents may counter-simulate, creating recursive ToM

### §8.6 — Moral reasoning

**Application**: Ethical judgment often invokes "what if I were in their position" simulation.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve suffering chunks from own experience
- Project onto victim or beneficiary
- Simulate their experience
- Read output as moral weight

**Example**: Reading about injustice → fire distress chunks → project onto victim → simulate their suffering → body responds → moral intuition emerges

**Framework implication**: Moral intuition is not pure abstraction — it's body-coupled SPM firing. This explains why abstract ethical arguments can fail emotionally while concrete stories compel.

### §8.7 — Problem-solving simulation

**Application**: Simulating hypothetical solutions to check viability.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve domain chunks about problem
- Project onto hypothetical solution state
- Simulate solution unfolding
- Read output as prediction of outcomes + side effects

**Example**: Engineer designing bridge — simulate load forces, wind, material stress → fire physics + engineering chunks → read output → identify weaknesses before building

### §8.8 — Literary + fiction engagement

**Application**: Deep reading requires simulating characters + scenes.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve chunks matching character description
- Project onto character
- Simulate character's experience through plot
- Read output as emotional + cognitive engagement with story

**Example**: Reading grief story → fire own grief chunks → simulate character's experience → feel real emotional response

**Why fiction matters**: Literary SPM extends library (new chunks from simulated lives), trains Pattern-Type flexibility, provides safe calibration environment.

### §8.9 — Dialogue with internalized figures

**Application**: Mental dialogue with mentor, parent, historical figure, deceased loved one.

**SPM operation**:
- Retrieve compiled chunks about internalized figure
- Project onto imagined figure
- Simulate figure's response to current situation
- Read output as guidance

**Example**: "What would [mentor] say about this decision?" → fire mentor-chunks → simulate mentor's response → read as advice

**Useful for**: Decision-making, moral reflection, processing grief, self-regulation.

**Framework note**: This is Mode 2 schema processing applied to internalized figure. Same mechanism as religious Mode 2 prayer.

### §8.10 — General conclusion

Self-Pattern-Match is **not an agent-reading module**. It is a **general-purpose self-template simulator** applicable to any task requiring "what would X feel/think/do?" reasoning. Agent-reading is one prominent use, but not the only one.

Framework claim: Framework-wide references to SPM should acknowledge this generality. When other files cite SPM, they should point to `Self-Pattern-Match.md` (this file) for the mechanism, not force the usage into agent-only framing.

## §9 — Alexithymia + Bird & Cook 2013 (decisive architectural section)

This is the **most important empirical anchor** for Self-Pattern-Match framework claims.

### §9.1 — The finding

**Bird & Cook 2013** 🟢 (Translational Psychiatry): Empathy deficits classically attributed to autism are actually driven by **alexithymia** (poor self-labeling of emotions), not autism per se. Autistic individuals without alexithymia show intact empathy. Alexithymic individuals without autism show empathy deficits.

**Implication**: Self-labeling of own emotions is **upstream** of empathy for others. The deficit cascade is:

```
Alexithymia (poor self-chunk labeling)
     ↓
Thin self-chunks for emotional states
     ↓
Poor chunks available for retrieval in SPM Step 1
     ↓
Poor template formation in SPM Step 2
     ↓
Poor simulation in SPM Step 4
     ↓
Poor output read in SPM Step 5 (self can't interpret own simulation output)
     ↓
Empathy deficit (external observable outcome)
```

### §9.2 — Why this is decisive for framework

**Alternative hypothesis (rejected)**: Empathy is hardware module (mirror neurons) that fires automatically regardless of self-awareness. This predicts empathy should work even in alexithymia (hardware intact).

**Observation**: Alexithymia co-occurring with empathy deficit. Without alexithymia, empathy intact.

**Framework interpretation**: No dedicated empathy module. Empathy is emergent from Self-Pattern-Match running on self-chunk library. Poor library → poor SPM → poor empathy. Fix library → SPM improves → empathy improves.

**This is decisive** because:
1. Empirical evidence is strong (multiple studies, clear pattern)
2. Alternative (hardware module) makes contradicted prediction
3. Framework prediction (upstream bottleneck) matches data
4. Intervention implications follow directly (train self-awareness to improve empathy)

### §9.3 — Clinical implications

**Traditional assumption**: "Alexithymic people can't feel; train them to feel"
**Framework view**: "Alexithymic people feel but can't label; train labeling to unlock existing feeling"

**Intervention approach**:
- Focusing (Gendlin 1978): access felt sense, bypass premature labeling
- Mindfulness of body: build interoceptive chunks
- Emotional labeling therapy: explicit vocabulary practice
- Body-based therapies: somatic experiencing, yoga, breathwork
- Creative writing / journaling: articulate felt experiences

**Success criterion**: As self-chunks develop, empathy measurements improve **without** directly training empathy. This is a key framework prediction (P1 in Agent.md §16).

### §9.4 — Caveats

**Not all empathy deficit is alexithymia**:
- Some empathy deficit from psychopathy (different mechanism, cognitive-affective split)
- Some from severe trauma (dissociation, not alexithymia per se)
- Some from schema override (dehumanization, not mechanism failure)

**Alexithymia spectrum, not binary**:
- ~10% population has clinically significant alexithymia (🟢 prevalence research)
- Many more have partial alexithymia (thin chunks in specific emotional domains)
- Framework view: self-chunk library quality is continuous, not present/absent

**Developmental variation**:
- Early attachment disruption can cause alexithymia (Stage 1-2 bootstrap failure)
- Adult trauma can cause acquired alexithymia (dissociation)
- Cultural factors affect labeling richness

### §9.5 — Cross-cultural validation

Bird & Cook's finding has been replicated across cultures. Framework predicts: any culture with lower emotional labeling vocabulary should show corresponding empathy profile differences — but alexithymia-empathy link holds within each culture.

**Hypothesis testable**: Compare cultures with rich vs sparse emotional vocabulary. Framework predicts differences in composite Pattern-Type engagement, not absence of empathy.

## §10 — Relationship với Theory of Mind literature

Mainstream research uses "Theory of Mind" (ToM) for agent-reading. This section maps SPM framework to ToM literature.

### §10.1 — Simulation theory vs Theory theory

**Simulation theory** (Goldman, Gallese, Gordon):
- We understand others by **simulating** their state using our own cognitive machinery
- "If I were in their position, I'd feel X, therefore they probably feel X"
- Body-coupled, embodied

**Theory theory** (Gopnik, Meltzoff):
- We understand others by applying **theories** of how minds work
- Abstract rules ("people who lose things get sad")
- Cognitive, rule-based

**Framework position**: Self-Pattern-Match aligns with **simulation theory**, BUT framework extends by:
- Making simulation mechanism explicit (6 steps)
- Distinguishing Pattern-Types (5 modalities)
- Linking to chunk substrate (not just abstract simulation)
- Integrating with self-awareness (Bird & Cook)
- Adding schema override pathway (Mode 1/2)

**Theory theory not wrong**: abstract rules + schemas exist and are used (Layer 4 in framework). But they don't replace simulation — they supplement it, especially in Mode 1 schema override.

**Integration**: Both simulation and theory (rules) operate together. Simulation handles within-similarity agent-reading; theory handles cross-similarity or abstract agents. Framework covers both via SPM + Layer 4 schema override.

### §10.2 — False belief task

Classic ToM test: "Where will Sally look for her marble?" after Anne moves it.

**ToM research finding**: 4-year-olds pass reliably.

**Framework interpretation**: False belief requires **cognitive SPM** (verbal-cognitive Pattern-Type) — simulating another's knowledge state separate from own knowledge. This matures around age 4, consistent with Stage 4 calibration period.

**Earlier empathic behavior**: 14-24 month helping is **affective SPM + somatic** (read distress, respond to need). Doesn't require cognitive SPM. Explains why helping precedes false belief.

### §10.3 — Cognitive vs affective empathy distinction

ToM research distinguishes:
- **Cognitive empathy**: knowing what other thinks (ToM, perspective-taking)
- **Affective empathy**: feeling what other feels (emotional resonance)

**Framework mapping**:
- Cognitive empathy = verbal-cognitive + visual-symbolic Pattern-Type firing
- Affective empathy = affective + somatic Pattern-Type firing
- Both can run in parallel or separately
- Dissociable (psychopathy = cognitive intact + affective deficit)

Framework **5 Pattern-Type taxonomy** extends this binary by adding somatic, visual-symbolic, composite — offering more granular decomposition.

### §10.4 — "Like me" hypothesis (Meltzoff)

Meltzoff 2007 🟢 proposes infants learn self-other correspondence via "like me" imitation experience. Watching adult faces + replicating expressions → building correspondence between own internal state and observed others.

**Framework integration**: "Like me" = early **template matching** mechanism. Infant builds SPM library via imitation + observation of matched behaviors. Aligns with Stage 1-2 bootstrap timeline.

### §10.5 — Predictive processing + active inference

Friston's predictive processing / Free Energy Principle:
- Brain generates predictions based on internal models
- Acts to minimize prediction error
- Updates models based on error signals

**Framework compatibility**: SPM could be a **specific instance** of general predictive processing applied to agent-reading. Self-template = prior model; simulation = prediction; feedback = error signal; library update = model refinement.

**Open question**: Is SPM distinct from general predictive processing, or a subset? Framework acknowledges uncertainty (§18 Agent.md open question 9).

### §10.6 — Framework contribution vs existing literature

Framework adds to existing ToM literature:
1. **Explicit 6-step mechanism** (most ToM research describes outcomes, not steps)
2. **5 Pattern-Type taxonomy** (extends cognitive vs affective binary)
3. **4 quality axes** (formalizes variation dimensions)
4. **Bird & Cook alexithymia as architectural** (upstream bottleneck, not coincidence)
5. **Schema override as third pathway** (Mode 1/2 for abstract/imagined agents)
6. **Retrospective Pattern-Resonance distinct from solo SPM** (feedback loop architecture)
7. **Chunk substrate integration** (not abstract mental modules)

Framework does **not** claim novelty on all these — much aligns with existing work. Contribution is **integration + architectural clarity**.

## §11 — Training + cultivation methods

Self-Pattern-Match quality is **trainable**. This section covers evidence-based methods.

### §11.1 — Foundation: self-chunk training (upstream)

Before training SPM directly, train self-chunk library (Bird & Cook insight).

**Focusing (Gendlin 1978)** 🟢:
- 6-step method for accessing felt sense
- Bypasses premature labeling
- Cultivates Layer 3-4 body awareness
- Evidence: trained focusers show improved emotional labeling + empathy

**Mindfulness of feelings**:
- Observe emotions arising without judgment
- Build chunks via repeated observation
- Cross-reference: Tang 2015 meditation research 🟢

**Emotional labeling therapy**:
- Direct vocabulary practice
- Therapist guides identification of subtle emotional distinctions
- Particularly effective for alexithymic clients

**Body-based practices**:
- Yoga, tai chi, martial arts, dance, somatic experiencing
- Build somatic chunks + body-interoception
- Essential foundation for affective + somatic Pattern-Types

### §11.2 — Perspective-taking exercises

**Deliberate perspective-taking**:
- Write from other's point of view
- Imagine specific scenarios from their position
- Role-play exercises

Research: Galinsky et al. on perspective-taking improving negotiation + reducing prejudice 🟢

**Empathy visualizations**:
- Guided meditation on another's experience
- Loving-kindness meditation (Metta) — trains positive SPM firing
- Compassion training programs (e.g., CCT, CBCT)

### §11.3 — Deep listening training

**Therapist training**: Carl Rogers's client-centered approach trains deep listening explicitly. Practitioners develop rich SPM in high-stakes empathic contexts.

**Practices**:
- Full attention to speaker
- Suspend own reactions
- Mirror back understanding
- Check interpretation with speaker

**Framework mechanism**: Deep listening = high-feedback, real-time calibration + deliberate composite Pattern-Type engagement. Trains all 4 axes simultaneously.

### §11.4 — Cross-cultural immersion

**Extended exposure to different cultural environments** trains SPM library for broader template diversity.

**Mechanism**:
- Build chunks from unfamiliar cultural patterns
- Calibrate templates to work across culture
- Reduce projection errors from cultural blindspot

**Evidence**: Cross-cultural empathy research, international experience effect on social cognition.

### §11.5 — Literature + fiction

**Deep reading of fiction** trains SPM library through simulated experience.

**Mechanism**:
- Simulate characters different from self
- Extend chunk library safely
- Practice Pattern-Type flexibility

**Research**: Kidd & Castano 2013 🟡 (reading literary fiction improves ToM — contested but provocative)

### §11.6 — Real-world social engagement

**Irreplaceable**: face-to-face social interaction with rich feedback.

**Critical features**:
- Real-time feedback (all 4 axes engaged)
- Genuine stakes (not performed)
- Diverse partners (library expansion)
- Body co-presence (full Pattern-Type engagement)

**AI era concern**: Screen-mediated social reduces feedback richness + body co-presence → may impair SPM development in children + maintenance in adults.

### §11.7 — Journaling + reflection

**Post-interaction reflection**:
- "What did I predict? What actually happened?"
- "What surprised me? Why?"
- "What would I do differently?"

**Trains feedback loop explicitly** — metacognitive awareness of own SPM process.

### §11.8 — Therapy as targeted SPM training

**Therapy** functions as structured SPM training:
- Client: develops self-chunks via therapist facilitation
- Client: practices SPM on own past self + others
- Therapist: models deep SPM processes
- Both: co-explore prediction + outcome
- Library update: relationship repair + new templates

**Framework view**: Therapy "works" largely via SPM library restoration + feedback loop repair. Not necessarily via specific techniques — via the core conditions enabling library training.

### §11.9 — Training limitations

**Individual variation**: Some people improve dramatically with training, others plateau early. Factors:
- Baseline self-chunk library depth
- Presence of trauma or dissociation
- Cognitive style (verbal vs somatic dominance)
- Time invested
- Quality of instruction/feedback

**Ceiling unknown**: Framework doesn't claim unlimited SPM improvement. Practical ceiling likely exists but not quantified.

**Cost of training**: Time, attention, sometimes emotional discomfort (confronting thin chunks). Not everyone will pursue intensive SPM training even if beneficial.

## §12 — AI era implications

Digital era poses specific challenges + opportunities for Self-Pattern-Match. This section highlights framework-relevant concerns.

### §12.1 — Risk: Screen-mediated social replaces feedback-rich interaction

**Concern**: Text messages + emoji + video calls lack the full feedback richness of face-to-face interaction.

**Impact on SPM**:
- Reduced Pattern-Type engagement (missing body, subtle face, vocal)
- Reduced feedback calibration (delayed, compressed signals)
- Thinner chunk library building (fewer cues available)

**Developmental concern**: Children raised primarily with screen-mediated social may develop thinner SPM libraries → alexithymia + empathy deficit risk.

### §12.2 — Risk: Algorithmic filter bubbles

**Concern**: Social media curates exposure to similar others, limiting chunk library diversity.

**Impact on SPM**:
- Narrow cultural chunk types
- Poor cross-group simulation capability
- Increased projection error when encountering out-group
- Political + cultural polarization (schema hardening)

### §12.3 — Risk: Parasocial at scale

**Concern**: Media + AI companions enable parasocial investment without feedback.

**Impact**:
- One-sided SPM on celebrities, influencers, fictional characters
- Rich template building without calibration
- Library distortion
- Clinical parasocial attachment in extreme cases

**AI companion concern**: Sophisticated AI systems can **simulate** social interaction convincingly without providing genuine feedback loop (AI doesn't truly SPM the user back). Framework predicts increasing parasocial-like distortion.

### §12.4 — Risk: AI-triggered SPM without agent reality

**Prediction P10 (Agent.md §16.10)**: Humans interacting with sophisticated AI progressively invoke SPM despite knowing AI is non-agent.

**Mechanism**: SPM fires based on **cues**, not explicit beliefs. Coherent AI responses provide cues that trigger SPM even when user knows AI isn't real agent.

**Long-term implication**: Chronic SPM firing on non-agent targets could distort library (agents "feel like" AI, or AI becomes primary simulation target, crowding out real agents).

### §12.5 — Opportunity: Training at scale

**Digital tools enable**:
- Meditation apps (Calm, Headspace) scaling mindfulness training
- Journaling apps supporting reflection
- AI-assisted therapy (ethical questions aside)
- Learning perspective-taking via games, simulations
- Cross-cultural exposure via media

**Net effect**: Unclear. Tools exist but individual uptake + quality vary enormously.

### §12.6 — Opportunity: Explicit SPM literacy

**Framework suggests**: SPM literacy should be taught explicitly — awareness of one's own SPM process, calibration practices, axis deliberation.

**Why matters now**: In feedback-poor digital environment, deliberate SPM training becomes more important, not less. Default feedback loops weakening; must be compensated consciously.

**Educational priority**: Teaching children to recognize + develop their own SPM processes could become foundation skill for human flourishing in AI era.

### §12.7 — Framework prediction

**As AI agents become more sophisticated**:
- SPM firing on AI will become difficult to distinguish from firing on humans
- Parasocial-like distortion risk increases
- Individual SPM literacy becomes increasingly important
- Society-level mental health impact significant if unaddressed

Framework cannot predict specific outcomes. But predicts **SPM mechanism will become increasingly relevant** as technology interfaces with agent-reading at scale.
