## §2 — DRAFT COMMENT (Phase 4)

### Draft v4 (3 structural points + validation)

```
  ─────────────── DRAFT V3 ───────────────

Your honest uncertainty throughout — the inconclusive
foot-in-the-door evidence, the evals skepticism, your candor
about disclosure — makes this a useful foundation for examining
what weak regulations can realistically accomplish. Three
structural observations that might shift the frame:

First, the ban may be structurally infeasible, not just
politically difficult. The coordination problem here is more
severe than nuclear non-proliferation. Nuclear weapons involved
~9 state actors, carried no consumer benefit, and had Hiroshima
as trigger event. AI development involves billions of participants
across every sector, provides genuine daily value, and poses
risks that unfold gradually. Each actor — individual, company,
nation — gains competitive advantage from continued development.
If the US pauses, China continues — and both know it. This
isn't a buy-in problem solvable with persuasion — it's a
coordination failure where defection is rewarded at every scale.

Second, the risk most worth regulating may not be extinction.
The post evaluates all eight policies against a single axis:
extinction risk from misaligned ASI. But the risk that's
currently observable — and that weak regulations can actually
address — is the growing stratification between AI-skilled and
AI-unskilled populations. This follows the pattern of every major
technological transition: the Industrial Revolution didn't cause
human extinction, but it created massive upheaval before
institutions adapted. Evaluating whistleblower protections against
extinction risk makes them look marginal; evaluating them against
stratification and arms-race dynamics makes them look significantly
more valuable.

Third, "ban until proven safe" has a circularity problem. Proving
safety requires knowledge that only emerges from building and
using the technology. Your own evals analysis supports this: "you
can't just evaluate models, you have to actually figure out how
to make them safe," and evals increasingly fail as models detect
testing. Ban → can't build → can't generate safety knowledge →
can't prove safe → can't lift ban.

These three points converge: if a ban is structurally infeasible,
the most actionable risk is social rather than existential, and
safety knowledge requires iteration — then weak regulations
matter for a different reason than the post explores. Not as
Overton window shifts (evidence inconclusive, as you note), but
as infrastructure for iterative safety: evals generate capability
data, disclosure enables monitoring, whistleblower protections
allow course correction. They're valuable because they are the
mechanism through which both safety knowledge and social
adaptation accumulate.

The strongest counter to all of this: if the first major failure
is extinction-level, there's no iteration, and the stratification
frame is beside the point. But even precautionary regulation
depends on evaluation capacity built through experience with the
technology — which leads back to the iterative path.

Which is precisely why analysis like this matters. In practice,
criticism and development aren't alternatives — they run
simultaneously. People raise concerns about AI risk while
continuing to build with it, and that tension isn't contradiction;
it's how safety standards have always evolved. Neither extreme
works: halting everything until every concern is resolved, or
racing forward without oversight. What works is serious
examination feeding back into how the technology is governed —
which is what this post does.

  ─────────────── END DRAFT V4 ───────────────
```
