## §1 — AUTISM: FRAMING + HETEROGENEITY

### §1.1 — Spectrum, not binary

```
🟢 DSM-5 ASD (APA 2013):

  2 DOMAINS, 3 LEVELS:

  Domain A — Social Communication + Social Interaction:
    A1: Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
    A2: Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors
    A3: Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships
    → ALL 3 criteria required

  Domain B — Restricted, Repetitive Behaviors (RRBs):
    B1: Stereotyped/repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech
    B2: Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines
    B3: Highly restricted, fixated interests
    B4: Hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input ← NEW in DSM-5
    → AT LEAST 2/4 criteria required

  3 Levels (support needs):
    Level 1: "Requiring support"
    Level 2: "Requiring substantial support"
    Level 3: "Requiring very substantial support"

  ⚠️ "Level" ≠ "severity" ≠ "functioning"
  → Level reflects SUPPORT NEEDS, not intelligence or capability
  → Same person may be Level 1 in Domain B, Level 2 in Domain A
```

### §1.4 — Genetics

```
🟢 Heritability — VERY high:

  Twin studies:
    → Bailey et al. 1995 (Psychological Medicine 25(1):63-77):
      MZ concordance 60% autism, 92% broader phenotype
      DZ concordance 0% autism, 10% broader phenotype
    → Tick et al. 2016 (JCPP 57(5):585-595): meta-analysis ALL twin studies
      Heritability: 64-91% depending on prevalence assumptions

  Risk genes identified:
    → Satterstrom et al. 2020 (Cell 180(3):568-584): largest exome study
      102 RISK GENES at FDR ≤0.1 from 35,584 samples (11,986 ASD)
      3 functional clusters: gene expression regulation (58),
        neuronal communication (24), cytoskeletal (9)
      49 overlap with severe neurodevelopmental delay, 53 more ASD-specific

  Common variation = primary driver:
    → Gaugler et al. 2014 (Nature Genetics 46:881-885):
      Narrow-sense heritability ~52.4% from COMMON INHERITED variation
      De novo mutations = ~2.6% population variance (important per-individual,
        but NOT the main driver at population level)

  → Genetics = POLYGENIC (1000+ common variants of small effect + 102+ rare variants)
  → No "autism gene" — hundreds of genes × interaction × environment
  → = CONSISTENT with framework: configuration DIFFERENT = many parameters differ SIMULTANEOUSLY

### §1.5 — Brain: IDIOSYNCRATIC connectivity

```
🟢 ORIGINAL model — underconnectivity:
  → Just et al. 2004 (Brain 127(8):1811-1821):
    Functional connectivity between cortical areas consistently LOWER in ASD
    Proposed frontal-posterior underconnectivity

🟡 REFINED — local over + long-range under:
  → Courchesne & Pierce 2005 (Current Opinion Neurobiology 15(2)):
    Frontal cortex EXCESSIVELY connected locally
    POORLY synchronized with distant regions

🟢 CURRENT — IDIOSYNCRATIC patterns:
  → Hahamy et al. 2015 (Nature Neuroscience 18(2):302-309):
    ASD brains show IDIOSYNCRATIC distortions of connectivity
    Increased INTER-INDIVIDUAL VARIABILITY is the core feature
    Previous over/under findings = this heterogeneity mischaracterized

  → Current consensus:
    Simple "long-range under + local over" = OUTDATED
    Actual finding = EACH autistic brain = unique connectivity pattern
    = CONSISTENT with framework: each autistic person = unique configuration

🟡 Early brain overgrowth:
  → Courchesne et al. 2001, 2003 (JAMA 290(3)):
    Brain enlargement 2-4 yo. Sudden increase 1-14 months.
    Cerebral white matter +18%, cerebellar +39%
  → Current: real but MORE NUANCED and SUBGROUP-SPECIFIC
    Overgrowth ≠ universal to all autistic individuals

🟡 Amygdala:
  → Faster growth 6-24 months (Cohen's d = 0.56 by 12 months)
  → Right amygdala significantly larger in ASD (Hedges' g = 0.44)
  → Significant HETEROGENEITY across studies

```

    = OLDEST and MOST REPLICATED biomarker in autism (~60+ years)

  → Puts et al. 2017 (Autism Research 10(4):608-619):
    GABA reduced in SENSORIMOTOR cortex (MRS MEGA-PRESS)

    Region-specific (occipital GABA = normal)

  → Rubenstein & Merzenich 2003 (Genes, Brain & Behavior 2(5)):
    Increased excitation/inhibition ratio in key neural systems
    Influential framework but OVERSIMPLIFIED
  → Nelson & Valakh 2015 (Neuron 87(4)):
    Homeostatic compensation complicates — E/I imbalance NOT linear
    Increased E/I NOT necessarily = increased firing
  → 2025: mGlu5 receptor ~15% lower (AJP) — potential mechanism

🔴 Oxytocin:
  → Meta-analysis 2024 (54 trials, N=2,593): INCONSISTENT effects

  → = Hypothesis NOT yet supported as therapeutic target

### §2.2 — MIXED pattern: hyper AND hypo per-modality

```
🟢 NOT "more sensitive" or "less sensitive" — MIXED:

  Tavassoli et al. 2014 (Autism 18(4):428-432):

### §2.3 — Reduced HABITUATION = key mechanism

```
🟢 Green et al. 2015 (JAMA Psychiatry 72(8):778-786):

  Study: fMRI sensory processing in ASD with/without SOR
  Finding: ASD+SOR group showed DECREASED NEURAL HABITUATION
    in sensory cortices AND amygdala

  WHAT THIS MEANS:
    → Typical brain: repeated stimulus → response GRADUALLY DECREASES (habituation)
    → Sound of air conditioner: first 10 seconds = notice. 5 minutes = gone.
    → Autistic brain (with SOR): response DOES NOT DECREASE
    → Sound of air conditioner: 5 minutes = STILL firing. 30 minutes = STILL FIRING.

  FRAMEWORK MAPPING:
    Body-Feedback-Mechanism.md §2: Sensory-Driven input
    → Typical: sensory input → body-feedback → HABITUATE → signal fades
    → ASD+SOR: sensory input → body-feedback → NO HABITUATION → ACCUMULATES
    → Each additional input ADDS to cumulative load
    → Over time: body-feedback OVERLOAD → PFC capacity exhausted → meltdown/shutdown

  → Reduced habituation = SIMPLEST mechanism explaining sensory overload
  → No need for "overly sensitive" (hyper) — just need body to NOT REDUCE response
  → = Cumulative, NOT threshold — each additional second of input = additional load
  → = WHY "fine in the morning, meltdown by afternoon" = ACCUMULATION
```

## §3 — MULTISENSORY INTEGRATION

### §3.1 — Temporal Binding Window: twice as wide

```
🟢 Foss-Feig et al. 2010 (Experimental Brain Research 203(2):381-389):

  FLASH-BEEP ILLUSION paradigm:
    → Light flash + beep sound presented at varying time intervals
    → Brain "binds" them as 1 event if within temporal window

  Result:
    Typical development: TBW ≈ 300ms
    Autism: TBW ≈ 600ms

    = TWICE AS WIDE temporal window

  WHAT THIS MEANS:
    → ASD brain treats 2 separate events as "together" even when
      separated by up to 600ms (instead of 300ms)
    → OR: ASD brain FAILS to separate events that occur within 600ms
    → = More events get "bound" together → more cross-modal interference
    → = Harder to parse rapid sequences (like speech + lip movement)
```

### §3.2 — Speech-specific deficit

```
🟢 Stevenson et al. 2014 (Journal of Neuroscience 34(3):691-697):

  KEY FINDING: TBW enlargement NOT uniform across all stimuli:
    → Simple stimuli (flash-beep): moderate enlargement
    → SPEECH stimuli: LARGEST enlargement
    → = Complex, socially-relevant stimuli suffer MOST

  Perceptual binding strength correlated with:
    → Low-level temporal processing abilities
    → = Foundation matters — low-level timing → high-level integration

  IMPLICATION:
    → Speech = inherently audiovisual (lip movement + sound + facial expression)
    → Wider TBW for speech → audiovisual speech binding DIFFERENT
    → = Difficulty following conversation in group (multiple audio-visual streams)
    → = Preference for text-based communication (removes temporal binding requirement)
    → = Consistent with Sasson 2017: bias disappears with TEXT-only

🟢 Baum, Stevenson & Wallace 2015 (Progress in Neurobiology 134:140-160):
  Comprehensive review: ROBUST and CONSISTENT multisensory integration impairment
  Neuroimaging: failure to activate large-scale cortical networks
  Reduced long-range functional connectivity supports integration differences
```

### §3.3 — Framework: Social = inherently multi-modal

```
🟡 07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md §1.1:

  "There is no meaningful single-channel social event."

  Social event = MULTIPLE channels fire SIMULTANEOUSLY:
    → Visual (face + posture + gesture)
    → Auditory (voice + intonation + content)
    → Tactile (handshake, hug, proximity)
    → Interoceptive (emotional state mirroring)
    → Olfactory (pheromones, familiar scent)

  4 binding mechanisms (07-Social-Recognition-Arc §6):
    ① Temporal co-occurrence + Hebbian binding
    ② Multisensory neurons (superior colliculus + STS)
    ③ Default Mode Network scaffolding
    ④ Amygdala emotional tag as binding cue

  IF 1+ mechanism DIFFERENT (not absent — DIFFERENT):
    → Social chunk compilation CASCADE different
    → Social chunks compile with DIFFERENT temporal resolution
    → = "Social difficulty" = DOWNSTREAM of integration difference
    → = NOT "social brain broken" — integration parameters DIFFERENT
```

### §3.4 — From integration to social: the cascade

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK SYNTHESIS:

  STEP 1: TBW wider (~600ms vs ~300ms)
    → Brain binds more events into single percept
    → OR fails to separate rapidly alternating events

  STEP 2: Social events = rapid multi-modal streams
    → Conversation: words at ~150ms intervals + facial expressions at ~200ms
    → Group conversation: multiple speakers overlapping
    → Each stream requires temporal binding PRECISION

  STEP 3: Wider TBW → DIFFERENT social chunk compilation
    → Nuances that depend on precise timing → missed or blurred
    → "Sarcasm" = requires detecting MISMATCH between tone (audio) and content
      within very narrow temporal window → harder with wider TBW
    → "Social cues" = often multi-modal and time-dependent

  STEP 4: Different social chunks → different Self-Pattern-Modeling predictions (§5)
    → Self-Pattern-Modeling runs on OWN chunk library
    → Different social chunks → different predictions about others
    → Neurotypical predicts using neurotypical social chunks → fails for autistic person
    → Autistic person predicts using own social chunks → fails for neurotypical
    → = BIDIRECTIONAL failure (double empathy, §5.4)

  → "Social deficit" reframe: INTEGRATION DIFFERENCE → COMPILATION DIFFERENCE
    → DOWNSTREAM cascade, not primary "social brain" failure
```

## §4 — PREDICTIVE PROCESSING: 3 THEORIES × FRAMEWORK

### §4.1 — Hypo-priors: world "too real"

```
🟢 Pellicano & Burr 2012 (TiCS 16(10):504-510):

  CORE CLAIM:
    → Typical brain: strong PRIORS (predictions based on past experience)
      → Perception = prior + sensory input (Bayesian brain)
      → Strong priors → SMOOTH experience (fill in gaps, predict next event)
    → Autistic brain: WEAKER PRIORS
      → Sensory data DOMINATES → world perceived "more raw"
      → Less smoothing → more detail perceived → BUT more overwhelming

  EXPLAINS:
    → Enhanced perceptual abilities (§9): weaker priors = less top-down smoothing
      → SEE MORE DETAIL (not filtered by expectation)
    → Sensory sensitivity: raw data without dampening = LOUDER, BRIGHTER, MORE
    → Resistance to optical illusions (some studies)
    → Difficulty with social prediction (social = heavily prior-dependent)

  FRAMEWORK MAPPING:
    → prediction-delta = difference between PREDICTED and ACTUAL
    → Weak priors → fewer/weaker predictions → MORE prediction-delta per event
    → = Body-feedback fires MORE OFTEN, for MORE things
    → = Consistent with sensory overload (§2) and reduced habituation (§2.3)
```

### §4.2 — HIPPEA: inflexible precision of prediction errors

```
🟢 Van de Cruys et al. 2014 (Psychological Review 121(4):649-675):

  CORE CLAIM:
    → Typical brain: FLEXIBLY adjusts precision of prediction errors
      → "Noisy context" → turn DOWN precision (tolerate more mismatch)
      → "Precise context" → turn UP precision (catch small errors)
    → Autistic brain: prediction errors given inflexibly HIGH precision
      → EVERY mismatch treated as IMPORTANT → cannot ignore minor deviations
      → = OVERFITTING to details

  EXPLAINS:
    → Insistence on sameness: changing routine = prediction error
      → HIGH precision → error treated as SIGNIFICANT → distress
    → Detail focus (§9): high precision on sensory errors → notice fine details
    → Difficulty with ambiguity: ambiguous situations = many prediction errors
      → all treated as important → OVERWHELMING
    → Comfort with rule-based systems: clear rules → few prediction errors → comfortable

  FRAMEWORK MAPPING:
    → prediction-delta AMPLIFIED
    → Same mismatch → LARGER body-feedback response in autism
    → = Consistent with Insistence on Sameness (IS factor from §1.7)
    → = IS = strategy to REDUCE prediction errors (control environment → predictable)
```

### §4.3 — Aberrant Precision: WHERE matters

```
🟢 Lawson, Rees & Friston 2014 (Frontiers in Human Neuroscience 8:302):

  CORE CLAIM:
    → Precision allocation ATYPICAL across cortical hierarchy
    → NOT uniformly hypo or hyper — DIFFERENT at different levels
    → Low-level sensory: TOO MUCH precision (over-weighted sensory details)
    → High-level social/abstract: TOO LITTLE precision (weak top-down models)
    → = BRIDGE between Pellicano (weak high-level priors)
        and Van de Cruys (inflexible low-level precision)

  EXPLAINS:
    → BOTH enhanced perception (low-level precision high)
        AND social difficulty (high-level precision low)
    → Simultaneously: SEE details + MISS social gist
    → = NOT paradox — DIFFERENT levels of hierarchy

  FRAMEWORK MAPPING:
    → Prediction model operates at multiple levels
    → Autism: low-level prediction (sensory) = overweighted
    → High-level prediction (social, abstract) = underweighted
    → = Consistent with cascade model (§2.5):
        sensory → rich/detailed chunks | social → sparser chunks
```

### §4.4 — Framework position: all 3 consistent

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK SYNTHESIS:

  Original question: "prediction-delta threshold LOWER vs AMPLIFIED?"
  Answer after research: ALL 3 theories consistent with framework:

  ┌──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────┐
  │ Theory               │ Mechanism                │ Framework mapping       │
  ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┤
  │ Hypo-priors          │ Weak predictions →       │ prediction-delta        │
  │ (Pellicano 2012)     │ more surprise per event  │ FIRES MORE OFTEN        │
  ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┤
  │ HIPPEA               │ Inflexible high-precision│ prediction-delta        │
  │ (Van de Cruys 2014)  │ → every mismatch large   │ AMPLIFIED per event     │
  ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┤
  │ Aberrant Precision   │ Precision atypical across│ prediction-delta        │
  │ (Lawson 2014)        │ hierarchy levels         │ DIFFERS by level        │
  └──────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────┘

  ALL 3 → prediction-delta LARGER than typical → larger body-feedback
  → consistent with sensory overload, insistence on sameness, meltdown

  Framework DOES NOT NEED TO CHOOSE 1:
    → At population level: all 3 predict the same behavioral pattern
    → At individual level: WHICH mechanism → DIFFERENT for each person
    → = Per-individual measurement needed (EEG/fMRI) to distinguish
    → = Framework can OBSERVE without committing to 1 mechanism
```

### §4.5 — Insistence on Sameness = prediction-delta REDUCTION

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK SYNTHESIS:

  IS (Insistence on Sameness) = LOGICAL response to prediction-delta LARGER:

  IF prediction-delta is larger than typical per-event:
    → STRATEGY 1: reduce NUMBER of unexpected events → ROUTINE
    → STRATEGY 2: control WHICH events occur → SAMENESS
    → STRATEGY 3: prepare prediction in advance → TRANSITION WARNINGS

  → = NOT "rigid" for no reason — FUNCTIONAL response to architecture

  Cross-ref: Cortisol-Baseline.md v2.0:

    → Larger input × same amplifier = larger cortisol response
    → = Double impact if ALSO cortisol-sensitive

  → Disrupting routine = removing REGULATORY strategy
  → = Like removing wheelchair from person who cannot walk
  → Support: PROVIDE routine + PREPARE for transitions
    (not "teach to be flexible" — address ROOT: prediction-delta load)

  → = Double empathy: ROBUST, REPLICATED finding
  → Published in Nature Human Behaviour = high-impact confirmation

### §5.6 — Face processing: ACTIVE avoidance for self-protection

```

  INTERPRETATION:

    → Eye avoidance is NOT "failure to detect social stimuli"

    → Eye contact → amygdala fires STRONGLY → body-feedback OVERLOAD
    → Avoidance = REDUCE body-feedback load (same logic as §2.6)

  FRAMEWORK:

    → Eye contact = INTENSE multi-modal social input

## §6 — MASKING: COST OF COMPILATION

### §6.1 — What masking is

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK DEFINITION:

  Masking = compile "social protocol" chunks (Trust Compile) to operate in neurotypical world.

  Chunk.md v2.1: Trust Compile = trust-based install (compliance, schemas)
  Masking = LEARN AND EXECUTE neurotypical social scripts MANUALLY:
    → Make eye contact (despite amygdala hyperarousal — §5.6)
    → Use "appropriate" facial expressions (despite internal state being different)
    → Suppress stimming (despite body-feedback regulation need)
    → Mirror neurotypical conversational patterns (despite different processing)
    → "Look normal" across multiple channels simultaneously

  = COMPILATION FOR PERFORMANCE, not understanding
  = Autistic person compiles "how to ACT neurotypical" chunks
    WITHOUT the underlying neurotypical processing that makes these behaviors natural
  = Like learning a foreign language's PRONUNCIATION without SPEAKING the language
```

### §6.2 — Hull et al. 2017: 3-stage model

```
🟢 Hull et al. 2017 (JADD 47(8):2519-2534):

  Qualitative study → identified 3-stage process:

  STAGE 1: MOTIVATIONS
    → Fitting in, avoiding bullying, maintaining employment
    → "I mask because the CONSEQUENCES of not masking are worse"
    → = Cost-benefit calculation: masking cost < social punishment cost

  STAGE 2: TECHNIQUES
    → Masking: hiding autistic traits (suppress stim, force eye contact)
    → Compensation: learn explicit social rules (study body language, scripts)
    → "I watch how others do it and copy" — explicit learning of implicit rules

  STAGE 3: CONSEQUENCES
    → Exhaustion (most reported): "like acting all day every day"
    → Identity confusion: "I don't know who I really am anymore"
    → Late/missed diagnosis: "I seemed fine so no one noticed"
    → Mental health decline: anxiety, depression, burnout
    → Loss of sense of self: Self-Pattern-Modeling self-chunks = compiled PERFORMANCE,
      not authentic self
```

### §7.2 — Alexithymia predicts, NOT autism

```

  → = "Emotion recognition deficit in autism" = artifact of alexithymia co-occurrence

🟢 Shah et al. 2016 (Cortex 81:215-220):

    Alexithymia predicted POOR accuracy. Autism did NOT.

  IMPLICATIONS:

    → = Critical methodological point for ALL autism empathy research

### §7.3 — Self-to-other model

```
🟢 Bird & Viding 2014 (Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews 47:520-532):

  SELF-TO-OTHER MODEL of empathy:
    Step 1: Represent OWN emotional/body state (interoception)
    Step 2: Map own state → other person (simulation via Compiled)
    Step 3: Feel response (empathic concern/distress)

  ALEXITHYMIA disrupts Step 1:
    → Cannot clearly READ own body-feedback
    → → Cannot clearly MAP to other person (Step 2 degraded)
    → → Empathic response ATTENUATED or absent

  AUTISM does NOT inherently impair Step 1:
    → Autistic without alexithymia: Step 1 intact → empathy intact
    → Autistic WITH alexithymia: Step 1 disrupted → empathy attenuated
    → = Alexithymia is the BOTTLENECK, not autism

  FRAMEWORK:
    → Feeling.md v2.1 §3: "feeling = PFC observation of body-chunk interaction"
    → Alexithymia = PFC observation DIFFICULTY
    → Body-feedback FIRES normally → but PFC cannot clearly READ signal
    → = OBSERVATION problem, not SIGNAL problem
```

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK SYNTHESIS:

    → Order NEVER reverses.

  Alexithymia = difficulty at OBSERVATION stage:

## §10 — MELTDOWN/SHUTDOWN THROUGH BODY-FEEDBACK LENS

### §10.1 — Meltdown: OVERFLOW, not tantrum

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK ANALYSIS:

  MELTDOWN defined:
    → Body-feedback OVERLOAD → PFC goes OFFLINE → motor/emotional DISCHARGE
    → Crying, screaming, hitting, throwing, running
    → INVOLUNTARY — person cannot "choose" to stop

  MELTDOWN ≠ TANTRUM:
    → Tantrum = GOAL-DIRECTED (want something → escalate to get it)
    → Meltdown = OVERFLOW (body-feedback exceeds PFC capacity → discharge)
    → Tantrum: audience changes behavior. Remove audience → often stops.
    → Meltdown: audience irrelevant. Remove audience → continues.
    → = Fundamentally different mechanism — same behavioral surface

  MECHANISM (Body-Feedback-Mechanism.md):
    → Sensory input → body-feedback (§2: no habituation → ACCUMULATES)
    → Prediction-delta → body-feedback (§4: amplified in autism)
    → Social demands → body-feedback (§5: Self-Pattern-Modeling mismatch → dissonance)
    → Cumulative load → EXCEEDS PFC processing capacity
    → PFC goes offline (Threat.md: NE α1 cascade, PFC disconnect)
    → Body-feedback DISCHARGES through motor/emotional output
```

### §10.2 — Shutdown: WITHDRAWAL, not avoidance

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK ANALYSIS:

  SHUTDOWN defined:
    → Body-feedback OVERLOAD → PFC WITHDRAWAL → freeze/collapse
    → Going silent, becoming non-verbal, "spacing out," lying down
    → INVOLUNTARY — person cannot "choose" to engage

  SHUTDOWN = DIFFERENT EXPRESSION, SAME CAUSE:
    → Same trigger: body-feedback exceeds PFC capacity
    → Different output: discharge (meltdown) vs withdrawal (shutdown)
    → May depend on:
      → Individual nervous system profile (fight/flight vs freeze)
      → Context (public → shutdown, private → meltdown)
      → Current arousal level (moderate → meltdown, extreme → shutdown)

  PFC-Configuration.md:
    → Mode ④ Disconnected: PFC partially offline
    → Mode ⑤ Dissociated: PFC withdrawal, emotional numbness
    → Shutdown ≈ Mode ⑤ — defense mechanism, not "not caring"
```

### §10.3 — Neuroscience evidence

```
🟢 Green et al. 2013 (JAACAP 52(11)):
  → Amygdala HYPERACTIVATION to mildly aversive sensory stimuli
  → Even controlling for anxiety → sensory over-reactivity = DISTINCT
  → Severity correlated with parent-reported sensory difficulties

🟢 Green et al. 2015 (JAMA Psychiatry 72(8)):
  → REDUCED NEURAL HABITUATION in ASD+SOR
  → Brain does NOT "get used to" repeated stimuli
  → = CUMULATIVE: each additional input ADDS to load (no decay)
  → = "Fine in the morning, meltdown by afternoon" = ACCUMULATION, not trigger

  FRAMEWORK:
    → Reduced habituation + amplified prediction-delta + sensory gain high
    → = TRIPLE load on body-feedback system
    → Throughout the day: load ACCUMULATES (no habituation to reduce)
    → Final straw: seemingly minor event → capacity EXCEEDED → meltdown/shutdown
    → Observers: "They overreacted to nothing" → WRONG — overreacted to EVERYTHING
      accumulated over HOURS, final event was just the ONE that tipped the scale
```

### §10.4 — Stimming = REGULATION mechanism

```
🟢 Kapp et al. 2019 (Autism 23(7):1782-1792):

  Study: autistic adults' OWN perspectives on stimming
  → Self-regulatory mechanism (soothing, communicating, processing)
  → Suppressing stimming = EFFORTFUL + DISTRESSING
  → Stimming serves MULTIPLE functions:
    → Self-soothing (reduce body-feedback load)
    → Communication (express internal state)
    → Processing aid (rhythmic movement supports thinking)
    → Enjoyment (pleasant sensory input)

  FRAMEWORK:
    → Fidgeting-Analysis.md v1.0: fidgeting = body-feedback regulation MILD
    → Stimming = body-feedback regulation STRONGER
      (same mechanism, higher regulation NEED due to higher sensory load)
    → Stimming = body-feedback DRAIN mechanism:
      → Rhythmic movement → predictable sensory input → prediction-delta LOW
      → = Creates "safe" sensory stream that reduces cumulative load
      → = Like background music that masks unpredictable noise

  SUPPRESSING STIMMING:
    → Remove regulation mechanism WHILE load continues building
    → = INCREASES risk of meltdown/shutdown (no drain + accumulating load)
    → = Forcing "quiet hands" = removing fire extinguisher during fire
    → Correct approach: allow stimming + reduce ENVIRONMENTAL load
```

### §10.5 — Recovery

```
🟡 FRAMEWORK ANALYSIS:

  Body-feedback must DRAIN before PFC re-engages:
    → After meltdown: cortisol + NE + body-feedback ELEVATED
    → PFC recovery = not instant (needs body-feedback to DECREASE first)
    → Body-first invariant (Feeling.md): body state → PFC capacity
      NOT PFC decision → body state

  WHAT HELPS:
    → Low-stimulation environment (reduce INPUT)
    → Time (body-feedback naturally drains if no new input)
    → Predictable, safe context (reduce prediction-delta)
    → Familiar sensory input (stimming, comfort objects, familiar textures)
    → NO additional demands (adding demands = adding load to already-overloaded system)

  WHAT HARMS:
    → Forcing engagement DURING overload → extends recovery
    → Demanding eye contact → adds amygdala activation
    → Bright/loud environment → adds sensory input
    → Social pressure ("calm down") → adds social prediction demand
    → "Processing" the event immediately → PFC not yet online
    → = All of above = ADDING LOAD to system that needs DRAINING
```

## §11 — DEVELOPMENTAL TRAJECTORY

### §11.1 — Framework baseline

```
🟡 Child-Development-Mechanism.md v1.0:

  → SAME architecture as adults
  → Same Perception-Action Cycle. Same chunk substrate.
  → Only DIFFERENT: chunk density
  → Newborn: near-zero chunks → EVERYTHING is new → every loop = learning

  AUTISTIC DEVELOPMENT:
    → SAME framework applies — same architecture, same cycle
    → DIFFERENT: sensory gain settings from the start → cascade from birth
    → 4+1 channel compile: Visual / Auditory / Motor / Interoceptive / Social
    → Autistic: channels compile at DIFFERENT RATES, different DEPTH
    → NOT "delayed" — DIFFERENT PATHWAY, different timeline
    → Example: visual compile RICHER (high gain) + social compile SLOWER
      (integration different)
```

### §11.2 — Early signs (12-18 months)

```
🟢 Earliest observable differences (meta-analyses):

  → Reduced joint attention (initiation + response) by 12-18 months
  → Delayed/absent pointing (both proto-imperative + proto-declarative)
  → Social smile timing may differ
  → Gaze patterns different (Farroni 2002 cross-ref: 03-Visual-System-Arc.md)
  → Response to name inconsistent
  → Less interest in faces (BUT: Hadjikhani 2017 — may be ACTIVE AVOIDANCE, not disinterest)

  07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md developmental events:
    → E12 Social smile (6-8 weeks): timing may differ
    → E14 Gaze following (6 months): reduced or different trajectory
    → E18 Joint attention (9-12 months): key divergence point
    → E19 Social referencing (9-12 months): reduced
    → Proto-declarative pointing (12-14 months): delayed or absent

  FRAMEWORK:
    → Early signs = DOWNSTREAM of sensory + integration cascade
    → Wider TBW (§3) from birth → social chunks compile DIFFERENTLY
    → Joint attention = multi-modal social event → integration difference → delay
    → "Early intervention" = support sensory + communication, not "fix" social
```

### §11.3 — Language: wide range within autism

```
🟡 Language development spectrum WITHIN autism:

  NON-VERBAL:
    → ~25-30% autistic children non-verbal or minimally verbal by age 5
    → Non-verbal ≠ non-thinking (critical distinction)
    → AAC (Augmentative Alternative Communication) can reveal rich inner world
    → Some remain non-verbal lifelong

  TYPICAL LANGUAGE TIMELINE:
    → Many autistic children reach language milestones on typical timeline
    → But PRAGMATICS (social use of language) often different

  HYPERLEXIA:
    → Early and intense fascination with letters/reading
    → Can read fluently BEFORE understanding content
    → = Pattern detection (Fresh) applied to visual symbols

  ECHOLALIA:
    → Repeating heard phrases (immediate or delayed)
    → NOT meaningless — often FUNCTIONAL:
      → Delayed echolalia = retrieving relevant phrase from chunk library
      → = Communication attempt using compiled audio chunks
    → Framework: chunk retrieval for communication when spontaneous generation difficult
```

### §11.4 — Adolescence: increased mental health risk

```
🟢 Research evidence:

  → Depression: ~4× higher than neurotypical peers (Hollocks 2019)
  → Anxiety: 39.6% (van Steensel 2011)
  → Risk increases through adolescence because:

  FRAMEWORK ANALYSIS:
    → Social demands INCREASE dramatically (middle/high school)
    → Peer relationships become MORE complex (subtle social rules)
    → Masking demands INCREASE (fitting in becomes higher stakes)
    → Self-awareness INCREASES → comparison with neurotypical peers → gap VISIBLE
    → Identity formation period → "Why am I different?" → can be crisis
    → Accumulated masking cost (§6.5) → chronic cortisol → mental health cascade

  → Adolescence = CONVERGENCE of multiple risk factors simultaneously
  → NOT "autism gets worse" — DEMANDS increase while resources remain the same
```

### §11.5 — Adult outcomes + aging

```
🟢 Howlin et al. 2004 (JCPP 45(2):212-229):
  → 68 autistic adults followed up:
    Only 12% "very good" outcome (independent, employed)
    46% "poor" outcome (dependent, limited autonomy)
  → BUT: pre-2004 services and awareness — likely OUTDATED for current cohort
  → Intellectual ability = most consistent predictor

🟢 Tafolla et al. 2025 (Annual Review Clinical Psychology 21:193-220):
  → Lifespan review: cognitive ability MOST PREDICTIVE of trajectory
  → Emphasis: support across lifespan, not just childhood
  → Adult services = major gap worldwide

🟢 Aging — MASSIVE knowledge gap:
  → Happé & Charlton 2012 (Gerontology 58(1):70-78):
    Almost NO research on older autistic adults
    First large cohort now entering old age (diagnosis became common ~1990s)
  → Stewart & Happé 2025 (Annual Rev Dev Psych 7:461-484):
    Updated review — still limited data
    Open questions: cognitive aging trajectory? Dementia risk? Quality of life?
    → Framework: unknown whether cognitive reserve patterns differ in autism

  → = CRITICAL research gap — large population aging WITHOUT evidence base
```

### §11.6 — Late diagnosis: identity re-compile

```
🟡 Lai et al. 2015 (JAACAP 54(1):11-24):

  Female autism phenotype → under-diagnosis:
    → Better surface social skills (masking §6)
    → More internalizing (anxiety > externalizing)
    → Interests appear more "typical"
    → Diagnostic tools biased toward male presentation

  Late diagnosis → identity RE-COMPILATION:
    → Self-Pattern-Modeling.md v2.3: Self-Pattern-Modeling uses self-chunks as templates
    → Pre-diagnosis self-chunks: "I'm awkward" / "I'm broken" / "I try so hard but fail"
    → Post-diagnosis: "I'm configured differently" / "I was masking this whole time"
    → = CHUNK-SHIFT (Body-Feedback-Mechanism.md §3.1)

  PARALLEL with ADHD (ADHD-Observation.md §10.4):
    → Same mechanism: late diagnosis → identity chunk shift
    → "Ah, everything explained" → relief + grief + anger (compound)
    → Relief: "I'm not broken"
    → Grief: "All those years of struggling without understanding"
    → Anger: "Why did no one catch this?"
    → Duration of re-compilation ∝ years of masking

  → Late diagnosis = NOT just "getting a label"
  → = FUNDAMENTAL re-compilation of self-chunks built over decades
```

### §11.7 — "Optimal Outcome" + Labeling Paradox

```
🟢 Fein et al. 2013 (JCPP):

  "OPTIMAL OUTCOME" — autism diagnosis LOST:
    → 34 people diagnosed with autism before age 5
    → Grown up NO LONGER meeting diagnostic criteria
    → Match typical peers on socialization, communication, face recognition
    → Prevalence across studies: 4-13%

  PREDICTORS (who is likely "optimal outcome"):
    → Early diagnosis (<31 months)
    → Higher cognitive ability
    → Milder social symptoms at onset
    → Reduced repetitive behaviors early (ages 2-3)
    → EARLY + INTENSIVE intervention

  ⚠️ CAVEAT:
    → "Optimal outcome" ≠ "autism is gone"
    → Executive function weakness + anxiety/depression vulnerability still present
    → May = MASKING very effectively (§6) rather than genuine change
    → OR = mild configuration + good support → compile sufficient social chunks
```

```
🟢 CASE TEMPLE GRANDIN — label + protection:

  1947: born. Did not speak until ~age 4.
  1949: diagnosed "brain damage" (term used in the 1950s for autism)

  DOCTOR + FATHER: recommended INSTITUTIONAL CARE
    → Standard practice in the 1950s
    → = Deprivation pathway (§1.9 ③) ON TOP OF autism configuration

  MOTHER (Eustacia Cutler): REFUSED.
    → Found different neurologist → speech therapy 3x/week from age 2.5
    → Manually taught social skills (manners, turn-taking, dress, punctuality)
    → Entered mainstream kindergarten ~age 4
    → Parents divorced over disagreement on this decision

  OUTCOME: Professor, revolutionized the livestock industry,
    TED speaker, Time 100 Most Influential (2010)

  FRAMEWORK INSIGHT:
    → Grandin HAD label but mother PROTECTED from label's consequences
    → Label → institution = label + deprivation = compound (§1.9)
    → Label → correct support = label + INPUT = configuration develops
    → = LABEL does not determine — HOW TO RESPOND after the label does
```

```
🟡 THE LABELING PARADOX:

  POSITIVE (having label):
    → Understanding oneself → identity formation → community belonging
    → Cooper et al. 2023 (PMC): autistic identity → LOWER depression
    → Access support, accommodation, resources
    → "I'm not broken — I'm configured differently"

  NEGATIVE (having label):
    → Lowered expectations from those around
    → Hornstra 2023: "Golem effect" — teacher expectations shaped by label
    → Self-fulfilling: "I can't because autism" → reduced effort → confirms label
    → AMA Journal of Ethics 2015: "The Autism Paradox"

  LATE-DIAGNOSED SUCCESSFUL ADULTS (developed WITHOUT label):
    → Anthony Hopkins: diagnosed ~age 80 (2017). Career already legendary.
    → Susan Boyle: diagnosed age 61 (2022). Global fame since 2009.
    → Wentworth Miller: adult diagnosis (2021). Actor, writer.
    → = All developed WITHOUT label constraining them → built career →
      diagnosis LATER = identity enrichment, not limitation

  FRAMEWORK SYNTHESIS — NO one answer fits all:
    → Label EARLY + support GOOD = best case (Grandin model)
    → Label EARLY + support POOR = worst case (institutional, self-fulfilling)
    → No label + develop freely = some succeed (Hopkins model)
    → No label + struggle alone = accumulated damage + late diagnosis grief

    → THE DECISION IS NOT "label or no label"
    → THE DECISION = "after label (or no label), what is the ENVIRONMENT like?"
    → = Same insight as §1.9: OUTPUT depends on INPUT (support, environment)
      more than it depends on the label
```
