# 05 — Motor Output Developmental Arc

> **File 05 of F1 Child-Chunk-Development sub-folder.** Third of six body-input arc files. This file traces motor output system chunk compile mechanism from **birth reflexes** through **walking + gestural communication** at ~24 months postnatal. Unlike auditory (04) where the system is receptive-dominant, motor is **output-dominant** — chunks produce body action rather than detect input. This creates a distinctive asymmetry the file exploits.

---

## §0 — Abstract

🟢🟡 **Claim**: Motor chunks compile over 0-24 months in two interleaved arcs — **hand motor** (palmar reflex → proto-declarative pointing → tool use) and **locomotion** (head control → walking). Both arcs ride on a common substrate (motor cortex + cerebellum + basal ganglia + spinal circuits) but compile at **different rates depending on visuomotor feedback availability**. Hand arc is more tightly visuomotor-coupled (rich visual feedback for each action); locomotion arc is more proprioceptive-vestibular-coupled (visual plays supporting but not primary role for balance).

The motor system is particularly instructive for F1 in one specific way: **motor chunks bridge receptive and productive sides**. Most F1 receptive milestones (visual, auditory, interoceptive) happen before their motor production counterparts. The **gap between receptive and productive** — formalized as H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry — manifests most clearly when a body system's motor output chunks lag behind its corresponding receptive chunks. Gestural communication emerges at ~14 months (E25) with specific need-to-gesture chunks **before** verbal labels for the same needs emerge at ~18-22 months (E21, E22, E23). This gives us a **~4-8 month gesture-verbal gap within the same productive motor system** — a clean within-body-input test of H11.

## §1 — Position within F1

### §1.1 — Motor as the "action side" of F1

F1's six body-input arcs divide roughly into receptive-dominant systems (visual, auditory, interoceptive) and production-dominant systems (motor, verbal). Social is cross-cutting. Motor is the **most purely productive** of F1's body-input arcs — its chunks produce action, not detect input.

This has two methodological consequences:

1. **Ladder classification skews higher**. Motor events are action-involved, so most reach at least L3 (crude coordinated action). L2 motor events are rare (pattern-match without action by definition means no motor output). L0 motor events are the birth reflexes. L1 motor events are post-hoc reactive movements.

2. **Receptive-productive gap is visible within motor itself**. A baby can intend to reach for an object (cognitive/visual side) long before the reach succeeds (motor side). Visuomotor mapping chunks must compile before the intention translates to accurate action. This within-motor asymmetry is a smaller version of the broader Convergence Point 5 H11 asymmetry.

### §1.2 — Two arcs with interleaved milestones

Motor development is best understood as **two parallel arcs** on shared substrate:

**Hand arc**: fine motor, visuomotor-coupled, manipulative, eventually communicative (pointing, gesturing, tool use). Heavy integration with visual chunks (03) + later with verbal chunks (08).

**Locomotion arc**: gross motor, proprioceptive-vestibular-coupled, balance-driven, eventually enabling independent exploration. Heavy integration with interoceptive (balance, effort) + environmental chunks.

**Why distinguish**: the two arcs compile at partially independent rates. A baby can be advanced on hand arc while average on locomotion arc (or vice versa). This is evidence that **motor chunks are body-part-specific**, not a single "motor maturity" axis. Parallels the non-uniform ladder progression property of plan §0.7.3 Property 3.

## §2 — Hand Motor Arc

> Hand arc = fine motor chunks for hand control, reach, grasp, manipulate, point, gesture, use tools. Ordered roughly by age of emergence.

### §2.1 — Palmar grasp reflex at birth (E7, L0)

🟢 **Event**: Newborn, when palm is stroked or an object placed in hand, reflexively closes fingers around it. Grip can be strong enough to support newborn's body weight briefly. Present from birth, typically disappears ~3-4 months. ([Feel-Example E7](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e7) line 391.)

🟢 **Research**: Standard pediatric neurological exam. Peiper 1963, Prechtl & Beintema 1964, Twitchell 1970 classic studies on infant reflexes.

🟡 **Chunks required**: None learned. Spinal-cord + subcortical reflex circuit (primitive motor pattern).

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L0** 🟢. Pure reflex.

**Framework note**: The palmar grasp reflex is **not the same motor system as later voluntary grasping**. Voluntary grasping (§2.5 below) emerges as the reflex fades and cortical motor control takes over. The reflex is **spinal/subcortical pattern**, voluntary grasp is **cortically-controlled compiled chunk**. Both look similar behaviorally (hand closing around object) but are mechanistically distinct.

### §2.2 — Rooting + sucking reflex at birth (E8, L0)

🟢 **Event**: Newborn, when cheek is touched, turns head toward touch and opens mouth to suck. Present from birth, persists variably through first months. ([Feel-Example E8](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e8) line 427.)

🟡 **Chunks required**: None learned. Brainstem reflex for feeding orientation.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L0** 🟢.

**Why include**: Rooting + sucking is the **mouth motor** reflex that later becomes the substrate for **articulatory motor chunks** (babbling, word production). The mouth is among the earliest motor systems with cortical control emerging — full vocal production arc in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md).

### §2.3 — Hand-to-mouth intentional (2-3 months)

🟢 **Event**: By 2-3 months, infants deliberately bring hand to mouth — not as reflex but as repeated voluntary action, often for self-soothing or exploration.

🟢 **Research**: Rochat 1989 "Self-perception and action in infancy" — described hand-to-mouth as emerging proprioceptively guided action.

🟡 **Chunks required**:
- Proprioceptive body-schema chunks (knowing where hand is)
- Arm motor chunks (shoulder + elbow + wrist coordination)
- Mouth-target motor chunk (directing hand to face)
- Repetition compile from multiple daily episodes

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L1-L2** 🟡. Pattern-match on proprioceptive state → motor output. Transitional event — emerging voluntary control.

**Framework importance**: Hand-to-mouth at 2-3 months is plausibly the **first deliberate motor chunk** in postnatal development — the first chunk linking proprioceptive awareness to voluntary motor output. This is a tiny but important milestone — substrate for all subsequent voluntary motor chunks.

### §2.4 — Visually guided reaching (4-5 months) ⭐ visuomotor bind

🟢 **Event**: Infants 4-5 months reach for visually perceived targets with reasonable accuracy. Before 4 months, reaching is "pre-reaching" — uncoordinated swipes not clearly guided by visual target location.

🟢 **Research**:
- von Hofsten 1979 — first demonstration of intentional reaching in young infants
- von Hofsten 1982, 1984 — trajectory of reaching development
- Clifton et al. 1993 — reaching happens even in the dark if infant has auditory/proprioceptive target info, showing reach is not purely visual-dependent
- Berthier & Keen 2006 — reaching trajectory analysis

🟡 **Chunks required** (substantial stack):
- **Visual object location chunk** (3D spatial coordinates from visual field)
- **Arm motor chunks** (shoulder, elbow, wrist coordination primitives)
- ⭐ **Visuomotor binding chunk** — the **coordinate transformation** from visual (retinal/3D) coordinates to motor (joint-angle/muscle) coordinates. This is one of the critical binding chunks in F1.
- Hand-shape chunk (pre-shaping hand for grasp as arm approaches object)
- Feedback-driven correction chunks (online error correction during reach)

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢. Coordinated action, goal-directed, but not yet fully reliable (accuracy improves over months after first emergence).

**⭐ Why visuomotor binding is load-bearing for F1**:

1. **First large-scale binding of visual + motor chunks**. Before visuomotor reaching, visual and motor were separate systems — visual chunks compiled independently (03 §3), motor chunks compiled independently (reflexes + early voluntary §2.3). At 4-5 months, the **binding** between them becomes functional and the baby can act on what they see.

2. **Convergence Point 3 visual-motor contribution**: this is the visual-motor side of the multi-modal binding mechanism. Mechanism candidates: Hebbian temporal co-occurrence (reaching during visual fixation creates co-firing), sleep consolidation of visuomotor mappings, error-driven learning from reach failures.

3. **Substrate for tool use + manipulation**. Every hand-based chunk from here on (grasping, pincer, pointing, tool use, gesturing) rides on the visuomotor binding established at 4-5 months. Without this bind, no hand-object chunks can compile.

### §2.5 — Grasp with full hand (5-6 months)

🟢 **Event**: 5-6 month olds grasp objects with full hand (palmar grasp, still whole-hand scoop — not yet pincer). Voluntary, goal-directed grasp replacing the primitive palmar reflex (§2.1) which has now disappeared.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Visuomotor reach chunks (§2.4) + grasp motor chunks (finger flexion) + grip strength modulation + release chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢.

### §2.6 — Object exploration via hand (5-9 months)

🟢 **Event**: Between 5 and 9 months, babies explore objects by grasping, mouthing, banging, transferring hand-to-hand, turning, examining. This is the **motor substrate for compiling object chunks** — the visual object file chunks (03 §3.7) get enriched with hand-motor interaction chunks creating multi-modal object chunks.

🟡 **Framework claim**: This phase is the **most productive compile window** for hand-based object knowledge. Each object interaction episode compiles: visual chunks (appearance) + motor chunks (how to grasp) + tactile chunks (texture, weight) + sometimes auditory chunks (sound from banging) + outcome chunks (what happens when you manipulate).

### §2.7 — Pincer grasp (9-10 months) — fine motor precision

🟢 **Event**: Infants 9-10 months pick up small objects (raisin, cheerio) using thumb + index finger opposition (pincer grasp). This is **precision fine motor** replacing whole-hand scooping for small targets.

🟢 **Research**: Halverson 1931 classic grasp taxonomy, still widely cited. Corbetta & Bojczyk 2002 developmental trajectory.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Visuomotor chunks for small targets + finger-specific motor chunks (thumb-index opposition) + grip force modulation for small/fragile objects.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3-L4** 🟢. Finer precision requires more coordinated chunks stack.

**Framework note**: Pincer grasp age is notably close to the age of proto-imperative pointing (§2.8) and approximately when joint attention emerges (03 §4.6). These **converging developments at 9-12 months** are not accidental — they ride on the same substrate of refined fine motor chunks + social-cognitive chunks maturing together.

### §2.8 — Proto-imperative pointing (9-10 months)

🟢 **Event**: 9-10 month olds begin using extended-finger pointing **to request objects**. "Proto-imperative" because it functions as a request ("give me that") — an imperative speech act in gestural form.

🟢 **Research**: Bates, Camaioni & Volterra 1975 "The acquisition of performatives prior to speech" — classic typology of proto-imperative vs proto-declarative pointing.

🟡 **Chunks required**:
- Finger-extension motor chunk (hand shape: all fingers curled except index extended)
- Arm-orientation chunk (pointing the arm toward target)
- Target-identification chunk (visual chunks for the desired object)
- **Adult-as-agent chunk** — understanding that the adult can retrieve the object, so pointing at the adult gets the object
- Communicative intention chunk (making the gesture for a purpose)

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢. Coordinated goal-directed action with social component.

### §2.9 — Proto-declarative pointing (12-14 months) ⭐

🟢 **Event**: 12-14 month olds begin pointing **to share attention** with the adult about an object — "look at that" rather than "give me that". This is the declarative gesture form. Typically accompanied by checking-back to adult (E18-style triadic gaze, see [03-Visual-System-Arc.md](03-Visual-System-Arc.md) §4.6).

🟢 **Research**: Bates et al. 1975, Tomasello 1995, Liszkowski, Carpenter & Tomasello 2007 — proto-declarative pointing as evidence of shared intentionality.

🟡 **Chunks required** (more than proto-imperative):
- All the proto-imperative chunks (§2.8) above
- **Shared attention schema chunk** — treating attention as a thing that two agents can jointly hold on an object
- **Communicative purpose chunk** for non-request (sharing is different from requesting)
- **Check-back chunk** — the gaze loop that confirms shared attention

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L4** 🟢. Full coordinated execution, multi-chunk integration, goal is non-instrumental (sharing, not requesting).

**Framework importance**: Proto-declarative pointing is the **gestural version of joint attention initiation**. It integrates hand motor chunks + social chunks + pre-theory-of-mind chunks. Tomasello argues this is the gestural precursor to language — the capacity to reference something for another agent's benefit is the foundation of linguistic reference. See [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §3 for verbal-side treatment.

### §2.10 — Tool use + object manipulation (12-18 months)

🟢 **Event**: 12-18 month olds begin using objects as tools — spoon for feeding, cup for drinking, stick for reaching, container for carrying. Increasingly skilled manipulation.

🟢 **Research**: Piaget 1952 — classic substage 5 "tertiary circular reactions" describing tool use emergence. Bruner 1973 — skills learning via progressive refinement. Want & Harris 2001 — social learning of tool use.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Object-function chunks + hand-tool coordination chunks + goal-sequence chunks + sometimes imitation chunks (from watching adults).

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L4** 🟢. Coordinated, goal-directed, multi-chunk integration.

### §2.11 — Intentional gesture for need (E25, 14 months) ⭐ H11 evidence

🟢 **Event**: Baby ~14 months (not yet talking). Opens mouth + points to refrigerator = "want food". Raises both arms = "pick me up". Pushes other's hand away = "no, don't want". Each gesture has specific and consistent meaning. ([Feel-Example E25](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e25) line 1680.)

🟢 **Research**: Goodwyn, Acredolo & Brown 2000 "Impact of symbolic gesturing on early language development" — showed infants acquire gestural labels earlier than spoken labels for the same concepts. Baby sign language research has substantial literature on early gestural communication.

🟡 **Chunks required**:
- Interoceptive/need state chunk (hunger, desire to be lifted, desire to refuse)
- Gesture-specific motor chunk (one per need)
- **Need-to-gesture binding chunk** — linking specific need state to specific gesture output
- Social intention chunk (adult will understand and respond)

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L4** 🟢. Coordinated execution with differentiated need-to-output mapping.

**⭐ H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry evidence**: E25 at 14 months is **motor productive** for specific needs. Verbal productive for the same needs emerges at ~18-22 months (E21 "hungry", E22 "leg hurts", E23 "urge to urinate"). Gap = ~4-8 months.

**Critical framework observation**: E25 shows that the **receptive-productive gap is partially a motor-system gap**, not purely a cognitive one. The baby at 14 months:
- Has the need-detection chunk (interoceptive, receptive side)
- Has the specific-gesture chunk (hand motor, productive side)
- Can reliably link them

At 14 months, the baby **does not yet** have:
- Articulation chunks for the corresponding verbal labels (mouth motor coordination for "hungry", "pick me up", etc.)
- Or possibly: the verbal label chunk itself is compiling but articulation motor chunks are lagging

The 4-8 month gap from E25 to E21/E22/E23 is evidence that **articulation motor chunks specifically lag behind hand motor chunks** for communicative output. This is one of the cleanest within-motor-system demonstrations of H11 — and it will be drilled further in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §5.

**Implication from Feel-Example E25 Notes**: Feel-Example explicitly states (original Vietnamese): "chunks có thể được 'labeled' bằng gesture trước khi labeled bằng words. Output channel cho chunks không nhất thiết phải verbal." [Translation: "Chunks can be 'labeled' by gesture before being labeled by words. The output channel for chunks does not have to be verbal."] This is the **Convergence Point 6 contribution** — labeling attachment can use gestural handles as alternative to verbal handles. See §7.3 + §8.2 below.

### §2.12 — Delayed imitation (14-18 months, Meltzoff 1988)

🟢 **Event**: 14-18 month olds can imitate an action they observed hours or days earlier — demonstrating delayed recall + motor reproduction.

🟢 **Research**: Meltzoff 1988 — classic demonstration of delayed imitation in 14-month-olds observing an action with a novel toy, tested after 24 hours. Meltzoff & Moore 1994 — earlier (neonatal) imitation work.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Observed-action chunks + motor-reproduction chunks + action-sequence chunks + delayed recall (chunks persist across sleep/wake cycles).

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L4** 🟢.

**Framework importance**: Delayed imitation shows motor chunks are **durable across sleep consolidation**. This connects to §5.4 sleep consolidation mechanism for motor learning.

## §3 — Locomotion Arc

> Locomotion arc = gross motor chunks for body control, balance, movement through space. More proprioceptive-vestibular than visually-guided (though vision contributes).

### §3.1 — Postural reflexes at birth

🟢 **Event**: Newborns show various postural reflexes (Moro, stepping reflex, asymmetric tonic neck reflex, Galant reflex). These are **brainstem-driven primitive patterns**, not learned chunks.

🟡 **Chunks required**: None. Pattern generators at spinal + brainstem level.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L0** 🟢.

**Framework note**: Some reflexes persist briefly as the substrate for later voluntary motor chunks (stepping reflex eventually becomes walking), but most disappear as cortical motor control takes over. The disappearance of primitive reflexes is a **pediatric developmental marker** — retention past expected ages suggests developmental concerns.

### §3.2 — Head control (2-4 months)

🟢 **Event**: By 2-4 months, infants hold head up when prone, lift head against gravity, turn head to track visual or auditory targets.

🟢 **Research**: Bayley Scales of Infant Development — standard pediatric assessment.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Neck motor chunks + proprioceptive chunks (head-relative-to-body) + gravity compensation chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L1-L2** 🟡. Emerging voluntary control.

### §3.3 — Rolling over (4-6 months)

🟢 **Event**: 4-6 months, infants roll from back to side, then side to front, then front to back. Sequence typically front-to-back first (easier).

🟡 **Chunks required**: Torso rotation motor chunks + body-schema chunks + effort coordination chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2-L3** 🟢.

### §3.4 — Sitting unsupported (6-7 months)

🟢 **Event**: 6-7 months, baby can sit without support for extended periods, maintaining balance against gravity and minor perturbations.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Postural control chunks (trunk motor + proprioception + vestibular) + balance recovery chunks (respond to tipping).

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢. Coordinated ongoing motor output.

**Framework observation**: Independent sitting is a **major functional milestone** because it frees the hands for manipulation while the baby is upright. Before sitting, hand use during upright positioning requires adult support. After sitting, hands are free → hand-motor compile rate increases. This is one of the cases where **locomotion arc development enables hand arc development** via context change.

### §3.5 — Crawling (7-9 months)

🟢 **Event**: 7-9 months, babies begin crawling — moving on hands and knees (or belly commando-style first). Creates first **independent locomotion** → exploration of environment beyond adult-held range.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Limb coordination chunks (cross-pattern: left arm with right knee, right arm with left knee) + forward-motion motor chunks + balance-in-motion chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢.

**Framework observation**: Crawling enables **self-directed exploration**. Before crawling, the baby's world is what adults bring to them. After crawling, the baby can pursue visual targets, investigate, approach. This **dramatically expands chunks compile rate for visual + object chunks** because the baby now generates their own visual learning opportunities. Campos et al. 2000 "Travel broadens the mind" reviews cognitive consequences of crawling onset — spatial cognition, object permanence, emotion, social referencing all change character after crawling begins.

### §3.6 — Pull to stand (9-10 months)

🟢 **Event**: 9-10 months, babies pull themselves to standing position using furniture or adult for support. Not yet independent standing.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Leg extension motor chunks + standing postural chunks + grip chunks (for holding support).

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢.

### §3.7 — First independent steps (10-14 months)

🟢 **Event**: Between 10 and 14 months, babies take their first independent steps — letting go of support and walking 2-5 steps before falling or catching support. Wide range (10-15 months typical, longer tail in individual variation).

🟢 **Research**: Standard pediatric milestones; cross-cultural variation documented (Super 1976 on East African infants walking earlier due to caregiver practices; Karasik et al. 2010 cross-cultural review).

🟡 **Chunks required**: Upright balance chunks + stepping pattern chunks + weight transfer chunks + fall recovery chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3-L4** 🟢.

### §3.8 — Walking (12-15 months)

🟢 **Event**: By 12-15 months, babies walk independently with progressively less wobble, longer sequences, and purposeful direction.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Walking cycle chunks (left-right-left alternation) + direction control chunks + balance-in-motion chunks + obstacle-avoidance chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L4** 🟢. Full coordinated execution, goal-directed, reliable.

**Framework importance**: Walking is the **capstone of locomotion arc in first year**. It transforms the baby's world again (as crawling did, but more so). Walking + talking together mark the transition from "infant" to "toddler" — the moment when most body systems reach a first operational threshold for independent function.

### §3.9 — Running (18-24 months)

🟢 **Event**: 18-24 months, toddlers begin running — faster gait with brief airborne phase.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Walking chunks + faster-cycle execution + airborne balance chunks + energy management chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L4** 🟢.

### §3.10 — Locomotion arc summary

🟢 The locomotion arc is **relatively linear** — each milestone builds on the previous, with few parallel tracks. Head control → rolling → sitting → crawling → pull to stand → first steps → walking → running is a clean sequence. Individual variation exists in timing (some babies skip crawling, go directly from sitting to pull-to-stand and walking), but the general ordering is stable.

Contrast with hand arc which has **parallel tracks** (manipulation, pointing, gesturing, tool use can advance semi-independently). Locomotion's linearity reflects the unified nature of "moving the body through space" as a single body-level task.

## §5 — Compile Mechanisms for Motor Chunks

> Motor chunks compile via same 4 mechanisms but with **different weights** compared to receptive (visual/auditory) chunks.

### §5.1 — Repetition (LTP) — dominant

**Dominant for**: Virtually all motor chunks. Motor learning is classically repetition-driven (Thorndike's law of exercise, modern motor learning research).

**Evidence**: Every motor chunks event in §2-§3 emerges through hundreds-to-thousands of practice attempts. Hand-to-mouth (§2.3) practiced dozens of times/day. Reaching (§2.4) practiced with gradually improving accuracy over weeks. Walking attempts: ~2,400 steps/hour × practice hours (Adolph et al. 2012 "How do you learn to walk? Thousands of steps and dozens of falls per day").

**Framework claim**: Repetition is the **undisputed primary compile mechanism for motor**. This contrasts with auditory chunks where receptive can compile from passive exposure; motor **must involve active practice** to compile.

### §5.2 — Multi-modal binding — very important

**Dominant for**: Visuomotor chunks (reaching, pointing, gaze following), audio-motor chunks (head turn to sound), proprioceptive-motor chunks (balance, body schema).

**Evidence**: Most motor chunks above L1 are multi-modal — they bind motor output to sensory input of some kind. Pure motor-only chunks (with no sensory binding) are rare beyond reflexes.

**Framework claim**: Motor arc is **intrinsically multi-modal** because action requires sensory feedback for refinement. This is a key difference from receptive chunks which can compile without action.

### §5.3 — Emotional peak tagging — less relevant for motor

**Applied to**: Pain-associated motor chunks (withdraw from hot), comfort-associated motor chunks (reaching for caregiver), reward-associated motor chunks (repeated actions that brought desired outcome).

**Framework claim**: Emotional tagging is **less central** for most motor chunks than for receptive chunks. Most motor chunks (walking, grasping, pointing) compile through practice without strong emotional peaks. But reward-associated motor chunks (imitation learning, intentional actions) do get emotional tagging. This is a **compile-mechanism asymmetry across body-inputs** — a finding this file contributes to F1 synthesis.

### §5.4 — Sleep consolidation — critical

**Dominant for**: Motor skill consolidation, motor memory durability.

**Evidence**:
- Walker 2003, 2017 — sleep + motor learning extensive literature
- Stickgold 2005 — sleep-dependent motor memory consolidation
- Gomez et al. 2006 — infant learning benefits from nap (extendable to motor tasks)
- Friedrich et al. 2015 — infant memory consolidation in sleep

**Framework claim**: Infants sleep 14-17 hours/day in first months, much in REM-rich sleep. This is **ideal consolidation environment** for motor chunks acquired during wake. The dramatic motor improvements often seen after a nap or overnight sleep reflect **offline consolidation**, not conscious practice.

### §5.5 — Interaction of mechanisms

Typical motor chunk compile trajectory:

1. **Initial attempt** — random exploration (e.g., first uncoordinated swipe at object)
2. **Feedback-driven refinement** — visual + proprioceptive feedback during attempt
3. **Repetition** — many attempts across hours/days
4. **Sleep consolidation** — overnight reorganization strengthens useful patterns, prunes failed ones
5. **Stable chunk** — reliable execution, minimal conscious attention needed

Each motor chunk goes through this trajectory at its own rate. Fast-compiling chunks (hand-to-mouth, head turn to sound) take days-weeks. Slow-compiling chunks (walking, tool use) take weeks-months. The variation depends on the **complexity of the chunks stack required** — simple primitive chunks compile faster than multi-chunk integrated behaviors.

## §7 — Pre-verbal Gestural Production as Proto-Communication (Convergence Points 5+6 preview)

> This section is a focused analysis of gestural communication in the 9-18 month window — the cleanest within-F1 evidence for H11 and Convergence Point 6.

### §7.1 — The 4-month gap from gesture to verbal

🟢 **Empirical fact**: Intentional gesture for needs (E25) emerges reliably ~14 months. First verbal labels for needs (E21 "hungry", E22 "leg hurts", E23 "urge to urinate") emerge ~18-22 months. Gap = ~4-8 months.

🟡 **Framework claim**: The gap is NOT because the baby at 14 months lacks:
- ❌ The need-detection chunk — baby clearly detects the need (demonstrated by appropriate gesture)
- ❌ The communicative intention chunk — baby clearly intends to communicate (demonstrated by gesture directed at adult + waiting for response)
- ❌ The binding chunk — baby clearly binds need state to communicative output (demonstrated by specific gesture per need)
- ❌ Receptive vocabulary for the relevant words — by 14 months baby often comprehends "hungry", "eat", "pick me up" etc. (consistent with Bergelson & Swingley 2012 common noun comprehension 6-9mo)

🟡 **Framework claim**: The gap IS because the baby at 14 months lacks:
- ✅ **Articulation motor chunks** — the specific mouth motor chunks for producing the verbal labels reliably
- ✅ Possibly: the **verbal label chunk itself in productive form** — the form that retrieves + orchestrates articulation for output

**Evidence for the articulation-bottleneck framing**: Babies who are taught baby sign language show that the **receptive + binding + intention** side is ready earlier — they produce signs for needs months before they produce corresponding spoken words. Goodwyn et al. 2000 is the key citation. The ONLY thing added when the child eventually uses spoken words is the articulation motor output channel.

### §7.2 — Within-child test of H11

🟢 The gesture-to-verbal gap is a **within-child test** of H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry:

- Control conditions: same baby, same need states, same communicative intent, same adult audience
- Variable: output motor channel (hand vs mouth)
- Result: hand motor chunks compile 4-8 months earlier than mouth motor chunks for equivalent communicative outputs

This isolates **motor-channel compile rate differences** from other developmental factors. It is strong within-subjects evidence for H11, which broader cross-modality evidence (Mandel 1995 name gap 13.5 months in [04](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md) §9.3) complements.

**Full H11 formalization**: [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §5. This file's contribution is the motor-specific within-child evidence.

### §7.3 — Convergence Point 6 preview — gesture as alternative handle

🟡 **Framework claim**: Convergence Point 6 asks whether chunks always need verbal labels to function, or whether alternative labeling systems (gestures, actions, images) can serve the same chunk-handle role.

**Evidence from E25 and gesture communication**:
- Chunks for need states at 14 months function fully without verbal labels
- The gestural label is sufficient for communicative reference + retrieval + action
- Adult interpreting the gesture and responding appropriately = chunk is "labeled" in a non-verbal system
- When verbal labels later compile (18-22 months), they often **replace** gestures for the same chunks, but the chunks existed before verbal

**Framework implication**: Verbal labeling is **one of several labeling systems**, not the only one. Chunks can be "handled" via:
- Gestural labels (E25, baby sign language, deaf sign language)
- Action labels (habitual action sequences become their own label)
- Visual labels (image-based recognition)
- Verbal labels (dominant in adult cultures)
- Internal-only labels (felt sense without external output, Feel-Example notes)

**⭐ Feel-Example contribution** (from [E25 Notes](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e25) line 1720-1734): "chunks có thể được 'labeled' bằng gesture trước khi labeled bằng words. Output channel cho chunks không nhất thiết phải verbal. ... Tất cả đều là valid forms của 'feeling expressed'. Sự ưu tiên cho verbal output trong văn hóa dominant có thể bias chúng ta nghĩ 'feeling = verbal' — nhưng đó là confusion giữa output channel và chunk itself." [Translation: "Chunks can be 'labeled' by gesture before being labeled by words. The output channel for chunks does not have to be verbal. ... All are valid forms of 'feeling expressed'. The preference for verbal output in dominant culture may bias us into thinking 'feeling = verbal' — but that is a confusion between output channel and the chunk itself."]

This is the **framework commitment** this file inherits and operationalizes for Convergence Point 6 preview. Full verdict in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md).

## §9 — Open Questions

1. **Why does hand motor lead articulation motor by 4+ months?** Multiple hypotheses:
   - Hand motor has more direct visual feedback (you see your hand move)
   - Mouth motor feedback is proprioceptive + auditory (less salient)
   - Hand motor cortex has larger cortical representation
   - Hand motor is older evolutionarily
   None of these is definitively tested as the cause of the gap. Open framework-empirical question.

2. **Individual variation in first steps — what predicts timing?** Ranges from 9-18 months across typical babies. Environmental factors (caregiver practices, surface type), physiological (leg muscle mass, body proportions), neural (motor maturation). How much is chunks compile rate vs physical constraints?

3. **Crawling skip babies — cognitive consequences?** Some babies skip crawling and go from sitting → pulling to stand → walking. Campos et al. 2000 argues crawling has cognitive benefits (spatial cognition, emotion regulation, social referencing). Are the skipping babies disadvantaged, or do they catch up via alternative mechanisms?

4. **E25 intentional gesture emergence — is there a receptive prerequisite?** Does the baby need to see gestures used communicatively (in caregivers, other children) before they can produce them? Or does the motor + intention come first and the environmental model is icing? Deaf infants of signing parents develop gestural communication along typical timeline despite no verbal model — suggests motor + intention is primary.

5. **Delayed imitation (Meltzoff 1988) — does it require language?** 14-18 month olds imitate actions from days earlier. Is this delayed recall mediated by internal verbal rehearsal (unlikely in pre-verbal babies), by visual memory, by motor imagery, or by some other mechanism? Framework question for chunks persistence mechanism.

6. **Sleep consolidation in motor learning — can we quantify the improvement?** Adult motor learning shows ~20-30% improvement after sleep vs no-sleep controls. Infant motor learning should show similar or larger effect (more sleep, more REM). Direct comparison studies would strengthen the compile mechanism claim.

7. **Cross-cultural variation in motor milestones** — Super 1976 East African infants walk earlier (caregiver practice of upright holding). Karasik et al. 2010 review finds substantial cross-cultural variation. What does F1 framework predict about how caregiver practices shift chunks compile rates in specific motor arcs?
