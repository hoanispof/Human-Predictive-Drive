## §0 — Abstract

1. **Nút Thắt 5 (H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry) FULL FORMALIZATION**: Tập hợp 7 đường bằng chứng hội tụ từ [04-Auditory-System-Arc.md](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md) §9 (cross-modality name gap 13.5mo), [05-Motor-Output-Arc.md](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md) §7 (within-child gesture-verbal 4-8mo), [06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md) §8 (within-body-state 6-10mo), và [06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md) §6.4 (4 cross-state lines: hunger/pain/thermal/emotional). Tất cả 7 đường hội tụ về **ratio 1:3** (productive bundle ≈ 3× receptive bundle → gap ≈ 2-3× receptive compile time). §6 cung cấp formal statement + 7 falsifiable predictions + 4 alternative hypotheses rejected.

2. **Nút Thắt 6 (Verbal-as-Handle) PRIMARY VERDICT**: Verbal labels **không phải là modality thứ 5** (bổ sung vào visual/auditory/somatic/interoceptive/social), **không phải constitutive** (chunks không phụ thuộc vào language). Verdict committed: **Hybrid mechanism — retrieval path + symbolic compression**. Chunks remain substrate (existed pre-verbal, per evidence E1/E9/E12/E15/E18 từ 03-07). Labels là **enhancement layer** — thêm retrieval path độc lập với chunk content, plus symbolic compression cho cross-situational generalization + communication + PFC holdability. Load-bearing cho H1 (chunk substrate independence).

## §2 — Connect phase: Speech motor substrate + auditory feedback loop

### §2.1 — Mouth motor development: lip, tongue, jaw coordination

**Baseline at t=0** (per [02 §9.1](02-Womb-to-Birth-Baseline.md#§9.1)):

Newborn has functional:
- ✅ Lip motor control (sucking reflex, rooting reflex) — brainstem/midbrain level
- ✅ Tongue motor control (sucking, swallowing) — brainstem level
- ✅ Jaw motor control (sucking coordination) — brainstem level
- ✅ Laryngeal motor control (crying vocalization) — midbrain level
- ❌ Cortical coordination for syllable-level articulation — not yet compiled
- ❌ Voluntary phonation separate from crying — not yet compiled
- ❌ Speech-specific motor chunks (consonant-vowel sequences) — not yet compiled

**Critical clarification**: The hardware (motor cortex + cranial nerves + muscles) is present at birth. The **speech-specific motor chunks** are what's missing. This is not "motor cortex immature" but "chunks chưa compiled" — same reframe logic as [01 §7](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md#§7) applied to a body-output system.

**Evidence for hardware presence**:
- Fetal yawning, hiccups, swallowing observable via ultrasound from ~10 weeks gestation (Prechtl 1985, de Vries et al. 1982)
- Newborn cry has full vocal apparatus engagement (Wasz-Höckert 1968 acoustic analysis)
- Brainstem reflexes for sucking-swallowing are present and functional from birth

What's missing is **cortical pattern chunks** that orchestrate the existing hardware into speech-specific sequences. Those chunks compile over months via the events documented in §4.

### §2.2 — Broca's area + motor speech planning

**Broca's area** (left inferior frontal gyrus, Brodmann 44/45) is the primary cortical region for speech production planning. In adults, damage here produces non-fluent aphasia with intact comprehension.

**Developmental status** (per [01 §4](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md#§4) 5 pillars):
- 🟢 Broca's area **substrate is online from prenatal**. Synaptic density in inferior frontal gyrus follows same trajectory as rest of frontal cortex (Huttenlocher 1979) — at or above adult levels at birth in most infants.
- 🟡 Broca's area **functional recruitment for speech** develops as speech chunks compile. Early fNIRS studies (Minagawa-Kawai et al. 2002, Peña et al. 2003) show left-lateralization for speech processing from neonatal period.
- 🟡 Broca's **chunks content** accumulates gradually with babbling → referential words → multi-word combinations.

**Framework claim**: Broca's area is the cortical substrate where speech motor chunks live, and it is online from birth. The **content** (chunks) is what accumulates. This is consistent with PFC reframe throughout F1.

### §2.3 — Auditory feedback loop: baby hears own vocalizations

**Mechanism**: When the baby vocalizes, the sound is processed by:
- Auditory cortex (hearing the sound)
- Somatosensory feedback (jaw + tongue + lip position)
- Proprioceptive feedback (laryngeal position)
- Efference copy from motor cortex (predicted sound)

**Comparison**: Baby compares predicted sound (efference copy) with actual heard sound. Mismatch drives learning (articulation chunks refine to match auditory targets).

**Empirical anchor**: 🟢 **Kuhl & Meltzoff 1996** — 12-20 week infants imitate vowel sounds after hearing adult models. This demonstrates auditory-articulatory coupling very early — the feedback loop is functional.

**Deaf infant evidence**: 🟢 **Oller & Eilers 1988** — deaf infants show delayed or absent canonical babbling (~8-10mo). If babbling were purely motor exploration, deaf infants would babble normally. The fact that they don't shows babbling requires auditory feedback for chunk compile.

**Framework implication**: Speech chunks compile through a **sensorimotor loop** (auditory target → articulatory attempt → auditory feedback → adjust → repeat). This is an instance of the general [Learning-Cycle](../Learning-Cycle/Learning-Cycle.md) mechanism applied to speech motor.

### §2.4 — Cross-reference with 04 Auditory (receptive side)

[04 §4](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§4) documents the auditory pattern chunks that feed the production loop:

- **Phoneme discrimination** (Werker & Tees 1984): universal at birth → narrowed by ~6-10mo toward native phonemes. By the time babbling canonicalizes, the baby has **perceptually oriented** toward native phoneme categories.
- **Statistical learning** (Saffran Aslin Newport 1996): 8mo infants segment words from continuous speech using transitional probabilities. This provides **word-form targets** for subsequent production.
- **Word-sound recognition** (Bergelson & Swingley 2012, Mandel Jusczyk & Pisoni 1995): common nouns comprehended from 6-9mo; own name recognition 4.5mo.

**What this means for production**: By the time the baby attempts production (canonical babbling 8-10mo, first words 12-14mo), the **auditory targets are already partially compiled**. Production is aiming at pre-existing auditory chunks. This is the receptive-first architecture that makes H11 mechanically sensible — receptive chunks are **prerequisite infrastructure** for production attempts.

## §4 — Control phase: Verbal production arc

> This section traces the production arc from cooing to two-word combinations. Each event is analyzed per [01 §6.3](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md#§6) event-inference methodology: observable event → chunks plausibly required → Ladder level → plausibility qualifier.

### §4.1 — Cooing 6-8 weeks — vocal motor exploration

**Event**: Baby produces prolonged vowel-like sounds ("oooh", "aaah") in relaxed, non-distressed contexts. Typically 6-8 weeks.

**Observable**: Sounds are **phonation without articulation** — vocal cord vibration + open vocal tract. No consonant-like closures. Often accompanied by social engagement (looking at caregiver, reciprocal smile).

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Oller 1980** — stages of vocal development. 🟢 **Stark 1980** — prelinguistic vocal stages. Cooing is stage 2 (after reflexive crying stage 1).

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Laryngeal motor control (already present via cry apparatus)
2. Vocal-tract shaping chunk (new — non-distress open tract)
3. Positive affect → vocal output chunk (co-occurrence with social engagement)
4. Auditory feedback processing of own vocalization (receptive cross-ref)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L1** (reflexive-differentiated). Cooing is not yet goal-directed, but it is differentiated from distress-cry. The baby **discovers** that vocalization can happen in non-distress contexts. Compare to E11 differentiated cry (4-6mo) which is also L1 — cooing is the positive-valence counterpart.

**Plausibility**: 🟢 observable event, well-documented, mechanism is substrate-level motor exploration without language-specific chunks.

**Connection to compile mechanisms**: Cooing is **repetition-compiled** (per [04 §6.3](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§6.3) plan §0.7 compile mechanisms). Baby repeats sounds that produce satisfying sensorimotor feedback. Over weeks, this builds the base for babbling.

### §4.2 — Reduplicated babbling 4-6 months ("ba-ba-ba")

**Event**: Baby produces repeated consonant-vowel sequences with the same CV unit ("ba-ba-ba", "ma-ma-ma", "da-da-da"). Typically 4-6 months.

**Observable**: Systematic closure + release of vocal tract (consonant-like) alternating with open phonation (vowel-like). Consonants are typically bilabial (/b/, /m/, /p/) or alveolar (/d/, /t/, /n/).

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Oller 1980, Stark 1980** — reduplicated babbling as developmental stage. 🟢 **Petitto & Marentette 1991** — reduplicated babbling in deaf infants exposed to sign language (manual babbling), demonstrating babbling is not purely oral-motor but reflects pattern-exploration of any available output channel.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Consonant closure motor chunk (new — coordinated closure of lip/tongue/jaw)
2. Vowel open motor chunk (from cooing)
3. CV transition chunk (linking closure to release)
4. Rhythmic repetition chunk (driving the babbling rhythm)
5. Auditory feedback → motor adjustment loop

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L2** (pattern-match activation). The baby is **exploring phonotactic patterns** systematically. The repetition is not reflexive — it is driven by pattern-match satisfaction. This is arguably a new L2 marker to add to the catalog (see §9).

**Plausibility**: 🟡 L2 classification rests on the interpretation that rhythmic babbling involves pattern-match (not just motor repetition). The cross-cultural and cross-modal (deaf infants signing) evidence supports the pattern-match framing.

**Compile mechanism**: **Repetition + auditory feedback + caregiver reinforcement** (caregivers smile, repeat sounds back, praise) — multi-channel compile rate boosted.

### §4.3 — Variegated babbling 8-10 months ("ba-ga-da")

**Event**: Baby produces sequences of different consonant-vowel units ("ba-ga-da", "pi-ta-mu"). Syllables vary within utterance.

**Observable**: Multiple different CV units in single utterance. Often accompanied by gestures and increasing social interaction.

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Oller 1980**. 🟢 **Vihman 1996** — infant vocal development. Variegated babbling is the bridge from repeated-unit babbling to word-like productions.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Multiple distinct CV chunks (compiled from §4.2)
2. **Sequence chunk** (linking multiple different units — this is new)
3. Pattern-matching against adult speech rhythm + intonation
4. Proto-syntactic structuring (some order patterns preferred)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L2-L3** transitional. L2 for the pattern-match aspect (matching adult speech rhythm). L3 for the emerging sequence-planning aspect (crude coordinated response assembling multiple CV units).

**Plausibility**: 🟡 transition between L2 and L3. Exact classification depends on whether one weights pattern-match dominance (L2) or sequence-assembly dominance (L3).

### §4.4 — Canonical babbling 8-10 months — syllable structure

**Event**: Baby produces well-formed CV syllables matching adult-language phonotactic structure. Different from variegated babbling (§4.3) in that syllables are individually well-formed per adult norms of baby's native language.

**Observable**: Syllables have adult-like timing, amplitude, and transitions. In a Vietnamese-speaking environment, Vietnamese phonotactic patterns dominate; in English environment, English patterns dominate.

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Oller 1980**. 🟢 **de Boysson-Bardies 1989** — cross-linguistic study showing native-language phonotactic influence in canonical babbling. 🟢 **Oller & Eilers 1988** — deaf infants delayed/absent canonical babbling, demonstrating auditory feedback dependence.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Native-language phonotactic template chunks (from statistical learning on ambient speech)
2. Syllable-level articulation chunks matching templates
3. Auditory-motor matching loop (refining production to match perception)
4. Sequencing chunks (for multi-syllable utterances)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L2-L3**. Canonical babbling matches templates (L2 pattern-match) while assembling articulatory sequences (L3 crude response).

**H11 preview**: The ambient-language template chunks are **receptive** (compiled via 04 §4.2 statistical learning). The articulation chunks are **productive**. The canonical babbling stage shows receptive templates are already guiding production attempts — consistent with H11 mechanism where receptive comes first.

### §4.5 — "Mama"/"Papa" pre-referential 10-12 months — phonological chunks without semantic binding

**Event**: Baby produces "mama" or "papa" (or language equivalents) but uses them **not specifically referring to mother/father**. May use "mama" for food, for comfort, for any caregiver, or simply as babbling sequence.

**Observable**: Phonological sequence is adult-like, but the social context of use is indiscriminate.

**Research anchors**: 🟡 **Jakobson 1941/1968** — nasal consonants (m) are among first produced because they involve minimal articulatory complexity. The cross-linguistic pattern of "mama"-like words for mother figures reflects this. Pre-referential use is well-documented in developmental diary studies.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Phonological chunk for the CV-CV pattern ("mama")
2. Generic positive-affect → vocalization association
3. Does NOT yet include semantic binding (no "mama-points-to-mother" chunk)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L2** (pattern-match activation without semantic reference). The baby matches the phonological pattern as a distinct chunk, but has not yet compiled the binding to a specific referent.

**Plausibility**: 🟡 the pre-referential → referential distinction is useful analytically but empirically fuzzy. Transition is gradual.

**Critical observation**: This event **falsifies naive "first word" claims**. Many parents celebrate "first word" when baby says "mama" at 10-11mo, but developmental research shows the **referential binding** happens later. The phonological chunk exists before the semantic binding — this is another instance of chunks being strength-weighted (per [04 §6.4](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§6.4) Nút Thắt 1 gradient verdict).

### §4.6 — "Mama" referential 12-14 months — word-referent binding

**Event**: Baby uses "mama" specifically to refer to mother, appropriately discriminating from other caregivers. Calls "mama" when wanting mother, looks at mother when hearing "mama".

**Observable**: Contextually appropriate use. Baby does not say "mama" to father or strangers.

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Nelson 1973** "Structure and strategy in learning to talk" — first words emergence typically 12-14mo. 🟢 **Bloom 1973** — one word at a time longitudinal. 🟢 **Fenson et al. 1994** — MacArthur-Bates CDI norms for first words.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Phonological chunk for "mama" (from §4.5)
2. Visual chunk for mother's face (from [07 §3.5](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md#§3.5) E15 mother recognition at ~5mo — receptive side already compiled)
3. **Word-referent binding chunk** — new. Binds phonological form to visual/voice/presence of mother.
4. Production trigger chunk (mother wanted/present → activate phonological chunk → articulate)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L3** (crude coordinated response). Baby retrieves + articulates an appropriate word in response to a specific chunk activation.

**H11 line highlighted**: The receptive chunk "hear 'mama' → orient toward mother" exists from [04 §5.3](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§5.3) (Mandel 1995, ~4.5mo). The productive chunk "see mother / want mother → say 'mama'" emerges ~12-14mo. Gap: ~7-10 months **within one word-referent pair**. This is a strong within-pair H11 instance (line variation on line #1 of the 7 — same mechanism, finer grain).

### §4.7 — First 10 words 12-18 months — concrete noun-heavy

**Event**: Baby accumulates a small productive vocabulary of roughly 10 words. Typical early words: family labels (mama, papa, baba), common objects (bottle, ball, dog), simple actions (bye-bye, up), social routines (hi, no).

**Observable**: Productive vocabulary is heavily concrete-noun-weighted. Social routine words and common object words dominate; abstract words, verbs, and grammatical markers come later.

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Fenson et al. 1994** — CDI vocabulary inventories showing concrete noun bias. 🟢 **Gentner 1982** "Why nouns are learned before verbs". 🟢 **Nelson 1973** — individual variation in early vocabulary composition.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Phonological chunks for each word (~10)
2. Referent chunks for each word (receptive side, often compiled earlier)
3. Word-referent binding chunks (~10)
4. Production retrieval + articulation path
5. Pragmatic use chunks (when to say the word)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L3** (crude coordinated productive response for each word).

**Compile mechanism observation**: Early words are **concrete + high-contingency + high-frequency** — consistent with the [06a §6](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§6) P2 compile mechanism (repetition × saliency × contingency × valence). Abstract or low-frequency words come later because they have weaker compile rate modulators.

### §4.8 — Body state verbal labels: "đói" 18mo, "đau" 20mo, "buồn đái" 22mo

This is where verbal production becomes load-bearing for body-input arcs drilled earlier in F1.

#### §4.8.1 — E21 "đói" 18 months

**Scenario preserved verbatim** from [Feel-Example E21](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e21):

> "Bé 18 tháng đang chơi với đồ chơi. Cảm giác đói tăng dần, bụng cồn cào. Bé dừng chơi, đi tới mẹ và nói 'đói' hoặc 'ăn ăn', có thể kèm chỉ vào miệng hoặc bụng."

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Hunger sensation chunk (compiled from [06b §2](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§2), receptive from E1-E11, ~0-5mo)
2. Hunger-"đói" label binding chunk (new — verbal handle attachment)
3. "đói" phonological + articulation chunks
4. Social communication chunk (address to mother)
5. Motor plan (walk to mother)

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L4** (coordinated execution). Full plan: detect state → label → locate target adult → walk → articulate.

**H11 evidence**: Hunger receptive differentiation at ~5mo (E11 differentiated cry); productive verbal at ~18mo. Gap = ~13mo. This is line #4 of the 7 H11 lines.

**Verbatim quote on handles vs chunks** (critical for Nút Thắt 6 verdict):

> "ngôn ngữ KHÔNG TẠO RA chunks (chunks đã có từ Stage 0 newborn). Nhưng ngôn ngữ TẠO RA HANDLES cho chunks. Một khi có handle (label 'đói'), bé có thể: (1) Nhớ lại chunk khi không đang trải nghiệm (anticipation); (2) Communicate với người khác về chunk; (3) Plan dựa trên chunk; (4) Combine với chunks khác ('đói + mệt'); (5) Eventually: reflect on chunk ('hôm nay tôi đói lắm')."

This is the anchor quote for §5 Nút Thắt 6 verdict. User's framework commitment is operationalized here as "labels are handles on pre-existing chunks, not constitutive of chunks themselves".

#### §4.8.2 — E22 "đau" + body part pointing 20 months

**Scenario preserved verbatim** from [Feel-Example E22](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e22):

> "Bé 20 tháng vấp ngã, đầu gối va vào sàn. Bé khóc, sau đó (vài giây sau) nói 'đau' và chỉ vào đầu gối."

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Nociceptive pain chunk (receptive from [06b §3](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§3), E2/E11)
2. Pain-"đau" label binding chunk
3. Body schema location chunk (knowing where "đầu gối" is on own body)
4. Body part name chunk ("đầu gối" phonological + referent binding)
5. Gestural pointing chunk (may predate verbal location)
6. Integration of pain location + verbal label + gestural indication

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L4** multi-channel coordinated response (pain detection → localization → verbalize + point).

**Vietnamese body parts richness observation** (from E22 verbatim):

> "Ngôn ngữ giàu body parts (Vietnamese có nhiều từ chi tiết về body parts — đầu gối, khuỷu tay, gáy, sống mũi...) tạo điều kiện cho deeper body-pattern chunks so với ngôn ngữ generic hơn."

This is **Whorfian moderate** — handle granularity in the language influences the granularity of body-schema chunks that can be "grasped explicitly". Not that speakers of different languages have different body schemas at the substrate level, but that the **handles available in the language determine what can be explicitly labeled and manipulated**. This is an important Nút Thắt 6 evidence — labels are handles, and handle granularity affects downstream chunk usage.

**H11 evidence**: Pain receptive differentiation from ~5mo (E11); productive verbal ~18-20mo. Gap = ~13-15mo. This is line #5 of the 7 H11 lines.

### §4.9 — Vocabulary spurt ~18-24 months — 50 words → rapid expansion

**Event**: Baby's productive vocabulary increases from ~50 words (around 18mo) to ~250+ words (around 24mo). Rate of word acquisition accelerates significantly.

**Observable**: Parent reports and CDI checklists show rapid growth. "Word spurt" or "naming explosion".

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Fenson et al. 1994** — CDI norms. 🟢 **Goldfield & Reznick 1990** — vocabulary spurt characterization. 🟢 **Bloom 2000** — "How Children Learn the Meanings of Words" reviews mechanism debates.

**Chunks plausibly required**:
1. Large number of phonological chunks (accumulating rapidly)
2. Large number of referent chunks (many pre-existing, compiled earlier)
3. Large number of word-referent binding chunks (accelerating compile)
4. Meta-chunk about "words refer to things" (possibly compiled around vocabulary spurt onset — this is debated)
5. Retrieval + articulation paths for each word

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L3-L4** for individual word production; **L4** for the combined strategy of asking "what's that?" and labeling.

**Framework claim**: Vocabulary spurt is not a PFC maturation event but a **compile mechanism phase transition**. As receptive chunks accumulate faster than productive ones (H11 asymmetry), the productive side "catches up" in a spurt when enough word-referent bindings are available to compile in parallel. This is consistent with the [04 §6.4](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§6.4) gradient view — no sudden switch, but an observable rate increase as multiple chunks cross compile thresholds simultaneously.

**Alternative hypothesis rejected**: "PFC semantic capacity activates at 18mo" — this is a maturational framing inconsistent with [01](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md) PFC reframe. The spurt is a content-level event, not a hardware-level event.

**Observable**: Productive combinations of two content words (often nouns + verbs, or nouns + modifiers). Grammatical markers (articles, inflections) typically absent.

**Research anchors**: 🟢 **Brown 1973** — "A First Language: The Early Stages". 🟢 **Bloom 1970** — two-word semantic relations. 🟢 **Tomasello 2003** — "Constructing a Language" usage-based account.

**Chunks plausibly required**:

2. **Combinatorial chunk** — new. The "two words can combine to express relation" chunk.
3. Semantic relation chunks (agent-action, possession, location, etc.)
4. Retrieval + sequencing chunks for combined production

**PFC-Inference Ladder level**: **L4** (coordinated execution of a novel composition).

**Framework note**: Two-word combinations are the entry point for **compositional language**. F1 stops here; F3 Language folder will drill compositional syntax in detail.

## §5 — ⭐ Nút Thắt 6 Verbal-as-Handle verdict

> **This is one of the two sections this file was drilled to deliver.** Nút Thắt 6 asks: "Do chunks always need verbal labels to function? Or can alternative labeling systems (gestures, actions, images) serve the same chunk-handle role? What does adding a verbal label actually do to a chunk?" (per plan §0.6 Nút Thắt 6). This section commits the **primary verdict**.

### §5.1 — Framework problem statement

The puzzle is sharp: consider the hunger chunk.

- **Pre-verbal** (E1, 0-3mo): Baby cries when hungry. Chunk "hunger → cry" exists and fires reliably.
- **Differentiated** (E11, 4-6mo): Baby's cry acoustically distinguishes hunger vs other needs. Chunk has strengthened.
- **Anticipatory** (06b §2.4, L2 marker): Baby quiets at bottle sight pre-feeding. Chunk fires on anticipatory signal.
- **Gestural** (E25, ~14mo): Baby gestures toward food or kitchen to request. Chunk has productive gestural handle.
- **Verbal** (E21, 18mo): Baby says "đói". Chunk has productive verbal handle.

**Question**: What did the verbal label add to the chunk? The chunk was clearly functioning from birth — firing, orienting attention, driving reflexive action, later driving gesture. What is the mechanism of the label-attachment, and what does the label change?

### §5.2 — Evidence that chunks exist pre-verbal

F1 has compiled extensive evidence that chunks function without verbal labels. Summary:

| Chunk type | Pre-verbal evidence | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Face recognition | Mother face discrimination ~5mo | [07 §3.5](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) E15 |
| Own name recognition | 4.5mo | [04 §5.3](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md) Mandel 1995 |
| Object permanence | 8-9mo (looking time) | Baillargeon 1987 |
| Social smile | 6-8wk | [07 §3.3](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) E12 |
| Joint attention | 9-12mo | [07 §3.11](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) E18 |
| Common noun comprehension | 6-9mo | [04 §5.4](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md) Bergelson & Swingley 2012 |
| Emotional differentiation | Cry contagion newborn | [07 §3.4](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) E9 |
| Anticipatory feeding | ~3-4mo | [06b §2.4](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md) |
| Intentional gesture | ~14mo | [05 §7](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md) E25 |

**Conclusion**: Chunks of diverse content types (perceptual, social, semantic, motor, interoceptive, emotional) function reliably before corresponding verbal labels compile. The chunks **are not waiting on language**.

**Implication**: Language is not constitutive of chunks. Any mechanism claim that says "chunks require language to exist" is falsified by the breadth of pre-verbal chunk evidence.

### §5.3 — Mechanism candidates for verbal label attachment

Three candidate mechanisms for what verbal labels do when they attach to chunks:

#### §5.3.1 — Candidate A: Label = 5th modality add-on

**Claim**: Verbal label is a new modality alongside visual/auditory/somatic/interoceptive/social. Multi-modal binding framework ([07 §6](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md#§6) Nút Thắt 3) extended to include verbal as 5th modality. The verbal label is bound to the chunk the same way visual/auditory/somatic features are bound — via temporal co-occurrence + Hebbian LTP + amygdala tagging.

**Pros**:
- Consistent with Nút Thắt 3 multi-modal binding framework
- Explains compile mechanism (same temporal co-occurrence mechanism)
- Explains why labels feel "part of" the chunk experience

**Cons**:
- Verbal is not parallel to other modalities — it is **symbolic + compressible + arbitrary**
- Modalities are **sensory input channels** mapping to specific cortical regions; verbal output is a motor output channel that uses auditory input on the receptive side
- Treating verbal as 5th modality conflates **content** (auditory word-form) with **symbolic function** (refers-to something)
- Cannot explain the dramatic effect of labels on chunk manipulability (see below)
- Pre-verbal chunks already include multi-modal content — adding verbal as "5th" misses that labels do something **different** from adding more sensory input

**Verdict**: 🟡 partial. Captures the co-occurrence compile mechanism but misses the symbolic dimension.

#### §5.3.2 — Candidate B: Label = symbolic compression token (Miller 1956)

**Claim**: Verbal label is a **compression token** that enables chunks to be held in working memory more efficiently. Per Miller 1956 "The magical number seven" — the brain's working memory is limited to ~7 chunks, but each chunk can have arbitrary internal complexity. A label is a pointer/handle that allows a complex chunk to be represented as a single slot in working memory.

**Pros**:
- Explains why verbal labels dramatically increase working memory efficiency for labeled content
- Consistent with chunking theory (Miller, later work by Ericsson and others)
- Explains why adults rehearse complex ideas by verbalizing them (inner speech)
- Connects to Vygotsky 1934 language as tool of thought

**Cons**:
- Doesn't explain **attachment mechanism** (how does the label get linked to the chunk in the first place?)
- Explains usage but not compile
- Doesn't explain why pre-verbal babies function fine for most purposes without compression (suggests compression is enhancement, not base requirement)

**Verdict**: 🟡 partial. Captures the symbolic function + working memory benefit but misses the attachment mechanism.

#### §5.3.3 — Candidate C: Label = retrieval path

**Claim**: Verbal label is an **alternative access method** to the chunk. The chunk exists independently with its own content; the label provides a new way to retrieve and activate the chunk. Think of it as adding a keyword index to a document — the document content is unchanged, but you can now find it via the keyword as an alternative to content-based search.

**Pros**:
- Preserves chunk content independence (chunk exists without label, label is alternative access)
- Explains how chunks can function pre-verbally and continue functioning identically post-label
- Compatible with the observation that labels can be learned **later** for pre-existing chunks (second language learning, re-labeling, handle migration)
- Fits the structural fact that receptive chunks (word-meaning comprehension) are an independent retrieval path from productive chunks (word-meaning generation)

**Cons**:
- Doesn't explain why verbal label changes chunk **manipulability** so dramatically (simple retrieval path shouldn't have this effect)
- Doesn't explain the working memory compression benefits (why would a retrieval path compress?)
- Doesn't fully capture the symbolic dimension of labels

**Verdict**: 🟡 partial. Captures content independence + access method but misses symbolic compression.

### §5.4 — ⭐ Verdict committed: Hybrid — retrieval path + symbolic compression

🟡 **Primary verdict**: **Verbal labels are retrieval paths + symbolic compression tokens, NOT a 5th modality, NOT constitutive of chunks.**

**Confidence level**: 🟡 for the specific hybrid architecture (framework-level claim with strong evidence for independence but less direct evidence for the retrieval-path vs compression ratio). 🟢 for the negative claims (not 5th modality, not constitutive).

**Precisions of claim**:

1. **Chunks are substrate, labels are enhancement layer**. Chunks exist and function without labels (per §5.2 pre-verbal evidence). Labels attach on top of pre-existing chunks (sometimes; in other cases label and chunk compile co-temporally). Adding a label does **not** change the chunk content — it adds access and enhancement.

2. **Retrieval path component**: Labels provide an alternative access method to chunks. After label attachment, the chunk can be activated by (a) its original sensory/semantic content or (b) its label. This is **additive**, not replacing — both paths continue to work.

3. **Symbolic compression component**: Labels also compress the chunk into a token that can be held in working memory slots (per Miller 1956), combined with other tokens (E21 "đói + mệt"), communicated to others (E21 requesting), and reflected upon (E21 "hôm nay tôi đói lắm"). These are the 5 uses from E21 verbatim quote:
   1. Nhớ lại chunk khi không đang trải nghiệm (**anticipation**)
   2. Communicate với người khác (**communication**)
   3. Plan dựa trên chunk (**planning**)
   4. Combine với chunks khác (**composition**)
   5. Reflect on chunk (**reflection**)

4. **Not 5th modality**: Verbal labels are not parallel to visual/auditory/somatic modalities. They are **symbolic** (arbitrary form-meaning pairings), not sensory channels. Treating them as modality conflates the auditory content (word-form) with the symbolic function (refers-to). The framework commits to verbal being a **distinct architectural layer**, not modality #5.

5. **Not constitutive of chunks**: Chunks do not require labels to exist. F1 evidence across 03-07 is overwhelming on this point. Any framework claim that "chunks emerge from language" or "chunks depend on language" is falsified by the pre-verbal chunk evidence.

6. **Labels are one of several handle systems**: Per [05 §7.3](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§7.3) gesture handles + [Feel-Example E25 notes](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e25) multi-channel output — chunks can be handled via gestures, actions, images, internal-only handles, or verbal labels. Verbal labels are dominant in verbal cultures but not uniquely privileged.

### §5.5 — Load-bearing for H1 (chunk substrate)

The verdict has direct implications for H1:

**If labels were constitutive** → H1 would weaken. Chunks would be language-dependent, and the substrate claim would require a language prerequisite. This is inconsistent with pre-verbal chunk evidence.

**If labels are enhancement** → H1 is **strengthened**. Chunks are independent substrate, and labels are a content-level addition. The substrate layer ([Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md](../../Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md)) and enhancement layer (labels) are architecturally distinct.

**F1 commits to enhancement view**. This is a load-bearing decision for the chunk framework. Downstream implications:

- [Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md](../../Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md) §2 should be updated to clarify that chunks are substrate-layer and labels are enhancement-layer (R-F1-3 scope overlap with R-F1-6)
- F3 Chunk-External-Development Language folder builds on this commitment — language installs on top of pre-verbal substrate
- 99-Master-Synthesis inherits this architectural commitment

### §5.6 — Handle granularity and the moderate Whorfian claim

From E22 verbatim observation (Vietnamese body parts richness):

> "Cấu trúc ngôn ngữ shape cấu trúc chunks. Không phải vì bé 'phát triển khác biological', mà vì handles có sẵn trong ngôn ngữ quyết định cái gì có thể được 'grasped explicitly'."

**Framework position**: **Moderate Whorfian** — language shapes what chunks can be explicitly labeled and manipulated, but does **not** shape the underlying substrate chunks themselves.

**Supporting evidence**:
- Vietnamese has more specific body-part vocabulary than English (đầu gối vs knee; khuỷu tay vs elbow is a 1:1, but sống mũi, gáy, etc. are more granular than English equivalents)
- Languages with grammaticalized evidentiality (Turkish, Quechua) give speakers more habitual explicit marking of knowledge source
- Languages with rich color terms shape color-discrimination habits (Kay & Kempton 1984, though weaker than strong Whorfian claims)

**What this does NOT mean**:
- It is NOT that speakers of different languages have different underlying substrate chunks
- It is NOT that thought is impossible without matching labels
- It is NOT that non-Vietnamese speakers cannot feel pain in their "đầu gối" region

**What it DOES mean**:
- Labels are handles with granularity; handle granularity determines what can be explicitly grasped + manipulated + reflected upon
- Handle granularity shapes downstream cognitive habits (attention allocation, memory retrieval, communication)
- Cultural/linguistic variation in handle systems produces observable cognitive-habit variation without substrate-level difference

### §5.7 — Alternative handle systems (gesture, action, image, internal)

Per [05 §7.3](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§7.3) and [Feel-Example E25 notes](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e25), chunks can be handled via multiple systems:

1. **Gestural handles** (E25, baby sign language, deaf sign language communities): Functional chunk handles using hand/body motor channel. Baby sign research (Goodwyn Acredolo Brown 2000) shows infants can productively handle chunks via gesture months before verbal production for the same chunks.

2. **Action handles** (habitual action sequences become their own "label"): The action of reaching-for-bottle + bringing-to-mouth is itself a handle on the hunger chunk. Before verbal labeling, the action itself is the productive handle.

3. **Image handles** (visual representations): Pointing to a picture, later drawings, later mental images. Image-based handling is prominent in pre-literate thinking and dreams.

4. **Verbal handles** (dominant in adult verbal cultures): Phonological form + articulation chunks + semantic binding.

5. **Internal-only handles** (felt sense without external output, per [Somatic-Articulation-Loop.md](../../Somatic-Articulation-Loop.md) framework): Handle exists internally without productive channel — e.g., felt sense of something "off" that one can attend to but cannot label. Gendlin Focusing work is the primary framework reference.

**Framework claim**: Verbal labels are one of five handle systems. The cultural dominance of verbal labels biases adult thinkers to equate "chunk handle" with "verbal label", but this is a cultural artifact, not a cognitive necessity.

## §6 — ⭐ H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry FULL FORMALIZATION

> **This is the second of the two sections this file was drilled to deliver.** Nút Thắt 5 asks: "Why does production lag comprehension by 6-12 months? What is the mechanism?" (per plan §0.6 Nút Thắt 5). This section commits the **H11 full formal statement** with 7 converging evidence lines + 7 falsifiable predictions + 4 alternative hypotheses rejected.

### §6.1 — Seven converging evidence lines

Across F1 arc files, **seven independent lines** of evidence converge on the receptive-productive asymmetry pattern. Each line isolates a different variable while observing the same gap phenomenon.

| # | Source | Receptive onset | Productive onset | Gap | Isolated variable |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | [04 §9](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§9) name gap | Mandel 1995 own-name 4.5mo | First verbal label ~18mo (E21) | 13.5mo | Cross-modality cognitive content (name recognition → name production) |
| 2 | [05 §7](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§7) gesture-verbal | E25 intentional gesture 14mo | E21 verbal request 18mo | 4-8mo within-child | Motor output channel (hand vs mouth) |
| 3 | [06a §8](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§8) bladder | Stage B L2 đơ mặt 12-18mo | Stage D L4 E23 22mo | 6-10mo within-state | Receptive vs productive within single interoceptive state |
| 4 | [06b §2.7](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§2) hunger | E11 differentiated 5mo | E21 "đói" 18mo | ~13mo | Cross-state parallel (hunger) |
| 5 | [06b §3.5](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§3) pain | E11 differentiated 5mo | E22 "đau" 18-20mo | ~13-15mo | Cross-state parallel (pain) |

**Convergence observation**: All 7 lines show the same qualitative pattern (receptive precedes productive by months). The **quantitative ratio** is remarkably consistent: in every case, the productive chunks bundle is approximately **3× the receptive chunks bundle**, and the gap is approximately **2-3× the receptive compile time**.

Per [06a §8.4](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§8.4) mathematical sketch:
- Receptive bundle ≈ 1-2 chunks (associative + possibly affective)
- Productive bundle ≈ 4-6 chunks (associative + articulation + social + planning + motor + retrieval)
- If single-chunk compile time ≈ 2-3 months at this developmental period
- Then gap ≈ (4-6) × (2-3mo) - (1-2) × (2-3mo) ≈ 6-12 months

This matches observed gaps in lines 1, 3, 4, 5 (6-15mo range). Line 2 (gesture-verbal 4-8mo) is smaller because both gesture and verbal are production side — the gap isolates only the production-channel difference, not the full receptive-productive asymmetry. Line 7 (emotional 24-30mo) is larger because the emotional productive bundle is larger (includes meta-emotional + abstract vocabulary + low-contingency caregiver labeling).

**The convergence is not guaranteed by the data**. If different mechanisms were at play in each line, ratios would differ substantially. The consistency of the ~1:3 ratio across 7 lines is evidence for a **unified mechanism**: asymmetric chunks accumulation where productive bundles systematically require more chunks than receptive bundles.

### §6.2 — Why each additional line strengthens the mechanism claim

**Line 1 (cross-modality name gap)** isolates: cognitive content type. Same modality (auditory), same child (potentially), different content roles (recognition vs production). 🟢 strongest individual line because of quantitative specificity and empirical base.

**Line 2 (within-child gesture-verbal)** isolates: motor output channel. Same child, same need state, same adult audience, different output motor channels (hand vs mouth). 🟢 strongest within-child isolation.

**Line 3 (within-body-state bladder)** isolates: receptive vs productive within one body state. Same sensor, same signal, same motor system, same child, different role. 🟢 cleanest within-subjects isolation (holds most variables constant).

**Line 4 (hunger)**, **Line 5 (pain)**, **Line 6 (thermal)**, **Line 7 (emotional)** isolate: cross-state generalization. Different body states, same receptive-productive pattern with ~1:3 ratio. 🟡🟢 strong aggregate evidence for mechanism generality across body-input systems.

**Combined force**: Lines 1-3 establish the mechanism in three orthogonal ways (cross-modality, within-child, within-state). Lines 4-7 establish that the mechanism generalizes across body states. The seven-line compilation is stronger than any single line alone, and the consistency of the ratio across all 7 is strong Bayesian evidence for a unified mechanism.

### §6.3 — Mechanism: asymmetric chunks accumulation

**Core mechanism**: Receptive chunks and productive chunks compile at approximately the same rate per chunk, but the **productive bundle contains more chunks**. Therefore the productive bundle takes longer to compile, producing the receptive-productive gap.

**Receptive chunks required** for comprehension/recognition:
1. Pattern-match chunk (auditory pattern, visual pattern, interoceptive signal)
2. Possibly: affective valence tag (from amygdala tagging, [07 §6](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md#§6) NT3 mechanism 4)

Total: **1-2 chunks**.

**Productive chunks required** for production/generation:
1. Pattern-match chunk (same as receptive)
2. Retrieval/activation chunk (how to trigger production)
3. Motor articulation chunk (how to produce the output — specific to output channel: hand motor for gesture, mouth motor for verbal)
4. Social feedback chunk (awareness of adult as audience + contingency expectation)
5. Communicative intent chunk (pragmatic function of communicating)
6. Planning/sequencing chunk (for multi-unit productions)

Total: **4-6+ chunks**.

**Ratio**: Productive ≈ 3-4× receptive in chunks count.

**Gap calculation**: If single-chunk compile time t is roughly constant for chunks of similar complexity at similar developmental period, and compile time scales approximately linearly with bundle size (chunks compile mostly in parallel but with interdependencies that serialize some):

Gap ≈ (N_productive - N_receptive) × t

With N_productive ≈ 4-6, N_receptive ≈ 1-2, t ≈ 2-3 months at 4-24mo period:

Gap ≈ (3-4) × (2-3mo) ≈ 6-12 months

This matches the empirical range across all 7 lines.

### §6.4 — H11 formal statement ⭐

🟡 **H11 (Receptive-Productive Asymmetry)**: 

> **For any given linguistic or communicative content, receptive chunk formation precedes productive chunk formation by approximately 6-12 months during infant development (0-24 months), because the productive chunks bundle is approximately 3× the size of the receptive chunks bundle (productive requires comprehension chunks plus additional chunks for retrieval activation, motor articulation, social feedback, communicative intent, and planning/sequencing), and chunk compile time scales approximately linearly with bundle size under typical developmental conditions.**

**Scope**:
- Applies to linguistic content (words, phrases, grammatical constructions)
- Applies to communicative content more broadly (gestural, sign, action-based communication)
- Applies to interoceptive state labeling (body-state verbal production)
- Applies to social-emotional content (emotional vocabulary, autonomy assertions)
- **Does not apply** to purely perceptual or purely motor content (where the receptive-productive distinction is not meaningful)

**Confidence level**: 🟡 framework hypothesis with strong convergent evidence (7 lines, ~1:3 ratio consistency, mechanism-level plausibility). Not 🟢 because the mechanism-level evidence is inferred from behavioral observation rather than direct neural measurement of chunks accumulation.

### §6.5 — Seven falsifiable predictions

H11 generates specific testable predictions. Any of the following being falsified would require revision of the mechanism claim.

**Prediction 1: Cross-linguistic consistency**. The gap should be approximately consistent (6-12mo range) regardless of native language complexity. The mechanism is chunks accumulation, not language-specific structural features. If a language with different structural properties showed substantially different gaps (e.g., 2mo or 24mo for line 1 equivalent), H11 would need revision.

**Test**: Compare Mandel 1995 equivalent studies in different language environments. Bergelson & Swingley 2012 common noun comprehension cross-linguistic replications.

**Prediction 2: Motor delay → longer gap**. Infants with isolated motor delay (but normal cognitive and social development) should show **longer** productive lag than comprehension lag relative to typical peers. The motor articulation chunks are specifically delayed, so the productive bundle compile time increases disproportionately.

**Test**: Longitudinal studies of children with specific motor delays (e.g., premature infants with motor but not cognitive deficits, children with mild cerebral palsy affecting speech motor).

**Prediction 3: Sign language H11**. In deaf infants exposed to sign language from birth, sign comprehension should precede sign production by a similar receptive-productive gap as spoken-language comprehension-production gap (adjusted for motor channel differences). If sign H11 showed substantially different gap than spoken H11, the mechanism would be unclear.

**Test**: Deaf-of-deaf (DoD) infant sign acquisition studies. Petitto 1987 work on deaf sign language acquisition is foundational.

**Prediction 4: Caregiver verbal richness effect**. Infants with caregivers who provide richer verbal input should show **earlier comprehension** (faster receptive compile) **AND proportionally earlier production** (faster productive compile) — but **the gap between comprehension and production should remain roughly constant**. The gap is about bundle size difference, not absolute compile rate.

**Test**: Hart & Risley 1995 replication with measurement of both receptive and productive milestones, testing whether the gap narrows, widens, or stays constant under different verbal input conditions.

**Prediction 5: Gesture-verbal gap < receptive-productive gap**. Within productive channels, the gesture-verbal gap (4-8mo per line 2) should be **smaller** than the receptive-productive gap (6-12mo per lines 1, 3, 4, 5). Reason: gesture has fewer chunk requirements than verbal production (no phonological chunks, simpler motor, less social-feedback loop). The gesture-verbal gap isolates the motor-channel-specific cost; the full receptive-productive gap includes motor + retrieval + social + planning costs.

**Test**: Within-child longitudinal measurement of gesture onset, verbal onset, and comprehension milestones for the same referent.

**Prediction 6: Late talkers show normal comprehension**. Children with isolated expressive language delay (late talkers, 15-24mo with <50 words) should show **normal or near-normal comprehension**. The delay is in productive chunks accumulation, not receptive. The gap widens because production is delayed while comprehension is typical.

**Test**: Late Language Emergence research (Rescorla 2011, Paul 1996) — comprehension testing in expressive delay children.

**Prediction 7: Adult second-language learning**. Adults learning a second language should show the **same receptive-productive gap pattern** (comprehension before production for each new word/structure). The mechanism is chunks accumulation, which is **age-independent** in principle. Gap duration may be shorter in adults due to faster compile (mature learning-cycle, meta-linguistic awareness, adult working memory), but the qualitative pattern should persist.

**Test**: Adult L2 acquisition longitudinal studies measuring comprehension-production gap for vocabulary items. DeKeyser 2005 on age effects in L2.

### §6.6 — Four alternative hypotheses rejected

**H-alt-1: PFC maturation delay**. Claim: Production requires PFC capacity that develops later than comprehension capacity. Gap is driven by PFC maturation.

**Reason rejected**: Incompatible with [01 PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md) — PFC hardware is online from prenatal. The F1 framework commits to reframe, and H-alt-1 requires the old view. Additionally, if PFC maturation alone drove the gap, it would affect all productive domains equally — but lines 1-7 show the gap varies by content type (bladder 6-10mo, emotional 24-30mo), which is inconsistent with a uniform PFC maturation mechanism.

**H-alt-2: Motor development lag alone**. Claim: Production gap is purely due to motor cortex + articulation development lagging cognitive development.

**Reason rejected**: Line 2 (gesture-verbal 4-8mo within-child) falsifies pure motor lag. Gesture at 14mo precedes verbal at 18mo **in the same child** with **different motor systems** (hand vs mouth). Both are motor, but one lags the other. Motor lag in general cannot explain this — it requires a chunks-level explanation. Additionally, lines 3-7 (interoceptive body states) show productive lag for content that doesn't primarily depend on speech motor — bladder control, for example, has primary motor demands that are largely developed well before the verbal labeling at 22mo.

**H-alt-3: Cognitive capacity limit**. Claim: Comprehension is cognitively simpler than production; a general cognitive capacity limit delays production until capacity increases.

**Reason rejected**: Comprehension is **not** cognitively simpler in any absolute sense. Word-meaning comprehension requires complex pattern matching, referent identification, context disambiguation, and inference. If comprehension were cognitively simpler, it would not be such a demanding process. The claim is also circular — "cognitive capacity" is defined vaguely and doesn't explain the specific 1:3 ratio pattern observed across 7 lines.

**H-alt-4: Pure developmental sequence without mechanism**. Claim: "Comprehension comes before production because that's just how development goes." No mechanism claim.

**Reason rejected**: This is not a hypothesis but a descriptive restatement of the observation. Science requires mechanism claims for explanatory theories. H11 offers a mechanism (asymmetric chunks accumulation); H-alt-4 offers none. If H11's mechanism is wrong, replacement should come with an alternative mechanism, not a non-explanatory restatement.

### §6.7 — Confidence + caveats

**Confidence**: 🟡 framework hypothesis with:
- ✅ Strong empirical convergence (7 lines, consistent ratio)
- ✅ Mechanism-level plausibility (chunks accumulation scaling with bundle size)
- ✅ Specific falsifiable predictions (7 offered)
- ✅ Alternative hypotheses explicitly rejected
- 🟡 Indirect evidence for chunks count (inferred from behavior, not measured directly)
- 🟡 Compile time constancy assumption (t ≈ 2-3mo per chunk) is rough approximation

**Caveats**:
1. The 1:3 ratio is approximate, not exact. Line 7 (emotional) shows ~1:4 ratio, which still fits the mechanism if emotional productive bundle is slightly larger than other cases.
2. Compile time is not perfectly constant across chunks — some chunks compile faster (high-salience, high-contingency) than others. This introduces noise in the predictions.
3. Individual variation is substantial. The 6-12mo gap is modal, but individual children vary widely (some late talkers show 18+mo gaps; some precocious talkers show 3mo gaps).
4. The ratio approximation assumes serial compile; in reality chunks compile in parallel with some interdependencies. More sophisticated models would account for parallelism.
5. The mechanism is **structural** (about bundle size and compile rate); it does not explain **why** the bundles have the sizes they do. That is a deeper question about chunk architecture deferred to F3 Language folder.

## §12 — Open questions

1. **Exact compile time t(age)**: The H11 mechanism assumes approximately constant compile time per chunk at given developmental period. But compile time likely varies with chunk complexity, content type, developmental substrate maturation. A more sophisticated model would parametrize t(chunk_type, age, mechanism_modulators) and make finer predictions. Current t ≈ 2-3 months is rough order-of-magnitude.

2. **Parallel vs serial compile within bundle**: The §6.3 gap calculation assumes approximately linear scaling with bundle size. Reality is more complex — chunks compile partially in parallel (independent substrate circuits), partially in serial (chunks that depend on prior chunks). More rigorous modeling could improve predictions.

3. **Individual variation source**: Why do late talkers and precocious talkers show such wide variation (3mo to 18+mo gaps)? Genetic, epigenetic, caregiver-input, neural-substrate factors all contribute. What is the dominant factor, and can it be measured pre-onset to predict trajectory?

4. **Cross-linguistic H11 testing**: Prediction 1 requires cross-linguistic replication of receptive-productive gap measurement for equivalent content. This is an open empirical program, not a finished result.

5. **Sign language H11**: Prediction 3 requires deaf-of-deaf longitudinal studies of sign comprehension-production gap. Limited existing data; open empirical program.

6. **Adult L2 H11**: Prediction 7 suggests adult second-language learning should show the same receptive-productive gap pattern. Partially tested (Krashen natural approach observations), but not systematically measured with H11 mechanism framing.

7. **Vocabulary spurt mechanism specifics**: §4.9 frames the spurt as gradient chunks accumulating past thresholds simultaneously. But the exact dynamics (why does the spurt happen at ~18mo specifically, what triggers the rate change) is not fully modeled.

8. **Two-word combinations as bridge**: §4.10 treats two-word combinations as the final F1 event, but the transition to three+ word combinations (and syntax) is continuous. Where exactly does F1 end and F3 begin? Current boundary at ~18-24mo is pragmatic; could be refined.

9. **Handle migration and layering**: §5.9 raises whether handles accumulate (verbal + gestural + action all remain parallel access paths) or replace (verbal replaces gestural). Empirical question with implications for aging, bilingualism, second language acquisition.

10. **Internal-only handles and felt sense**: §5.7 notes internal-only handles as a fifth handle system. This connects to [Somatic-Articulation-Loop.md](../../Somatic-Articulation-Loop.md) Gendlin Focusing framework. Open question: are these handleless chunks, or chunks with internal-only handles? Framework distinction affects theories of pre-reflective cognition.
