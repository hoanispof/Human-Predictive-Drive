## §0 — Abstract

PFC hardware is online from prenatal. Synaptogenesis puts newborn PFC synaptic density already at adult threshold (Huttenlocher 1979), peaking around 15 months postnatal at approximately 2x adult density (Huttenlocher & Dabholkar 1997). fNIRS studies confirm mPFC activation to eye contact at 4 months (Grossmann 2013) and dlPFC activation predicting hierarchical rule learning at 8 months (Werchan et al. 2016). Resting-state network architecture — including frontoparietal executive control and default mode network — is already a full adult-like facsimile by term birth (Doria et al. 2010). The frontal consciousness signature (P3b-like) is present from 5 months (Kouider et al. 2013), showing the same nonlinear access-consciousness mechanism as in adults, only slower and weaker. Newborns 2-5 days old already prefer direct gaze with ERP evidence of frontal processing (Farroni et al. 2002). Hodel 2018 is the explicit reframe anchor paper: "**perception of [PFC] as structurally and functionally underdeveloped in young infants and toddlers**" is an "**unfortunate cost**" of the older "late-developing" view. What develops slowly through age is: (1) compiled chunks from experience, (2) myelination of PFC intracortical fibers through the third decade (Yakovlev & Lecours 1967), (3) synaptic pruning through adolescence, (4) long-range integration efficiency. Framework implication for F1: drop "PFC developmental timeline" as the primary analytical axis. Adopt **event-inference methodology** — list observable events, infer what chunks must have compiled for each event to be possible, acknowledge PFC pattern-match firing where behavioral markers warrant it. Every PFC-involvement claim carries 🟡 plausibility qualifier; every observable behavior carries 🟢 where empirical support exists. This file delivers: (a) historical context for the old view + why it persisted pre-2000 (pre-fNIRS imaging era); (b) 5 evidence pillars each with research citations and interpretive notes; (c) Hodel 2018 anchor deep exposition; (d) hardware vs content architectural distinction table; (e) event-inference methodology foundation for subsequent arc files; (f) three concrete worked examples ("cannot feel the urge to urinate," "cannot recognize mother calling their name," "cannot communicate normally with adults") showing the reframe in action; (g) framework update recommendation R-F1-1 for 99-Master-Synthesis commit.

## §2 — The Old View: "PFC Offline In Infants"

### §2.1 — What the old view claimed

🔴 **Old claim** (as stated in PFC-Analysis.md pre-reframe, §4 timeline box original version):

> "FETAL → 2 YEARS OLD: PFC nearly offline. Parents = 'external PFC' for the child."

And in Body-Parenting-Optimization.md (pre-reframe, line 78):

> "PFC: ~10-20% → BARELY any Imagine-Final capacity"

These claims framed infant PFC as **structurally and functionally underdeveloped**, implying:
- Hardware not yet operational
- Processing capacity near zero
- Caregiver provides external replacement for missing PFC
- Infant behavior driven entirely by subcortical + body-base reflexes
- "Development" means "PFC turning on"

### §2.2 — Historical context — why the view persisted

🟡 The "PFC offline" view did not emerge from malice or careless scholarship. It emerged from real methodological limitations of the pre-neuroimaging era (roughly pre-2000 for infant-appropriate imaging):

**Limitation 1 — No non-invasive infant imaging before fNIRS**:
- Adult PFC imaging began routinely with PET (1980s) and fMRI (early 1990s)
- Infant imaging was effectively blocked until **functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS)** became developmentally appropriate around 2000-2005
- Before fNIRS, infant PFC could only be studied via:
  - Behavioral inference (working memory tasks, delayed response)
  - Post-mortem histology (Huttenlocher 1979 was an early exception)
  - Clinical cases (anencephalic infants, focal PFC lesions)
- Behavioral inference is fundamentally limited: an infant failing a delayed-response task could mean "no PFC function" OR "no chunks compiled for this task." The two hypotheses make identical behavioral predictions in pre-verbal subjects.

**Limitation 2 — Piaget's developmental stages framing**:
- Piaget (1936, 1952) framed infant cognition through stages of operations (sensorimotor → preoperational → concrete operational → formal operational)
- Stage language naturally invited a "stages of PFC" interpretation
- Piaget himself was more cautious, but post-Piagetian developmental psychology often collapsed "cognitive operations emerging" into "PFC maturing"

**Limitation 3 — Available neurobiology evidence was myelination-dominant**:
- Yakovlev & Lecours 1967 established PFC myelination continues through the third decade — a striking, famous finding
- Myelination slowness was extrapolated to "PFC is slow to develop" without distinguishing myelination (signal speed) from hardware (neurons + synapses + networks)
- "Late-developing" became shorthand that elided the distinction

**Limitation 4 — Behavioral failures were highly visible**:
- Infants visibly fail at delayed response, A-not-B search, verbal tasks, complex planning
- Visible failures are salient; visible successes on more basic tasks (face preference, statistical learning, intuitive physics) were often attributed to subcortical or "core knowledge" modules rather than PFC

### §2.3 — Why the view became problematic around 2000-2015

🟢 Several converging lines of evidence made the old view untenable:

1. **fNIRS becomes infant-appropriate** (2005+): First studies with robust infant-adapted fNIRS begin showing PFC activation to social stimuli in 4-6 month olds
2. **Preterm fMRI** (Doria et al. 2010): 70 preterm infants 29-43 weeks postmenstrual show adult-like resting-state network architecture by term
3. **Developmental EEG** (Kouider et al. 2013): Same frontal consciousness signature as adult observed at 5 months, just slower and weaker
4. **Huttenlocher re-examined**: Original 1979 data showed newborn PFC synaptic density already at adult threshold — a fact that had been present but under-emphasized for 20+ years

By 2018, sufficient evidence accumulated for a full explicit reframe paper: **Hodel 2018** "Rapid Infant Prefrontal Cortex Development and Sensitivity to Early Environmental Experience" (*Developmental Review* 48:113-144). Hodel directly names the old "late-developing" framing as an "unfortunate cost" and argues PFC should be characterized as a "rapidly developing region" with specific vulnerability windows driven by environmental sensitivity.

### §2.4 — Why the view persists in some framework writing

Even after 2018, the "PFC offline" framing persists in:
- Framework teaching materials aimed at non-specialist audiences
- Popular developmental psychology books
- Parenting guides that use "PFC not yet developed" as shorthand
- Some academic writing in child development focusing on behavioral deficits without imaging evidence

This is understandable shorthand but creates a cascading error: **if PFC is assumed offline, then chunks are invisible** (nothing to drive chunk formation under the model), and the whole question "how do chunks compile from zero at birth" becomes unaskable.

**F1 drill rejects this shorthand**. The cost of sloppy framing is too high — it hides the actual developmental mechanism (chunks accumulation) behind a false one (PFC maturation).

## §3 — 5 Evidence Pillars for the Reframe

> This section is the **citation backbone** of F1 drill. Every arc file citation back to "PFC hardware is online" traces to these 5 pillars. All 5 are 🟢 (multiple independent studies, converging methods).

### §3.1 — Pillar 1: Synaptogenesis — PFC is OVER-supplied at birth, not under-supplied

🟢 **Claim**: PFC synaptic density at newborn is already at adult threshold. Peak density occurs postnatally around 15 months at approximately twice the adult density. Pruning through adolescence brings density back down.

**Primary citations**:
- **Huttenlocher 1979** (*Brain Research* 163:195-205). "Synaptic density in human frontal cortex — developmental changes and effects of aging." First histological demonstration that synaptic density in middle frontal gyrus at newborn is already at adult levels. Peak around 1-2 years postnatal, gradual decline through adolescence and adulthood.
- **Huttenlocher & Dabholkar 1997** (*Journal of Comparative Neurology* 387:167-178). "Regional differences in synaptogenesis in human cerebral cortex." Refined the 1979 data with regional specificity. Synaptogenesis in PFC middle frontal gyrus **begins prenatally** (detectable from ~27 weeks conceptual age), rises sharply through the first postnatal year, peaks around 15 months at **roughly 2x adult density**, then gradually prunes through adolescence, reaching adult levels around age 16-20.

**Regional note**: Different cortical regions have different synaptogenesis timecourses. Visual cortex (V1) peaks earlier (around 4 months). PFC peaks later (around 15 months). Auditory cortex intermediate (around 3 months). **All three peak within the F1 drill window (0-24mo) or just before.**

**Interpretation for F1**:
- Hardware is not "developing" during the F1 window in the sense of "being built from scratch." Hardware is **already over-supplied** at birth.
- The developmental task is **not building hardware** but **compiling content** (chunks) to use existing hardware.
- Pruning is not "removing hardware"; it is **eliminating unused pathways** to sharpen signal-to-noise ratio.
- Synaptic over-production is consistent with the **experience-selection model** (Changeux & Dehaene 1989; Edelman 1987 selectionism): genetic program over-produces, experience selects which stay.

**Why this falsifies old view**:
- Under the old "PFC offline" view, synaptic density should be LOW at birth and RISE as PFC "comes online." This is the opposite of what Huttenlocher observed.
- Under the reframe, high synaptic density at birth is **expected** — the hardware is ready for content to be compiled.

🟢 Confidence: STRONG. Histological method is gold standard for synapse counting. Multiple independent labs have replicated similar curves (Petanjek et al. 2011 confirms and extends Huttenlocher).

### §3.2 — Pillar 2: fNIRS shows active PFC response from early months

🟢 **Claim**: Infant PFC is not silent. fNIRS studies show active hemodynamic responses to social, rule-learning, and attention-related stimuli from as early as 4 months, with responses structurally similar (though weaker and slower) to adult.

**Primary citations**:

- **Grossmann et al. 2013** (*Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* 7:340). "Eye contact and the emergence of social cognition in infants." mPFC activation measured via fNIRS in 4-month-old infants viewing faces with direct vs averted gaze. Direct gaze produces **stronger mPFC activation** than averted gaze. Implication: mPFC is not only active but **differentially responsive** to socially meaningful stimuli at 4 months.

- **Werchan, Collins, Frank & Amso 2016** (*Journal of Neuroscience* 36:10314-10322). "Role of prefrontal cortex in learning and generalizing hierarchical rules in 8-month-old infants." 8-month-olds performed a hierarchical rule-learning task while fNIRS monitored right dlPFC. Key findings:
  - Right dlPFC activation **increased when rule sets switched** (analogous to task-switching cost in adults)
  - Amplitude of dlPFC activation **predicted individual differences in learning performance**
  - This is the closest available evidence to a causal PFC-cognition link in infants (correlational but with specific amplitude-performance coupling)

- **Farroni, Csibra, Simion & Johnson 2002** (*PNAS* 99:9602-9605). "Eye contact detection in humans from birth." Newborns **2-5 days old** already show preference for direct gaze vs averted gaze. ERP evidence of frontal processing differences. This is the earliest documented PFC-related differential response in humans.

- **Lloyd-Fox et al. 2009, 2015** (*Child Development*, multiple). Series of fNIRS studies demonstrating infant PFC responses to biological motion, speech vs non-speech sounds, and social agent stimuli across 4-12 months.

**Interpretation for F1**:
- PFC is not merely "present" — it is **actively processing** relevant stimuli from the first postnatal days.
- Rule learning at 8 months (Werchan) is particularly striking: it demonstrates that infant PFC participates in **abstraction over rules**, not just simple stimulus-response. Amplitude-performance correlation strengthens this.
- The response profile (lower amplitude, longer latency than adult) is consistent with **chunks still compiling** and **myelination still incomplete**, not with "PFC offline."

**Why this falsifies old view**:
- Under the old view, infant PFC should show no or minimal hemodynamic response to cognitive stimuli. It shows robust, differentiated, cognitively-meaningful responses.
- Werchan's amplitude-predicts-performance finding in particular is hard to reconcile with "PFC offline" — if PFC weren't doing the work, why would its amplitude correlate with behavioral outcome?

🟢 Confidence: STRONG. Multiple independent labs, multiple paradigms, converging results. fNIRS has its own limitations (spatial resolution, depth penetration) but the directional finding (infant PFC active) is robust across methods.

### §3.3 — Pillar 3: Resting-state networks are present pre-birth and adult-like by term

🟢 **Claim**: Large-scale brain networks — including frontoparietal executive control network and default mode network — are already present and organized in a "full adult-like facsimile" by term birth. Infant network architecture is a **starting point**, not a goal state to build toward.

**Primary citations**:

- **Doria et al. 2010** (*PNAS* 107:20015-20020). "Emergence of resting state networks in the preterm human brain." Resting-state fMRI on **70 preterm infants** at 29-43 weeks postmenstrual age. Key findings:
  - Frontoparietal executive control network, salience network, and default mode network all identifiable in preterm scans
  - By **term birth (40 weeks)**, these networks are "full adult-like facsimiles" in architecture (though weaker functional connectivity)
  - Network architecture **precedes** behavioral demonstration of network-dependent cognition — scaffolding ready before chunks to drive it

- **Gao et al. 2009** (*PNAS* 106:6790-6795). "Evidence on the emergence of the brain's default network from 2-week-olds." Primitive default mode network (DMN) — including medial PFC and posterior cingulate — detectable in 2-week-old infants. Adult-like DMN by age 2.

- **Fransson et al. 2007** (*PNAS* 104:15531-15536). "Resting-state networks in the infant brain." Demonstrated five different resting-state networks in sleeping infants, including sensorimotor, visual, auditory, DMN-precursor, and bilateral frontal networks.

**Interpretation for F1**:
- Network architecture = the **wiring diagram** between regions. It is present and organized before the **functional content** that will flow through it.
- Analogy: A city's road network can be complete before any particular destination is mapped. The roads exist, the grid is laid out, but you need specific routes (chunks) to get anywhere useful.
- The DMN in particular is interesting — it is the "resting" network, often associated with self-referential and simulation processes in adults. Its presence from 2 weeks is consistent with substrate being ready for self-pattern compilation (link to Agent/Self-Pattern-Modeling §5 developmental bootstrap).

**Why this falsifies old view**:
- The old view implicitly predicted networks should **build up** through infancy. They don't — they're mostly there at birth.
- What develops is **functional connectivity strength** (how efficiently signal propagates through the already-present network), not architectural presence.

🟢 Confidence: STRONG. Multiple independent labs with different MRI paradigms. Finding has been replicated and extended through 2015-2025 with larger samples and higher-resolution imaging.

### §3.4 — Pillar 4: Myelination is the slow thing — not presence, just speed

🟡 **Claim**: What genuinely develops slowly in PFC is **myelination of intracortical fibers**, which affects signal conduction speed and signal-to-noise ratio — not the presence or operational status of PFC itself.

**Primary citation**:
- **Yakovlev & Lecours 1967**. "The myelogenetic cycles of regional maturation of the brain." Seminal myelination timeline study. PFC intracortical association fibers myelinate **last** among cortical regions, with the process continuing through the third decade of life (peaking around age 25-30 and continuing even after).

**Supporting evidence**:
- Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) studies (Miller et al. 2012, Tamnes et al. review) show PFC white matter fractional anisotropy continuing to increase through adolescence and early adulthood, consistent with ongoing myelination.

**Interpretation for F1**:
- Myelination increases **signal conduction speed** (~5x faster than unmyelinated fibers for similar thickness) and **reduces cross-talk** (signal stays in its intended fiber).
- Consequence of low myelination: higher latencies (consistent with Kouider 2013 finding that 5mo consciousness signature is ~900ms vs adult 300ms — roughly 3x latency) and lower signal-to-noise ratio.
- Low myelination does **not** mean "offline." It means "slower and noisier but operational."
- The old view's interpretive error: conflated "slow to fully myelinate" with "late to develop functionally."

**Analogy**: A cellular network with 2G vs 5G infrastructure. 2G can still route calls, just slower and with lower bandwidth. It doesn't mean the towers aren't there or the handsets don't work — it means throughput is limited. Infant PFC is analogously "lower-bandwidth operational" rather than "offline."

**Why this partially supports old view**:
- Myelination IS slow. The old view wasn't wrong that PFC "takes time to develop" — it was wrong about **which dimension** takes time. Myelination (speed) is slow; hardware presence (neurons, synapses, networks) is not.
- Reframe preserves the Yakovlev & Lecours finding while correcting interpretation.

🟡 Confidence: STRONG for myelination timeline itself. 🟡 for the interpretive claim that "slow myelination = operational but lower-quality" — this is framework inference, not directly measured.

### §3.5 — Pillar 5: Frontal consciousness signature present from 5 months

🟢 **Claim**: The same nonlinear frontal access-consciousness signature (P3b-like ERP component) observed in adults is **present at 5 months**, demonstrating that the frontal mechanism for access-consciousness is already operational in early infancy.

**Primary citation**:
- **Kouider, Dehaene et al. 2013** (*Science* 340:376-380). "A neural marker of perceptual consciousness in infants." ERP study of 5-month-old, 12-month-old, and 15-month-old infants using masked visual stimuli. Key findings:
  - 5-month-olds show the same nonlinear late ERP component as adults, **particularly prominent above frontal lobe** (P3b-like)
  - Latency: 5mo ~900ms, 12-15mo ~750ms, adult ~300ms
  - Shape of nonlinearity (sudden threshold transition for conscious access) is similar across ages
  - Conclusion: **"frontal-dependent consciousness mechanism already functional"** from 5 months

**Interpretation for F1**:
- Access-consciousness — the mechanism by which information becomes globally available for reasoning, report, behavioral control — is **not absent** in infants. It is **present and operational** from at least 5 months.
- What changes with age is **speed** (latency decreases) and **amplitude** (signal strength increases), consistent with:
  - Myelination improving conduction speed (pillar 4)
  - Chunks compiling, making relevant content more retrievable (main F1 mechanism)
  - Integration efficiency improving
- The mechanism itself — the frontal access-consciousness architecture — does not switch on at some age. It is there from early infancy.

**Why this falsifies old view**:
- The old view would predict no frontal signature until PFC "comes online" — some age threshold. No such threshold is observed.
- 5 months is notably earlier than most old-view predictions of "PFC turning on."

🟢 Confidence: STRONG. Kouider 2013 is high-profile, well-powered, widely cited. Methodology (ERP with masked stimuli, nonlinear fitting) is rigorous. Finding has been discussed and extended in subsequent consciousness literature.

## §4 — Hodel 2018: The Anchor Reframe Paper

### §4.1 — Why Hodel 2018 is the anchor citation

Among all the research supporting the reframe, **Hodel 2018** ("Rapid Infant Prefrontal Cortex Development and Sensitivity to Early Environmental Experience", *Developmental Review* 48:113-144) is the **explicit reframe paper**. Earlier studies provided component evidence (pillars 1-5 above), but Hodel 2018 is the first comprehensive paper that:

1. **Names** the old view explicitly
2. **Identifies** it as an "unfortunate cost" of "late-developing" framing
3. **Reframes** PFC as a "rapidly developing region"
4. **Ties** the reframe to developmental implications (environmental sensitivity windows)

F1 drill cites Hodel 2018 as **anchor** for this reason. It is the point-of-reference reframe paper.

### §4.2 — Direct quote preservation

🟢 **Hodel 2018 verbatim quote** (abstract, emphasis in original not preserved):

> "Widespread recognition of the extended development of prefrontal cortex in human children led to the perception of prefrontal cortex as 'late developing,' resulting in an **unfortunate cost**: a perception of it as **structurally and functionally underdeveloped in young infants and toddlers**. Contrary to this perception, this review presents evidence that prefrontal cortex is rapidly developing during early infancy."

**Additional verbatim characterization** from paper body: Medial prefrontal regions are described as "**among the thickest cortex at birth**."

### §4.3 — Hodel's main argument (paraphrased)

Hodel 2018 structures the reframe argument as follows:

1. **Historical framing problem**: "Late-developing" became shorthand that elided multi-dimensional PFC maturation (which dimensions are slow, which are fast)
2. **Evidence review**: Cortical thickness, functional imaging, behavioral experiments, cognitive measures — all reviewed
3. **Key finding**: Many PFC properties are **rapidly developing** during infancy — cortical thickness (mPFC among thickest at birth), functional connectivity, behavioral manifestations
4. **Environmental sensitivity**: Rapid development creates **vulnerability windows** — PFC is highly sensitive to early environmental experience (stress, attachment, caregiver responsiveness) precisely because it is in rapid development, not despite "being offline"
5. **Practical implication**: Intervention windows matter. Early experience shapes PFC in ways that late intervention cannot fully compensate.

### §4.4 — Why this matters for F1 framing

Hodel's **environmental sensitivity** point directly supports F1 drill's **body-state-at-compile mechanism** for Convergence Point 7 (refined N+4c2-REV1, formerly framed as "cortisol tagging"). If PFC is in rapid development during infancy, then the **body state fingerprint** (novelty vs threat direction + hormonal profile + mechanism activation) during chunk compile becomes developmentally consequential in two ways:

1. **Chunk association damage** — chunks compiled under a threat-dominant body state are tagged with cortisol-threat association; chunks compiled under a novelty-dominant body state are tagged with opioid-approach association. Cortisol level alone does not determine direction (per [06a §7.0.1](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0.1) novelty vs threat cortisol distinction).

2. **Neural wear on developing PFC** — chronic threat cortisol produces Stage 2 accumulated wear on PFC synapses (🟢 Arnsten 2009, McEwen 2007) compounded by PFC↓ Amygdala↑ vicious cycle (🟢 Vyas 2002, Shin 2006). This is **structural damage** to the substrate that would later update the damaged chunks. This is the mechanism behind 🟢 ACE (Adverse Childhood Experiences, Felitti 1998) dose-response findings. ## §5 — Hardware vs Content Architectural Distinction

### §5.1 — The component breakdown

🟡 **Framework claim**: "PFC maturation" is not a single monolithic process. It decomposes into at least 5 distinct components, each with a different developmental timeline and different relationship to infant cognitive capacity.

| Component | Status at newborn | Develops through | F1 relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neurons + synapses** | ✅ Present (over-supplied per Huttenlocher) | Synaptogenesis to ~15mo peak; pruning through adolescence | Substrate ready; selection via experience |
| **Large-scale networks** | ✅ Adult-like facsimile by term (Doria 2010) | Functional connectivity strengthens through first 2 years | Wiring diagram complete; traffic improving |
| **Functional responsiveness** | ✅ Present from 2-5 days (Farroni 2002), 4mo (Grossmann 2013), 8mo (Werchan 2016) | Amplitude increases, latency decreases | PFC participates in cognition from first days |
| **Myelination** | 🟡 Immature (Yakovlev & Lecours 1967) | Continues through third decade | Signal speed + SNR improving slowly |
| **Long-range integration efficiency** | 🟡 Low | Improves with myelination + chunks | Cross-region coordination gradually better |
| **Compiled chunks (content)** | 🔴 Minimal — **F1 target** | Experience-dependent accumulation — Precondition-2 bottleneck (Body-Feedback-Precondition) | **This is the actual F1 drill subject** |
| **Working memory capacity** | 🔴 Limited | Chunks accumulation + integration efficiency | Downstream of chunks |

### §5.2 — What old view got wrong

The old "PFC offline" view implicitly treated all components as collapsing into a single "PFC readiness" dimension that went from 0 to 100% through age. The decomposition above shows this is at least 7 distinct dimensions, each with its own timeline and mechanism.

**Specific errors of conflation**:

- Conflation 1: "Slow myelination" → "PFC not developed" (ignores hardware present)
- Conflation 2: "Behavioral failure at adult tasks" → "PFC offline" (ignores that missing chunks could explain failure even with PFC online)
- Conflation 3: "Behavioral success on simple tasks" → "subcortical or core knowledge modules" (ignores PFC may be involved)
- Conflation 4: "PFC ~10%" → "PFC hardware 10%" (should be "compiled content + WM capacity ~10%")

### §5.3 — The correct framing

✅ **PFC status at newborn = "online + active + low signal-to-noise + missing compiled content + missing myelin speed"**

All five qualifiers matter. Drop any one and the framing becomes misleading:
- Drop "online" → false "offline" claim
- Drop "active" → false "silent" claim
- Drop "low SNR" → false "fully mature operation" claim
- Drop "missing content" → false "just needs to grow up" claim (misses the actual mechanism)
- Drop "missing speed" → false "adult-like immediately" claim

F1 drill uses this five-qualifier framing throughout. Any shorthand that drops qualifiers is considered problematic.

### §5.4 — Why content (chunks) is the F1 drill target

Given this decomposition, F1 drill targets specifically the **"compiled chunks (content)"** row of the table. Why?

1. **It is the actually-missing component** — hardware is present, networks present, PFC active; what's missing is the specific compiled content that enables adult tasks
2. **It is the mechanism that explains infant failure** — infant fails a verbal task not because PFC is offline but because verbal chunks haven't compiled yet
3. **It is the mechanism that explains developmental progression** — chunks accumulate through experience, not through PFC hardware maturation
4. **It is observable through events** — we can infer chunks state from observable behavior via event-inference methodology (§6)
5. **It is the Body-Feedback-Precondition Precondition-2 bottleneck keystone** — Body-Feedback-Draft N+3 identified chunks base adequacy as the developmental keystone; F1 provides the accumulation mechanism

Other components (myelination, pruning, integration efficiency) continue in parallel but are **not F1 drill targets**. They provide background tempo but are not the mechanism F1 formalizes.

## §6 — Implication for Event-Inference Methodology

### §6.1 — The methodological problem

🟡 **Given that PFC hardware is online but we cannot directly measure PFC function in infants**, how do we make developmental claims about chunk-based cognition?

**The problem**:
- Adult PFC function is measured via fMRI, EEG, behavioral task performance, self-report, lesion studies
- Infant PFC function has **none of these available at high quality**:
  - fMRI requires sedation or rare quiet sleep scans
  - EEG is possible but has limited spatial specificity (Kouider 2013 is an exception, but used masked stimuli for consciousness-specific measurement)
  - Behavioral tasks require instruction (not available pre-verbal)
  - Self-report impossible pre-verbal
  - Lesion studies are rare and confounded by plasticity
- fNIRS is the main tool but has limited spatial resolution + depth penetration

**Conclusion**: We cannot **directly claim** "PFC function X at age Y" with any precision beyond group-level averaged imaging findings.

### §6.2 — The methodological solution: event-inference

🟡 **Framework commitment**: F1 drill does not make direct PFC function claims. Instead, it makes **event-inference claims**:

> "Event X is observable at age Y in baby population. Event X plausibly requires chunks A, B, C to have compiled. Therefore, by age Y, chunks A, B, C must have accumulated in some baby population members."

This is **weaker** than "PFC matures at age X" but **scientifically stronger** because:

1. **Observable events are empirically grounded** — can be replicated, debated, falsified
2. **Chunks requirement is framework-level inference** — explicit, criticizable, updatable
3. **Age claims are about events, not about PFC state** — avoids overreach

### §6.3 — The rule for F1 drill

**Every F1 claim about infant cognition must follow this template**:

```
[Observable event, with age range and research citation]
    ↓
[Chunks plausibly required for event to occur]
    ↓
[PFC-Inference Ladder level classification (L0-L4)]
    ↓
[Plausibility qualifier 🟢🟡🔴]
```

**Not allowed** (would be overclaim):
- "PFC activates at age X for task Y"
- "PFC matures at age X"
- "PFC develops task Y capability at age X"

**Allowed** (event-inference):
- "Event Y observable at age X (citation); this plausibly requires chunks A, B, C to have compiled; this places the event at ladder level L2 🟡"
- "Research shows event Z at age W (citation); chunks required: A, B, C, D; ladder level L3 🟡; framework inference, not direct PFC measurement"

### §6.4 — Plausibility qualifiers

Every claim in F1 drill carries a plausibility qualifier:

- 🟢 **High confidence**: Empirical research support, replicated studies, direct measurement (observable events almost always 🟢 or 🟢🟡)
- 🟡 **Medium confidence**: Framework inference, plausible mechanism, partial empirical support, falsifiable but not yet tested (chunks-requirement inference is typically 🟡; ladder classification typically 🟡)
- 🔴 **Low confidence**: Speculation, pending further research, contested (rare in F1 drill body, used for open questions)

### §6.5 — PFC-Inference Ladder as the event classifier

Plan §0.7 defines the 5-level PFC-Inference Ladder. This file (01) provides the methodological justification; the plan defines the levels. Brief recap:

- **L0** Reflex-only — innate, no chunks needed
- **L1** Post-hoc response — raw chunks compiling, response after stimulus
- **L2** ⭐ Pattern-match activation — chunks fire on antecedent, no action ("face-freeze")
- **L3** Crude response — partial action chunks
- **L4** Coordinated execution — full chunks stack

**The ladder is an event classifier**, not a developmental stage timeline. A 9-month-old can have events at L0 (reflexes still present), L1 (post-hoc cry after hunger), L2 (stranger anxiety face progression), L3 (crude pointing), L4 (joint attention) simultaneously. Classification depends on the specific event, not the baby's age.

### §6.6 — Same event climbs ladder over time

As chunks accumulate through experience, **the same observable event type** can move up the ladder. The bladder case (which will be drilled in detail in 06a) illustrates:

| Age | Event | Ladder | Chunks state |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-3mo (E3) | Cry after wet diaper | L1 | Raw discomfort chunk, not predictive |
| 12-18mo | "Face-freeze" before voiding | L2 | Predictive chunk fires; no action chunks yet |
| 14-20mo | Squirm + whimper before voiding | L3 | Partial action chunks |
| 22mo (E23) | "Need to pee" verbal + call caregiver | L4 | Full coordinated stack |

**Same body system, same sensor, same PFC hardware** — only chunks state differs across ages. This is F1 drill's central claim in action.

## §7 — Three Concrete Examples — Reframe in Action

> This section operationalizes the reframe by working through the user's three examples from §0.5 Q4 of plan.md:
>
> 1. "Cannot feel the urge to urinate"
> 2. "Cannot recognize when mother calls their name"
> 3. "Cannot communicate normally with adults"
>
> Each example shows: (a) the old "PFC offline" interpretation, (b) the reframed "chunks missing" interpretation, (c) empirical evidence supporting the reframe, (d) F1 drill home for deeper treatment.

### §7.1 — Example 1: "Cannot feel the urge to urinate"

**User's framing**: An infant does not verbally request the bathroom before wetting. Does this mean PFC is offline, or that PFC is present but lacks specific chunks?

**Old interpretation (rejected)**:
- "PFC offline → cannot feel bladder → wet diaper"
- Prediction: as PFC "turns on," child starts verbalizing

**Reframed interpretation**:
- Body component: Bladder mechanoreceptors fire from prenatal (fetus urinates into amniotic fluid from ~10 weeks gestation, proving motor pathway operational)
- Spinal afferent (S2-S4 parasympathetic + sympathetic) intact from birth
- Central representation (insula + ACC + pontine micturition center) present from birth
- PFC hardware: online per pillars 1-5 above
- **What's missing**: Chunks compiling the association "bladder stretch signal → predict urination event → label → plan action"
- This chunk cannot form without **repeated experience** of: bladder stretch → void → wet → discomfort sequence, captured by PFC observing the pattern

**Developmental gradient** (preview of 06a drill):

| Stage | Age | Event | Ladder | Why reframe makes sense |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | 0-6mo | Autonomic reflexive voiding | L0 | Brainstem pontine micturition center handles; PFC observing but no chunks to act |
| Repetition compile phase | 6-12mo | Crying after wet diaper | L1 | Post-hoc chunk fire; chunks accumulating but not predictive yet |
| B | 12-18mo | "Face-freeze" before voiding ⭐ | **L2** | **Chunks fire on antecedent signal; no action chunks yet — user's original insight** |
| C | 14-20mo | Squirm + whimper before voiding | L3 | Partial action chunks emerge |
| D | 20-24mo (E23) | "Need to pee" + call caregiver | L4 | Full coordinated verbal + motor + social chunks |

**Key falsification point for old view**: Stage B "face-freeze" is **impossible** under the "PFC offline" framing. If PFC were offline, no pattern-match would fire → no freeze observable. The fact that babies visibly freeze facially (🟡 user observation + consistent with infant interoceptive emergence literature) shows PFC is online, firing pattern-match chunk, with no action counterpart. This single observable phenomenon falsifies the old view directly.

**F1 drill home**: [06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md) — bladder case is the cleanest developmental arc in F1 drill because it spans L0→L4 within a single body system. Full exposition in §3-§5 of that file.

**Empirical anchor**: Feel-Example-Draft E1 (newborn undifferentiated cry, 2 weeks), E3 (cry after wet diaper, newborn), E23 "need to pee" verbal (22 months). The 22-month gap is the Precondition-2 Chunk-Substrate accumulation timeline — not a PFC maturation timeline.

### §7.2 — Example 2: "Cannot recognize when mother calls their name"

**User's framing**: A very young infant does not turn their head when mother calls their name. Does this mean PFC is offline, or that chunks for name recognition haven't compiled yet?

**Old interpretation (rejected)**:
- "PFC offline → cannot process speech → no response to name"
- Prediction: name response emerges when PFC "turns on" — perhaps age 2-3

**Reframed interpretation**:
- Body component: Fetal hearing from 26 weeks gestation. Auditory cortex + Heschl's gyrus mature early relative to other cortex.
- Prenatal auditory learning: DeCasper & Spence 1986 shows newborns **prefer stories their mother read during pregnancy** (proving prenatal auditory chunks compile). Mehler 1988 shows native language rhythm preference at birth.
- PFC hardware: online from prenatal per pillars 1-5
- **What's missing**: Chunks binding "this specific acoustic pattern → refers to self → social response expected"

**Empirical falsification of "PFC offline until age 2" prediction**:

🟢 **Mandel, Jusczyk & Pisoni 1995** (*Psychological Science* 6:314-317). "Infants' recognition of the sound patterns of their own names." Key finding: **4.5-month-old infants reliably recognize their own name** and show preferential attention to it over other names. This is **dramatically earlier** than the old view would predict.

**What this means under reframe**:
- PFC was never offline — it has been processing auditory input from the fetal period
- Chunks for name recognition compile around 4-5 months through repeated exposure (parents saying baby's name hundreds of times per week)
- Chunks for "name → turn head toward source" compile somewhat later (requires cross-modal binding of auditory chunk + motor response chunk)
- "Doesn't respond to name" in a 3-month-old = chunks not yet compiled, not PFC offline

**Developmental gradient**:

| Age | Event | Ladder | Chunks state |
|---|---|---|---|
| Birth | Prefers mother's voice | L2 | Prenatal auditory chunks from ~26wk compiled |
| Birth | Prefers native language rhythm | L2 | Prenatal phoneme statistics chunks |
| ~4.5mo | Recognizes own name (Mandel 1995) | L2 | Name acoustic chunk compiled; no motor response required for recognition |
| 6-9mo | Turns head to name | L3 | Name chunk + head-turn motor chunk bound |
| 9-12mo | Responds to name with attention + eye contact | L4 | Full social response stack |

**F1 drill home**: [04-Auditory-System-Arc.md](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md) §3 (auditory chunks formation) + §4 (auditory → motor binding).

**Empirical anchor**: Mandel et al. 1995 (name at 4.5mo), DeCasper & Spence 1986 (prenatal auditory learning), Mehler 1988 (native language rhythm at birth).

### §7.3 — Example 3: "Cannot communicate normally with adults"

**User's framing**: A toddler does not hold adult-style conversations. Does this mean PFC is offline, or that specific communication chunks haven't compiled yet?

**Old interpretation (rejected)**:
- "PFC offline → cannot produce speech → no communication until PFC matures"
- Prediction: communication emerges when PFC "turns on" around age 2-3

**Reframed interpretation**:
- Body component: Broca's area (motor speech planning) present from birth. Wernicke's area (receptive language) present from birth. Mouth motor pathways present from birth (newborns can cry, feed, coo).
- PFC social regions (mPFC) active from 4 months (Grossmann 2013)
- **What's missing**: Multiple chunk categories layered asymmetrically — see H11 formalization below

**H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry preview** (full formalization in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §6):

Comprehension chunks form 6-12 months **before** production chunks for the same linguistic content. Mechanism hypothesis: production requires more chunk categories than comprehension.

**Comprehension chunks for "hungry" [infant's first word example]**:
1. Auditory pattern chunk (acoustic binding)
2. Referent chunk (hunger body state)
3. Meaning binding chunk (auditory pattern + referent)
= 3 chunk categories

**Production chunks for "hungry"**:
1-3. All comprehension chunks (prerequisite)
4. Mouth motor articulation chunks (lip + tongue + jaw coordination)
5. Social feedback loop chunks (saying "hungry" → caregiver response → reward)
6. Verbal request framing chunks ("I state this word to obtain response")
= 6+ chunk categories

**Production requires roughly 2x chunks → roughly 2x accumulation time → ~12 month gap.**

**Empirical gap**:
- Name recognition 4.5mo (Mandel 1995) — receptive
- First verbal labels 18mo (Feel-Example E21) — productive
- Gap: **13.5 months**

**Gestural vs verbal evidence (strengthens H11)**:
- Gesture for hunger via pointing: ~14mo (Feel-Example E25 implicit in E21)
- Verbal label for hunger: ~18mo (Feel-Example E21)
- Gesture-verbal gap: **~4 months**

The gestural-verbal gap being **smaller** than the receptive-productive gap is consistent with H11 mechanism: gesture requires fewer chunk categories than verbal (no articulation chunks needed, just visual-motor chunks), so emerges earlier.

**What "cannot communicate normally" means under reframe**:
- Not "PFC offline" — PFC has been processing communication partners from 4mo (Grossmann mPFC)
- Not "nothing happening" — receptive comprehension accumulating throughout
- **Chunks compiling asymmetrically** — receptive first, productive later, with ~12 month gap built into mechanism

**Falsifiable prediction** (from H11, detailed in 08 §6):
- In babies with normal cognitive development but motor delay, the comprehension-production gap should be **longer** (motor articulation chunks delayed disproportionately)
- In deaf babies exposed to sign language, sign comprehension should precede sign production by a similar gap (mechanism is general, not specific to verbal)
- Cross-linguistic: gap should be ~consistent regardless of native language complexity (mechanism is chunks accumulation, not language-specific)

**F1 drill home**: [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §6 (H11 full formalization) + [06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md) §8 (bladder case as H11 anchor — same asymmetric chunks accumulation mechanism).

**Empirical anchor**: Feel-Example E21 (first verbal hunger label, 18mo), E22 (first verbal pain label, 20mo), E23 (first verbal urge-to-urinate label, 22mo), Mandel 1995 (name 4.5mo), gesture-before-verbal literature (Goodwyn, Acredolo & Brown 2000 baby sign).

### §7.4 — What these three examples establish together

Taken together, the three examples demonstrate:

1. **Infant "cannot do X" → never caused by "PFC offline."** In all three cases, PFC is online, participating. The missing component is specific chunks for the specific task.

2. **Developmental timeline is chunks timeline, not PFC timeline.** The age at which event Y becomes observable reflects how long it takes specific chunks to compile through specific experience, not how long it takes PFC to "come online."

3. **Same hardware handles L0 through L4 events simultaneously.** A 10-month-old has L0 reflexes intact, L1 post-hoc responses in some domains, L2 pattern-match activation in others (stranger anxiety), L3 crude responses, L4 coordinated execution for rehearsed activities (peek-a-boo). This is only possible if PFC hardware is handling all of them — an offline PFC could do none.

4. **Event-inference methodology is the defensible framing.** "Event X observable at age Y requires chunks A, B, C compiled" is empirically grounded, falsifiable, updatable. "PFC matures at age X" is unmeasurable and misleading.

These three examples are **the empirical core** of F1 drill's answer to the user's original Q4 question. Subsequent arc files deepen the mechanism; this file establishes the foundation.

### §10.3 — Counter-arguments + responses

**Counter-argument 1**: "fNIRS has limited depth penetration and cannot reach deep PFC structures. Maybe infant PFC activation at deep structures is absent."

**Response**: fNIRS covers dorsolateral and ventrolateral PFC effectively (depth ~1.5-3cm). These are the PFC regions most relevant for chunk-based cognition. Deep structures (anterior cingulate, medial deep regions) are covered by resting-state fMRI (Doria 2010, Gao 2009) which confirms network presence. Together, fNIRS + fMRI + histology + ERP cover enough to establish the reframe; specific deep-structure function is an open research question but does not change the reframe.

**Counter-argument 2**: "Synaptogenesis and network presence don't prove functional cognitive activity. Maybe everything is there but not doing anything useful."

**Response**: Werchan 2016 addresses this directly. Amplitude of dlPFC activation **predicts** learning performance in 8-month-olds. This is correlational, not causal, but shows meaningful cognitive-neural coupling. Combined with Kouider 2013 (same consciousness mechanism at 5mo) and Grossmann 2013 (differential responses to socially meaningful stimuli), the evidence goes beyond "hardware present" to "hardware functionally engaged with cognitive content."

**Counter-argument 3**: "Maybe infants have 'proto-PFC' that differs qualitatively from adult PFC, and adult-level PFC only emerges later."

**Response**: The "proto-PFC" framing is compatible with the reframe. We can call infant PFC "functional but lower-bandwidth" (i.e., "proto" in the sense of being lower capacity) without calling it "offline." The key correction is not about whether infant PFC equals adult PFC (it doesn't) but about whether infant PFC is **participating in cognition** (it is, per evidence). The framework reframe preserves "different from adult" while rejecting "offline."

**Counter-argument 4**: "Event-inference methodology is just dressed-up behaviorism — claiming we only care about observable events."

**Response**: Event-inference is explicitly **not** behaviorism. Behaviorism rejects inner representations; event-inference accepts inner representations (chunks) but claims we must infer them from observable events rather than measure them directly. The framework asks "what chunks must have compiled for this event to be observable" — chunks are real, just measurement-inaccessible in infants. This is closer to cognitive science with methodological humility than behaviorism.

### §10.4 — What would falsify this file's claims

🟡 **Falsification conditions**:

- Pillar 1 (synaptogenesis): Histology study showing newborn PFC synaptic density significantly below adult (no such study exists; Huttenlocher's finding is replicated)
- Pillar 2 (fNIRS): Study showing no PFC fNIRS response to cognitive stimuli in infants (large body of evidence shows opposite)
- Pillar 3 (networks): Study showing no large-scale network organization at term (Doria 2010 settled this)
- Pillar 5 (consciousness): Study showing frontal consciousness signature absent at 5mo (would require replication of Kouider 2013 with null finding)
- Event-inference methodology: Not directly falsifiable (it's a methodological commitment), but could be shown inadequate if chunks inferences routinely fail to predict future observations

All 5 pillars are **currently robust**. The reframe is **unlikely to be overturned** but could be refined as methodology improves (e.g., better infant-appropriate imaging).
