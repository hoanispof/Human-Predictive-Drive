## §0 — Abstract

🟢 **Claim**: The social system is inherently multi-modal. Every social event involves 2+ channels firing together (minimum: visual face + auditory voice; typically: + body warmth + interoceptive valence + emotional tag). Unlike other body-inputs which have meaningful unimodal events, **there is no unimodal social event**. This architectural property has a consequence: social chunks are **multi-modal from birth**, not compiled as single-modal first and then bound together.

🟡 **Convergence Point 3 Primary Verdict** (committed §6): Multi-modal binding at birth is a **multi-mechanism phenomenon**. The newborn has four concurrent mechanisms for binding cross-modal inputs into unified chunks:
1. **Temporal co-occurrence + Hebbian binding** (below-cortical synaptic + cortical LTP)
2. **Multisensory neurons** at superior colliculus + superior temporal sulcus (Meredith & Stein 1983)
3. **Default Mode Network early scaffolding** providing network-level substrate (Doria 2010)
4. **Amygdala emotional tag as binding cue** (affective valence at peak moments strengthens cross-modal associations)

All four mechanisms act concurrently. No single mechanism is sufficient or necessary alone. The social smile E12 at 6-8 weeks is the **keystone observable event** that requires all four to be operational in coordination.

🟡 **Framework contribution R-F1-3** (proposed): Cross-modal binding is not a single dedicated "binder" module. It is an **emergent property** of a multi-mechanism substrate operating on temporally-correlated afferent streams. The framework should commit to multi-mechanism binding rather than seeking a single "binder".

## §1 — Position within F1

### §1.1 — Social as inherently multi-modal body-input

Consider what counts as a "social event" from the baby's perspective:

- **Mother approaching**: visual (face + posture) + auditory (footsteps + voice) + olfactory (mother smell) + vestibular (if being held, movement) + thermal (body warmth) + interoceptive (anticipatory quieting or excitation)
- **Stranger approaching**: same channels but pattern-match against familiar library → no match → fear chunk activation
- **Cry contagion**: auditory (other cry sound) + interoceptive (own distress state activated)
- **Social smile**: visual (face) + auditory (voice) + body (calm state baseline) + emotional (positive valence)
- **Separation distress**: visual (absence of mother face) + memory (recent presence) + interoceptive (distress activation) + motor (seeking)

**There is no meaningful single-channel social event.** A visual-only face without voice is only partially social (and less engaging to babies per Farroni 2002 direct-gaze preference studies, which still co-involve implicit expectation of voice). An auditory-only voice without face is only partially social (and less effective at eliciting social smile). The **social experience is constitutively multi-channel** — remove a channel and it ceases to be a full social event.

**Implication for F1 methodology**: Social arc analysis cannot follow the single-channel "Connect → Read → Control" structure cleanly used for visual (03) or auditory (04) or bladder (06a). Social must be analyzed as **multi-channel coordination events** from the start.

### §1.2 — Why social is the Convergence Point 3 primary drill location

Convergence Point 3 asks: HOW does the newborn bind visual + auditory + somatic + emotional inputs into a single unified chunk when there is no verbal label and PFC coordination capacity is still compiling?

Social events are the **cleanest empirical handles** on this question because:

1. **Observable cross-channel behavior**: Social smile E12 requires visual face + auditory voice + calm body → unified smile output. The smile event can be tracked over time (when does it emerge, what conditions bootstrap it).
2. **Unimodal testing is possible**: Can babies social-smile at face-only? At voice-only? (Partial answers: less reliably — showing that the unified chunk needs the cross-modal input).
3. **Developmental timing is well-documented**: Social smile emerges at 6-8 weeks, reliably, across cultures.
4. **Blind infant evidence**: Blind infants also social-smile at ~8 weeks, using auditory + tactile cues. This confirms the chunk is **learned cross-channel binding**, not visually-hardwired.
5. **Resonance cases** ([Agent/By-Product-Gap-Resonance.md](../Agent/By-Product-Gap-Resonance.md)) are almost all social — cry contagion, smile contagion, mother recognition, empathy — giving a rich empirical base.

## §2 — Connect phase: Social hardware at t=0

### §2.1 — Hardware components

Per [01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md) §4 five pillars, the social-relevant cortical regions are hardware-online from prenatal:

**Face processing**:
- **Fusiform face area (FFA)** — visual face-specific region; functional from 2 months (Tzourio-Mazoyer et al. 2002) with domain-general activation pattern at birth refining to face-specific by 2-4 months
- **Superior temporal sulcus (STS)** — biological motion + gaze processing; multi-sensory convergence zone (see §2.3 below)

**Social-emotional**:
- **Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)** — social cognition region, **Grossmann 2013** showed activation at 4 months for eye contact vs averted gaze via fNIRS (cited in [01 §4 pillar 2](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md#§4))
- **Amygdala** — affective tagging, functional from birth, responsive to faces and emotional stimuli early
- **Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** — affective-motivational integration, active from early infancy

**Innate templates**:
- **CONSPEC** (Morton & Johnson 1991) — innate face-like-stimulus orientation, present from birth, drives face-following behavior in newborns
- **CONLERN** — learned face knowledge, compiles over first 2-3 months on top of CONSPEC

**Resonance substrate (per [Agent/By-Product-Gap-Resonance.md](../Agent/By-Product-Gap-Resonance.md) §2)**:
- Self-Pattern-Modeling mechanism requires own chunks + target cues → prediction
- Resonance emerges when 2+ agents' Self-Pattern-Modeling co-fire well
- Substrate is general-purpose cortical pattern-matching + chunks library, not a dedicated "mirror module"

### §2.2 — Multisensory neurons (cross-modal binding substrate)

**Superior colliculus multisensory neurons** (Meredith & Stein 1983, Stein & Meredith 1993): single neurons that respond to stimuli from multiple modalities (visual + auditory, visual + tactile, etc.). Temporal and spatial alignment of stimuli produces **superadditive** firing — the neuron fires more than the sum of unimodal responses.

**STS multisensory convergence** (Beauchamp 2005): superior temporal sulcus has heavy convergence from auditory + visual + somatosensory pathways. STS is active in adults for multi-modal social events (face + voice) and is believed to be one of the primary substrates for social multi-modal binding.

**Implication for newborn**: These multisensory substrates are **hardware-present from birth**. They provide a **below-cortical** mechanism for binding temporally-correlated inputs. A baby watching mother's face while hearing mother's voice has multisensory neurons firing above threshold due to the visual-auditory co-occurrence — this **pre-chunks the binding** at neural substrate level even before compiled cortical chunks exist.

Framework claim 🟡: Newborn multi-modal binding is **substrate-supported**, not chunks-dependent at the earliest stages. As experience compiles cortical chunks, the binding shifts from purely substrate-level to substrate + cortical chunks. The social smile E12 at 6-8 weeks represents the timing when **cortical chunks for specific face-voice-calm bindings** have compiled enough to drive behavioral output.

### §2.3 — Default Mode Network scaffolding

**Doria et al. 2010** (cited in [01 §4 pillar 3](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md#§4)): 70 preterm infants 29-43 weeks postmenstrual age. Frontoparietal + executive control + default mode network (DMN) **full adult-like facsimiles** at term birth. This means the **network architecture** for large-scale coordination including cross-modal integration is present from term birth, before any specific social chunks have compiled.

**Implication**: Binding mechanism has a network-level scaffolding component. The DMN in particular is implicated in adult cross-modal and social processing; its early presence provides architectural support for multi-modal social binding.

### §2.4 — Amygdala affective tagging

**Amygdala** activates to emotional stimuli from birth (neonatal amygdala responsiveness is well-documented). Amygdala projections to sensory cortices and PFC provide a **valence tagging mechanism** that strengthens associations when affective peaks occur.

**Implication for multi-modal binding**: When face + voice + body-warmth + positive-feeling fire together with **shared affective valence**, the amygdala adds a **global valence tag** to all co-firing elements. This tag strengthens the association not just pairwise but **network-wide** — all channels firing at the affective peak get bound together.

**Why this matters for E12**: Social smile typically emerges during a **peak-affect caregiving moment** (mother smiling, talking warmly, holding baby, eye contact). All channels fire concurrent with positive amygdala tag. The amygdala tag is the **fourth mechanism** (alongside temporal co-occurrence + multisensory neurons + DMN scaffolding) that makes E12 possible at 6-8 weeks rather than needing each pairwise association to compile separately over months.

### §2.5 — Baseline state at t=0 (social-specific)

Per [02 §9.1](02-Womb-to-Birth-Baseline.md#§9.1) baseline, with social-specific content:

### §3.2 — E9 Cry contagion (Resonance Stage 0, day 1)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E9](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e9) day 1-2):

> Hospital newborn ward. One baby starts crying. Within 30 seconds, several other babies in the room begin to cry as well — despite not being the same family and with no other physical cue.

**Ladder classification**: **L0/L1** — reflex-like contagion based on auditory pattern-match with own-cry seed chunk.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present** (compiled within minutes of birth): own-cry pattern chunk (baby has heard own cry several times in first hours)
- **Mechanism**: other-baby cry sound is **near-miss** to own-cry pattern → predictive coding generates prediction-delta → distress amplification → cry output
- **NOT a mirror neuron module**: mechanism is chunk-based, not hardware

**⭐ Framework-critical observation from Feel-Example E9 Notes**:

> "The mainstream explanation is 'newborn empathy' or 'innate mirror neurons'. Our framework REJECTS that explanation because: 1. The baby has had hours to days of experience with THEIR OWN crying — each time they cry, they hear their own cry. This is sufficient to form a seed chunk. 2. Another baby's cry is a NEAR-MISS to the own-cry pattern but not exact → creates near-miss dissonance in predictive coding → distress signal is amplified. 3. NO 'mirror module' hardware needed — only seed chunk + dissonance amplification from prediction-delta."

**E10 decisive evidence against hardware mirror**: E10 (own-cry recording) asymmetry — baby hears playback of own cry, distress response WEAKER than hearing other's cry. If hardware mirror were the mechanism, any cry sound (including own recording) should trigger the response. Reality: own = exact match = no prediction-delta = no amplification. Other = near-miss = prediction-delta = amplification. Dondi, Simion & Caltran 1999 confirmed the asymmetry with controlled newborn studies.

**Sagi & Hoffman 1976, Martin & Clark 1982** — foundational newborn cry contagion research; prevalence ~70-90% (not 100% — another data point against "hardware mandatory").

**Framework position**: E9 is **the earliest F1 evidence for Resonance as chunk-based simulation**. It appears on day 1 because the seed chunk (own-cry pattern) can compile within minutes, not months. It falsifies "mirror module waiting to activate" views and supports the chunks-based Resonance framework drilled in [Agent/By-Product-Gap-Resonance.md](../Agent/By-Product-Gap-Resonance.md) Session N+2.

### §3.3 — **⭐ E12 Social smile as Convergence Point 3 keystone (6-8 weeks)**

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E12](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e12) 7 weeks):

> 7-week-old baby looks at mother's face from ~30cm — the natural focal length for a newborn. Mother has a warm expression, speaking gently. Baby begins to smile — a genuine social smile, outward-directed, with engagement, distinctly different from the reflex smile during sleep that was already present from the womb.

**Ladder classification**: **L2 pattern-match activation** (early form) → **L3 crude** (reciprocal attempt) → rapidly **L4** (reliable reciprocal smile within 1-2 weeks of first emergence).

**Chunks required for E12** (the multi-modal binding):

1. **Visual face chunk**: CONSPEC + CONLERN has compiled weak face pattern chunks by 6-8 weeks
2. **Auditory voice chunk**: prenatal maternal voice chunk (from [02 §4](02-Womb-to-Birth-Baseline.md#§4)) + postnatal refinement
3. **Body-state chunk**: calm baseline state (not hungry, not wet, not cold, not overwhelmed)
4. **Emotional valence chunk**: positive affect tag from caregiver-infant interactive history
5. **Binding chunk (the key multi-modal unit)**: "face pattern X + voice pattern Y + calm body + positive valence → smile output"

**Why E12 is the keystone**: Social smile requires ALL of (1)-(5) to co-fire and produce a coordinated motor output (smile). If ANY single component is absent, the smile does not reliably occur:
- Overwhelmed baby (body dysregulated) → no smile despite face + voice
- Stranger face with no prior positive history → no smile despite face + voice + calm
- Silent face → weaker smile (voice is partially load-bearing)
- Face hidden (only voice) → weaker smile (face is partially load-bearing)

This **conjunction requirement** is what makes E12 a multi-modal binding event, not a single-channel event.

**Why 6-8 weeks and not earlier**: Per Feel-Example E12 Notes, "this chunk is NOT innate — it requires ~6-8 weeks of cortical maturation combined with face exposure". The 6-8 week timing reflects the compile time for the binding chunk on top of the unimodal chunks. This is parallel to 06a §3.2 bladder repetition compile phase — repeated multi-modal co-occurrence events strengthen the binding chunk over weeks.

**Blind infant evidence** (E12 Notes): "Evidence from congenitally blind infants: they also social-smile at approximately 8 weeks, but using auditory and tactile cues instead of visual. This confirms that the smile chunk is a learned multi-channel binding, not hardware face-driven."

**Implication**: The chunk is **flexibly multi-modal** — any sufficient combination of channels that constitutes "caregiver present + positive context" can drive the smile output. Visual is typical but not uniquely required. This **flexibility is diagnostic of chunks-based mechanism**: a hardware face-driven module would not adapt to missing visual channel.

**Framework-critical framing**: E12 is **the first observable cross-channel binding event in the F1 developmental trajectory**. Before E12, each channel is processing somewhat independently (visual face pattern in FFA/STS, auditory voice pattern in auditory cortex, body state in insula, valence in amygdala). At E12, the unified binding chunk has compiled enough to drive coordinated motor output.

**Implication for Convergence Point 3 (verdict commitment in §6)**: E12 at 6-8 weeks tells us that multi-modal binding **compiles in weeks, not months** — which is only possible if substrate-level mechanisms (multisensory neurons, DMN, amygdala tagging) are doing much of the binding work in parallel with chunks compile. The full verdict is formalized in §6.

- "Other gaze direction → target spatial location" chunk
- Self-other distinction weakly present (baby treats other's gaze as pointing to something distinct from own current gaze)
- NOT yet joint attention full form (baby doesn't check back at mother yet)

**Framework significance**: E14 is the **first step toward joint attention** (E18 at 9-12 months). It demonstrates that by 6 months, baby treats other's gaze as **informative about the world** (where to look), not just as a social signal. This is the developmental precursor to theory of mind.

Tomasello 1995 — foundational joint attention research. Gaze following is precursor. Autism literature shows gaze following is typically absent or delayed in autism spectrum, making it an early diagnostic marker.

### §3.7 — Stranger anxiety ⭐ L2 Marker #3 (7-9 months, E16)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E16](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e16) 8 months):

> 8-month baby is playing happily in mother's lap. A stranger (the neighbor woman) enters the room and approaches. Baby turns away, clings tightly to mother, and may start crying as the stranger moves closer.

**Ladder classification**: **L2** (initial pattern-match face-progression) → **L3** (withdraw + cling).

**⭐ L2 Marker characteristic**: Careful observation of the **first few seconds** of stranger anxiety reveals a **face progression** before the L3 withdrawal: neutral → curious → puzzled → distressed over 5-10 seconds. Each face state is a **distinct pattern-match event**:
- Neutral: incoming face not yet matched
- Curious: processing, not yet classified
- Puzzled: CONLERN library searched → no match → dissonance
- Distressed: dissonance + "unfamiliar = danger" chunk fires
- Withdraw: action chunks engage (L3 transition)

**This sequence is observable within ~5-10 seconds if one watches carefully.** It is L2 marker #3 in the catalog (after bladder face-freeze from 06a and vaccination pre-cry from 06b §3.3).

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- Familiar face library has compiled (mother, siblings, regular caregivers)
- Unfamiliar face = no match in library → dissonance chunk fires
- "Unfamiliar + proximity → danger" chunk activating (compiled via caregiver emotional signaling — specifically, the compile body state carried novelty-arousal with a mild defensive tag from observing caregiver's own vigilance. Per [06a §7.0](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0) framework refinement: this is not "cortisol tagging" as a unified mechanism — it is chunk compile under a specific body state fingerprint where amygdala activation from novelty-uncertainty + social-referencing of caregiver's vigilance shapes the chunk's defensive association)
- Approach action chunks inhibited; withdraw action chunks activated

**Sroufe 1977 research base**. Prevalence ~70-90% in typical development; wide individual variation (some babies show minimal, some show strong).

**Framework-critical observation from E16 Notes** (depth-creates-blindspot insight):

> "This is an important framework lesson: depth on one axis can create a blindspot on another axis. Securely-attached babies tend to have stronger stranger anxiety (counterintuitive but correct) — because their familiar library is more distinct → mismatch with strangers is more distinct → fear is stronger."

**Chunks are not "more = better linearly"**: chunks create categories, categories create boundaries, boundaries create both acceptance AND rejection. This insight applies throughout F1 and beyond — it is a **dual-edge property** of chunks that will be relevant for later files (emotional regulation, social skills, cross-cultural adaptation).

### §3.8 — E17 Separation distress (9 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E17](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e17) 9 months):

> 9-month baby. Mother sets baby down and goes to another room. Baby cries intensely immediately, may crawl after mother, cling to mother's leg.

**Ladder classification**: **L3-L4** (distress + motor seeking + possibly proto-verbal "mama").

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- "Mother present" chunk bound to calm baseline
- "Mother absent" chunk bound to distress
- **Object permanence emerging** (Piaget tradition, refined by Baillargeon gradient per [03 §3.8](03-Visual-System-Arc.md#§3.8))
- Motor seeking chunks (crawl, grab)
- Primitive future-oriented chunk ("mom will come back if I call/seek")

**Framework-critical observation from E17 Notes**:

> "This is the first instance of a future-oriented chunk ('mom will come back if I call/seek') — a primitive form of prediction and agency. The baby already has a model of the world ('if I do X, the world responds Y') very early, not as a capability that emerges late in preschool. This is a framework point: predictive chunks appear much earlier than verbal chunks."

This is another support for H11 receptive-productive asymmetry — **predictive chunks compile receptively early** (9 months) while verbal labels for prediction compile productively much later. The world-model exists before the word.

**Attachment quality connection**: Ainsworth Strange Situation paradigm tests separation distress + reunion patterns as indicators of attachment style. Secure (Type B) produces moderate distress + comfort-seeking on reunion. Insecure-avoidant (Type A) shows muted distress. Insecure-ambivalent (Type C) shows exaggerated distress + difficulty soothing. **Disorganized (Type D)** shows contradictory patterns. [06b §5.8](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§5.8) covers the emotional compile mechanism per the refined framework — specifically the imposed-parent dual-source mechanism where parent is simultaneously connection source + threat source (per [06a §7.0.6](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0.6)). 07 notes the behavioral marker emerging here as a window into the compile architecture that already took shape in the 0-12mo emotional compile window.

### §3.9 — E19 Social referencing (9-12 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E19](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e19) 10 months):

> 10-month baby approaches an unfamiliar dog. Baby stops at a safe distance, turns to look at mother. If mother smiles and nods encouragingly, baby approaches further. If mother frowns with concern, baby backs away or turns to go.

**Ladder classification**: **L3-L4** — uncertainty chunk + check-other + use-response-as-info + action modification.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- "Uncertainty → check mother's face" chunk
- "Mother's face positive → safe" + "Mother's face negative → danger" chunks
- **Other-as-information-source chunk** — treating mother's emotional response as **valid information about the world**, not just as social feedback

**Framework significance**: E19 is the **first instance of Resonance being used for practical action decisions**, not just affective sharing. Per E19 Notes:

> "Resonance is not only a passive mechanism (sensing) but also active (acting on the output). Tight action coupling (Axis 5) appears much earlier than conventional thinking. A 10-month baby already has a 'social information → physical action' loop running."

**Sorce, Emde, Campos & Klinnert 1985 visual cliff experiment**: baby will NOT cross a perceived (but safe) visual cliff if mother shows worry; will cross if mother smiles. This is action-level validation of social-referencing chunks.

**Clinical implication**: In environments with **inconsistent or pathological caregiver emotional signals** (postpartum depression, abuse, neglect), the "mother's response = info about world" chunk compiles with unreliable data → adult outcome of difficulty trusting others' emotional signals, hypervigilance or dismissal.

### §3.10 — **⭐ E20 Still-face Phase 3 as L2 Marker #4 (6-9 months)**

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E20](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e20), 6-9 months, Tronick 1978 paradigm):

Baby is in reciprocal interaction with mother (smiling, cooing, "ah ah"). Mother suddenly holds a still face, no expression, no response, for ~2 minutes. Baby response sequence:
1. **Engagement escalation**: baby tries to restore interaction (smiles more, vocalizes louder, waves)
2. **Puzzled phase** ⭐ L2 marker: baby freezes for 1-3 seconds, expression shifts to confused, gaze shifts, brows may furrow
3. **Distress phase**: baby fusses, may start crying
4. **Withdrawal**: baby looks away, self-soothes

**⭐ L2 Marker is Phase 2 "puzzled freeze"**: This is an observable pattern-match event — baby's expectation chunk ("I smile → mom smiles back") has fired, mother's face pattern violates the expectation, prediction-delta is detected, PFC registers the violation, no action chunk is immediately available → brief freeze (parallel structure to bladder face-freeze from 06a §3.3).

**Why it's L2 not L3 at Phase 2**: Baby has registered expectation violation but is not yet actively attempting a fix (that's Phase 1 or Phase 3 escalation). The puzzled freeze is the **pattern-match recognition without action** — the defining L2 signature.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- "Expectation chunk for reciprocal interaction" compiled from weeks-months of contingent caregiver interaction
- Prediction-delta chunk fires on Phase 2 violation
- Freeze is the body-level trace of expectation-violation pattern-match without action

**Framework significance**: Still-face provides **controlled laboratory-grade L2 marker** evidence. Unlike naturalistic face-freeze (requires parent to observe during play) or stranger anxiety (requires stranger visitation), still-face can be produced on demand by any caregiver — making it the most **experimentally reproducible** L2 marker. Mesman et al. 2009 meta-analysis of 500+ still-face studies confirms the puzzled phase is reliable across infant populations.

**Clinical implication**: Postpartum depression creates **chronic still-face exposure** (depressed mother has muted facial expression). Babies of depressed mothers show developmentally adapted response patterns (faster withdrawal, reduced engagement attempts, muted affect). Field 1995; Tronick & Reck 2009 document these effects. The framework explanation: chronic expectation violation compiles **different default expectation chunks** with downstream emotional-regulation consequences.

### §3.11 — E18 Joint attention triadic (9-12 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E18](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e18) 10 months):

> 10-month baby is playing with a toy. Mother points at a dog outside the window and says "look over there, a dog!" Baby follows the direction of mother's hand, sees the dog, then looks BACK at mother, then looks at the dog again. This gaze sequence (object → mom → object) is the defining sign of joint attention.

**Ladder classification**: **L4** — coordinated multi-channel triadic chunk.

**Chunks required** (the triadic chunk requires prior chunks from multiple sources):

1. Point gesture chunk — understanding that tip of pointing hand is a vector, not the target itself
2. Gaze following (from E14 6mo)
3. Other-as-information-source (from E19 9-12mo)
4. Shared-experience chunk (new at E18) — "mom sees this, I see this, we both see this"
5. Triadic attention coordination — me + mom + object simultaneously held

**Framework significance**: E18 is **the foundation for language and theory of mind**. Per E18 Notes:

> "Tomasello argues joint attention IS THE FOUNDATION for language and all social cognition. Before joint attention, communication is only dyadic (me-you). After joint attention, there is triadic (me-you-it), opening the way for: Reference, Sharing experience, Shared intentionality, Eventually: language explosion at 18-24 months."

**Difference from gaze following E14**: Joint attention includes the baby **looking BACK at mother** to check shared experience. Gaze following is just "follow the vector"; joint attention is "we are both experiencing this thing together". The check-back is the critical addition — it requires treating mother as having **internal state about the object**, not just as a gaze source.

**Cross-reference**: [03 §3.8](03-Visual-System-Arc.md#§3.8) covered joint attention from the visual-side. 07 adds the multi-modal + social-cognitive layer. 08 will cover the language implications (naming + reference + vocabulary explosion).

### §3.12 — **⭐ E30 Peek-a-boo anticipation as L2 Marker #6 (10-14 months)**

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E30](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e30) 12-18 months):

> Mother covers face with hands, says "peek... boo!" and opens her hands. 12-18 month baby laughs delightedly at the "boo!" reveal. After a few rounds, baby actively covers their OWN face with their hands to initiate the game in reverse.

**⭐ L2 Marker is the anticipatory excitement BEFORE "boo!"**: During the "peek..." pause (mother's hand covering face), baby's face shows anticipatory expression — mouth slightly open, eyes wide, sometimes giggling before the reveal. This is an **L2 pattern-match event with positive valence** — baby has predictive chunk firing on temporal cue ("peek..." sound + covered face), chunk content is "reveal coming with smile", body-level manifestation is anticipatory excitement.

**Contrast with face-freeze (06a §3.3) and vaccination pre-cry (06b §3.3)**:

| L2 marker | Valence | Body-level trace |
|---|---|---|
| Bladder face-freeze (06a) | Neutral-to-mild-negative | Passive freeze |
| Vaccination pre-cry (06b) | Peak negative | Active distress cry |
| Peek-a-boo anticipation (07) | Peak positive | Active excitement + giggle |

All three are L2 (pattern-match activation without full action chunks yet) but the body-level manifestation varies with affective valence of the firing chunk. This is the framework refinement noted in [06b §3.3](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§3.3) — **L2 ladder level is valence-independent in structure but valence-dependent in body manifestation**.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- Object permanence chunk (mother's face still exists behind hand)
- Temporal prediction chunk (reveal coming at predictable time)
- Resolution + relief chunk (laughter at reveal)
- Reciprocal play chunk (turn-taking)

**Framework significance**: Bruner 1976 analyzed peek-a-boo as a universal early game across cultures. Per E30 Notes, it simultaneously teaches:
1. Object permanence
2. Temporal expectation
3. Reciprocity
4. Tension-release dynamic
5. Trust (predictability creates safety)
6. Role reversal (eventually baby initiates)

*(Note: This event was specifically highlighted during framework development as a key example of how pattern-building occurs through simple repeated play.)*

### §4.1 — Reciprocal smile production (6-8 weeks onwards)

Already covered in §3.3 (E12). The smile is simultaneously an input event (baby recognizes the cross-modal context) AND an output event (motor smile production). Social is distinct from other body-inputs in this Read-Control overlap.

### §4.2 — Proto-imperative pointing (9-10 months)

**Observable event**: Baby 9-10 months extends arm and index finger toward a desired object (bottle, toy out of reach) while looking at caregiver. The gesture says "give me that".

**Ladder classification**: **L3** — motor action chunk (pointing) + social communication chunk + request intent.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- Hand motor chunk (per [05 §2.8](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§2.8) pointing arc)
- Object-desire chunk (want specific thing)
- Caregiver-as-agent chunk (mother can act on object on my behalf)
- Communication-intent chunk (gesture is for mother, not for object)

**Framework significance**: Proto-imperative pointing is an **early form of alternative handle to verbal** (Convergence Point 6 preview continued from [05 §7.3](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§7.3)). The baby uses gesture to label "I want that" before having verbal label for either "want" or the specific object. This is consistent with the framework commitment that **verbal is one of several labeling systems**, not the only one.

Cross-reference: [05 §2.8](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§2.8) pointing motor arc. [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §5 will address Convergence Point 6 in full.

### §4.3 — Proto-declarative pointing (12-14 months)

**Observable event**: Baby 12-14 months points at an interesting object (airplane in sky, dog outside) while looking at caregiver, not to request it but to **share the experience**. The gesture says "look at that with me".

**Ladder classification**: **L4** — coordinated triadic chunk with share-intent.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- All proto-imperative chunks above
- PLUS share-attention chunk (E18 joint attention)
- PLUS theory-of-mind precursor (mother will want to see this because she is like me in her relationship to interesting things)
- Communication intent is declarative (share), not imperative (request)

**Framework significance**: Proto-declarative pointing is **more sophisticated than proto-imperative** because it does not have an immediate individual benefit (baby is not asking for the airplane). It is a pure sharing act, implying:
- Baby has chunks about mother's internal states (she doesn't know about the airplane yet)
- Baby wants to modify mother's internal state (make her see the airplane)
- Baby recognizes that shared attention is its own reward (not just a means to object acquisition)

This is the **earliest clear proto-ToM evidence** in F1 window. Full ToM (false-belief understanding) emerges much later (~4 years per Wimmer & Perner 1983), but the conceptual precursors are visible here at 12-14 months.

### §4.4 — E29 Delayed imitation (14-16 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E29](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e29) 16 months):

> Father is washing dishes. 16-month baby picks up a toy plate, wipes it with their hand, copying father's action. Or mother talks on the phone, baby picks up a toy phone, holds it to their ear, and says "hello hello".

**Ladder classification**: **L4** — action sequence chunk from memory + motor execution + self-agency.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- Action sequence chunk stored stably (Meltzoff 1988 showed delayed imitation up to 24 hours at 14 months, up to weeks later)
- "I can do what mother/father does" — self-agency chunk + imitation-as-learning chunk
- Bridge toward symbolic play (object substitution: toy phone = real phone)

**Framework-critical note (E29 Notes)**:

> "THIS IS NOT HARDWARE MIRROR — this is active learning of action chunks. Imitation IS Resonance applied to action — using one's own body as a simulator to recreate observed action. NO mirror-neuron hardware needed — only own motor patterns + visual mapping of other's action + practice + trial-and-error feedback."

**Meltzoff 1988** — foundational delayed imitation research. **Meltzoff's "Like Me" hypothesis** (2007) — babies treat others as "like me" agents, enabling imitation as a mechanism for learning from observation. This hypothesis is consistent with the chunks-based Resonance framework from [Agent/](../Agent/) — it does not require dedicated mirror neurons, only own-action chunks + visual-motor mapping.

### §4.5 — E26 Egocentric empathy (18 months) — cross-reference back to 06b

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E26](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e26) 18 months):

Baby brings own comfort object to distressed mother (covered in full at [06b §5.5](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§5.5)).

**Ladder classification**: **L4** — compound chunks (Resonance + own-template as social action).

**Framework significance** (from 07 social side): E26 is the **first clear use of own-emotional chunks as simulation templates for others' states**. Baby has:
- Recognized mother's distress via Resonance
- Matched it to own distress chunk
- Retrieved own comfort template (bear helps me when I'm distressed)
- Applied the template outward (bring bear to mother)

Hoffman 2000 "egocentric empathy" — Hoffman empathy development Stage 3. This is the clearest F1-window evidence of **Resonance used for pro-social action**. The limitation (egocentric) is that baby assumes mother wants what baby would want — full theory of mind (understanding that mother's desires may differ) emerges later.

**Cross-reference**: [06b §5.5](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md#§5.5) covers the interoceptive side (own chunks as content). 07 covers the social action side (applied outward). Together they give the full analysis of E26.

### §4.6 — E31 "No" autonomy assertion (18-24 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E31](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e31) 18 months):

> 18-month baby. Mother offers food. Baby says "no" even though hungry. Or baby actively reaches for a spoon to feed themselves instead of letting mother do it — "I'll do it!"

**Ladder classification**: **L4** — self-as-agent chunk + verbal + motor autonomy action.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- Self-as-agent chunk: "I want something DIFFERENT from what mother wants — and I want to be allowed to want differently"
- Self-other distinction at level of will/preference (not just identity)
- "No" as marker of self-distinct-from-other (not literally about food refusal)
- Verbal + communicative intent

**Framework significance**: E31 is **NOT about the specific thing being refused** — it is about **exercising agency itself**. Per E31 Notes:

> "Erikson called this phase 'autonomy vs shame and doubt' (~18 mo - 3 yr). 'No' is a MARKER OF SELF-AGENCY, not about the specific thing being refused."

**Self chunks as distinct category**: Per E31 framework implication, **self chunks** (chunks about "I as agent with preferences") are a distinct category from body chunks (hunger, bladder, pain) or social chunks (mother, stranger). Self chunks have content about:
- "I am an entity"
- "I have preferences"
- "My preferences can differ from others' preferences"
- "I can act on my preferences"

Within the social arc itself, different events reach different ladder levels at different ages. This non-uniformity is itself informative:

## §6 — ⭐ Convergence Point 3 Primary Verdict: Multi-modal binding mechanism

> **This is the section this file was drilled to deliver.** Convergence Point 3 asks: "HOW does newborn bind visual + auditory + somatic + emotional inputs into a single unified chunk without a verbal label and with PFC coordination capacity still compiling?" (plan §0.6 Convergence Point 3.) This section commits the **primary verdict** using E12 social smile as keystone evidence.

### §6.1 — The question framed

**Binding problem framing**: When a baby experiences mother's face + mother's voice + body warmth + positive feeling simultaneously, these inputs are processed by **different cortical regions** (FFA/STS for face, auditory cortex for voice, insula for body state, amygdala/OFC for emotional valence). The baby's subsequent behavior (social smile) requires a **unified chunk** that fires based on the conjunction of all channels. How does the binding happen?

**Two naive hypotheses rejected**:
- **Hardware mirror module**: A dedicated "social binding module" is present at birth and fires when all required channels are active. → Rejected because E9/E10 asymmetry + smile contagion timing (3-4 months lag after social smile) + blind-infant smile at 8 weeks via non-visual cues all show the mechanism is **chunks-based**, not hardware-dedicated.
- **PFC top-down coordinator**: A mature PFC binds the cross-modal inputs. → Rejected because PFC is present from birth (per 5 pillars) but binding emerges at specific times (E12 at 6-8 weeks, not immediately) and is not under voluntary control even in adults.

**Framework question**: What combination of mechanisms produces the observed developmental trajectory of multi-modal binding events (cry contagion day 1, direct gaze 2-5 days, social smile 6-8 weeks, smile contagion 4 months, etc.)?

### §6.2 — Four converging mechanism lines

The framework commits to **four concurrent mechanisms** for multi-modal binding, each with independent empirical support:

#### §6.2.1 — Temporal co-occurrence + Hebbian binding (substrate LTP)

**Mechanism**: When inputs from different modalities fire within a narrow temporal window (~50-500 ms), Hebbian binding strengthens synapses between the co-firing regions. Over repeated co-occurrences, the binding stabilizes into a chunk that fires as a unit.

**Empirical anchor**: Early-LTP and late-LTP mechanisms (Bliss & Lømo 1973, Abraham & Williams 2008) — cited in [04 §6.3](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§6.3) for the gradient view of chunks. The same mechanism applies cross-modally when the modalities share downstream targets (multisensory neurons, STS, amygdala).

**Timing prediction**: Chunks that require **many repetitions** to stabilize (like specific mother-face to voice binding) take weeks-months. Simple co-occurrences (own-cry pattern, which you hear every time you cry) stabilize in **hours-days**.

**Fit to data**:
- E9 cry contagion day 1 — single chunk (own-cry pattern), hours-level compile → ✅
- E12 social smile 6-8 weeks — multi-channel binding chunk, weeks-level compile → ✅
- E13 smile contagion 4 months — requires stable own-smile pattern (compiled from E12 weeks of practice), months-level → ✅

**Confidence**: 🟢 (well-established mechanism with specific timing predictions that match developmental data)

#### §6.2.2 — Multisensory neurons (substrate-level binding)

**Mechanism**: Superior colliculus, superior temporal sulcus, and related regions contain neurons with **direct multimodal receptive fields** — a single neuron responds to visual + auditory stimuli at the same spatial/temporal location. When stimuli align, the neuron fires more than the sum of unimodal inputs (**superadditive**).

**Empirical anchor**: Meredith & Stein 1983 (*Science* 221:389-391), Stein & Meredith 1993 ("Merging of the Senses"). The neurons are **hardware-present from birth** (multisensory integration matures over infancy but substrate is there at term).

**Why this matters for newborns**: Multisensory neurons provide **pre-chunks-compile binding**. Before any cortical chunk has compiled, the newborn's multisensory neurons are already binding temporally-aligned inputs at the neural substrate level. This means the newborn has **some multi-modal binding from day 1** even with no compiled chunks.

**Fit to data**:
- Newborn auditory+visual attention orientation (Mehler 1988, multimodal tracking) → ✅
- Early crude binding before E12 chunks → ✅
- Rapid compile of richer chunks because substrate already provides initial binding → ✅

**Confidence**: 🟢 (decades of neurophysiology literature + developmental studies)

#### §6.2.3 — Default Mode Network scaffolding

**Mechanism**: Large-scale network architecture (DMN + frontoparietal + executive control) provides the **distributed substrate** for cross-region communication. Cross-modal binding at the network level requires functional connectivity between distant regions. If the network is present, binding across regions is feasible; if not, it is not.

**Empirical anchor**: Doria et al. 2010 (*PNAS* 107:20015) — 70 preterm infants, DMN + frontoparietal + executive control networks adult-like at term birth. Gao et al. 2009 primitive DMN at 2 weeks postnatal. Fransson 2007 sensorimotor networks early.

**Why this matters**: Without functional DMN, cross-modal binding would be constrained to local circuits only. With DMN, distant regions (e.g., visual cortex + insula + amygdala) can coordinate binding events. The DMN scaffolds the **possibility space** for multi-modal binding.

**Fit to data**:
- Social smile E12 requires distant-region coordination (visual + auditory + insula + amygdala) → DMN provides substrate → ✅
- Preterm infants with disrupted DMN development show delayed social milestones → ✅ (predicted)
- Adult social-binding failures (autism, some schizophrenia) correlate with DMN abnormalities → ✅

**Confidence**: 🟡 (strong network-level evidence, specific chunks-level binding link is inferred)

#### §6.2.4 — Amygdala emotional tag as binding cue

**Mechanism**: The amygdala activates to emotionally-valenced stimuli and projects broadly to sensory cortices + PFC + insula. When affective peaks occur during multi-modal events, the amygdala applies a **global valence tag** to all co-firing elements. This tag strengthens the cross-modal association **more than neutral co-occurrences**.

**Empirical anchor**: Classical fear conditioning literature (LeDoux 2012 among others) — amygdala tagging strengthens associations across modalities. Infant emotional memory literature shows similar effects (e.g., bottle preference correlates with feeding-experience emotional tag).

**Why this matters for E12**: Social smile typically emerges during **peak-affect caregiving moments** — mother smiling, talking warmly, holding baby, eye contact, mother feeling love + delight. The baby's amygdala receives a positive valence signal. This **globally tags** all concurrent channels, strengthening the multi-modal binding substantially compared to neutral co-occurrences.

**Framework prediction**: Multi-modal chunks bound with peak-affective moments compile faster than same-content chunks without affect. Flashbulb memory mechanism (Brown & Kulik 1977 for adults) has an infant version — high-affect social moments compile stronger chunks.

**Fit to data**:
- E12 social smile typically emerging during high-affect caregiving → ✅
- Chronic low-affect environments (postpartum depression) delay or muffle social smile → ✅
- Flashbulb-memory-like infant chunks for emotionally-peak events → ✅
- Body-state-at-compile mechanism (from 06a §7 + 06b §6.3, refined N+4c2-REV1) is consistent with amygdala-based binding contribution → ✅ Note: the earlier "cortisol tagging" framing was refined — cortisol is the sustainer of a body state, not the binding agent. The amygdala affective tagging mechanism in §6.2.4 is one of 4 concurrent mechanisms for multi-modal binding; it is distinct from (but complementary with) the imposed-parent dual-source mechanism for emotional chunks compile.

**Confidence**: 🟡 (mechanism well-established; infant-specific evidence is indirect but consistent)

### §6.3 — ⭐ Verdict committed: Multi-mechanism binding

🟡 **Primary verdict**: **Multi-modal binding at newborn is an emergent property of 4 concurrent mechanisms** — temporal co-occurrence + Hebbian LTP, multisensory neurons, DMN scaffolding, and amygdala affective tagging — operating on temporally-correlated afferent streams. No single mechanism is sufficient; all four contribute. E12 social smile at 6-8 weeks is the keystone observable event that requires all four to be operational.

**Confidence level**: 🟡 for the specific multi-mechanism architecture (strong mechanism-level evidence, less direct infant-specific evidence for each mechanism's individual contribution). 🟢 for the negative claim (no single "binder module").

**Precisions of claim**:

1. **No single binder**: Framework rejects hypothesis of a dedicated "cross-modal binding module". Binding is distributed across multiple mechanisms.

2. **Substrate-first, chunks-second**: Newborn has substrate-level binding (multisensory neurons + DMN + hardwired pathways) from birth. Compiled chunks add specificity + pattern-completion on top of substrate binding.

3. **Affective valence accelerates compile**: Chunks bound with peak-affective amygdala tags compile faster than neutral-affect chunks. This explains why social smile (high-affect caregiving) compiles in weeks while more mundane cross-modal bindings take longer.

4. **Developmental trajectory explained**: The observed timing of multi-modal events (cry contagion day 1, direct gaze 2-5 days, social smile 6-8 weeks, smile contagion 4 months) is consistent with multi-mechanism compile where different chunks require different amounts of repetition × affect × substrate support.

5. **The "binding problem" is ill-posed**: The classical binding problem asks how distributed neural activity becomes a unified experience. The framework answer is that **there is no binding step** per se — there is continuous multi-mechanism constraint satisfaction that produces unified behavior as an emergent property. Asking "how is X bound to Y" is analogous to asking "how does a chord sound harmonious" — the answer is not a step but a property of the system.

### §6.4 — Implications of the verdict

**For H1 (chunk substrate)**:
- H1 is supported: chunks are **strength-weighted associative networks** (per 04 §6.4 Convergence Point 1 verdict) with **cross-modal content** from the start. Chunks are not single-modality first and then bound; they are multi-modal from the substrate level.
- The architecture is consistent with biological substrate (Hebbian LTP across distributed regions, amygdala tagging, multisensory neurons).

**For Convergence Point 2 (Precondition-2 compile mechanism)**:
- Adds **multi-modal richness** as an implicit modulator of compile rate (from 06b §6 framework)

- Formula refinement: compile rate ≈ f(exposure, saliency, contingency, valence, multi-modal richness)

**For old "PFC offline" view**:
- Further falsified. Multi-modal binding requires distributed cortical substrate (DMN) and PFC reception (for chunks firing to generate behavioral output). If PFC were offline, E12 social smile could not emerge.

## §11 — Open questions

4. **Temporal window for binding**: What is the precise temporal window within which channels must co-fire for binding to occur? Adult multisensory research suggests ~50-500 ms; infant window may differ. Testable with staggered multi-modal presentation studies.
