# 04 — Auditory System Developmental Arc ⭐ Convergence Point 1 primary

> **File 04 of F1 Child-Chunk-Development sub-folder.** Second of six body-input arc files. This file traces auditory system chunk compile mechanism from **fetal 26 weeks gestation** (when the fetal cochlea becomes functional) through ~24 months postnatal. The auditory system is **unique among F1 body-inputs**: it is the ONLY body-input where chunks compilation begins PRENATALLY and a library of compiled chunks is already present at birth.
>
> **⭐ Convergence Point 1 primary verdict file**: phoneme discrimination trajectory 0-12 months provides the **cleanest gradient evidence** in the entire F1 drill. This file commits the primary verdict on proto-chunk gradient vs discrete view.

---

## §0 — Abstract

🟢 **Claim**: Auditory chunks compile in a **clear gradient** over 26 weeks gestation → ~24 months postnatal. The trajectory is especially instructive because:

1. **Chunks compile begins prenatally** — DeCasper & Spence 1986 + Mehler 1988 + Moon 1993 + Kisilevsky 2003 give four independent lines of evidence that specific auditory chunks (maternal voice, native language rhythm, specific story patterns, maternal voice vs stranger) are detectable at birth or even in utero. The newborn arrives with an auditory chunks library already in place.

2. **Phoneme discrimination shows clean gradient** — universal discrimination at birth → native-language-biased narrowing over 6-12 months (Werker & Tees 1984 classic paper + Kuhl perceptual magnet work). This is **not a discrete threshold event** but a smooth monotonic trajectory that provides the cleanest "proto-chunk → stable chunk" gradient evidence available in developmental psychology.

3. **Earliest cleanly documented L2 post-name-recognition event is 4.5 months** — Mandel, Jusczyk & Pisoni 1995 demonstrate own-name recognition at 4.5 months, a **pattern-match event requiring compiled auditory chunk** for the specific name sound string. This is one of the most falsification-strong evidence points against the old "PFC offline" view — a 4.5-month-old doing stimulus-category discrimination of a compiled auditory string.

4. **Receptive-productive asymmetry preview** — name recognition 4.5 months (receptive) vs first verbal production ~18 months (productive, Feel-Example E21) = **13.5 month gap**. This is the receptive side of Convergence Point 5 / H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry, drilled fully in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md).

## §1 — Position within F1

### §1.1 — Unique status: auditory is the prenatal system

Of F1's six body-input systems, **only auditory has substantial prenatal chunk compilation**:

| Body-input | Hardware at birth | Chunks at birth |
|---|---|---|
| Visual | Ready (03 §2) | Only CONSPEC innate template, essentially no compiled chunks |
| **Auditory** | **Ready (functional 26 wk gestation)** | **Substantial library: maternal voice, native rhythm, story patterns, heartbeat** |
| Motor | Ready (reflex circuits) | Minimal (some fetal movement patterns) |
| Interoceptive | Ready (viscera + nociceptors) | Essentially none for post-birth body states |
| Social | N/A at birth | None (no post-birth social experience) |
| Verbal | Articulatory apparatus immature | None |

**Why auditory is prenatally privileged**:

- **Physical**: Amniotic fluid transmits low-frequency sound well; maternal voice at ~65 dB intrauterine + ~45-60 dB for external sounds. Fetus hears continuously in last trimester.
- **Hardware**: Cochlear function begins ~26 weeks gestation (Moore et al. 1995, 1996). Auditory cortex develops in parallel — Heschl's gyrus primary auditory area is among the earliest cortical regions to reach functional maturity.
- **Exposure duration**: ~14 weeks of prenatal auditory exposure (26 wk → 40 wk birth) with continuous maternal voice + environmental sounds provides substantial repetition for chunk compile mechanism (LTP-based repetition compile, [Chunk.md](../../Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md) §2).

**Framework implication**: The auditory system **pre-compiles chunks** before birth. The newborn's auditory system does not start from zero — it starts from a library of ~14 weeks of exposure. This asymmetry across body-inputs has important implications for Convergence Point 2 (Precondition-2 Chunk-Substrate compile rate) — auditory has a 14-week head start on all other systems.

### §1.2 — Why auditory is the Convergence Point 1 primary verdict file

Phoneme discrimination 0-12 months is the **cleanest empirical window** available in developmental psychology for observing **chunk compile trajectory as a smooth gradient**. Three reasons:

1. **Well-defined categorical task**: phoneme discrimination is binary (same or different?) and testable via habituation / head-turn procedure with high reliability.
2. **Multiple reliable age points**: Werker & Tees 1984 tested same children longitudinally (6mo, 8-10mo, 11-12mo) with same stimuli showing progressive narrowing.
3. **Contrast between two conditions** (native vs non-native phoneme): creates clean gradient for "chunks compile for used categories, weaken for unused categories".

No other F1 body-input system has this combination of cleanliness + age resolution + longitudinal data. Visual (03) has gradient evidence but more muddled (object permanence involves multiple sub-chunks each with own trajectory). Motor, interoceptive, social, verbal each have their own stories. **Auditory phoneme discrimination is the keystone for the gradient verdict.**

## §2 — Connect Phase (Hardware Readiness)

> Unlike visual (03 §2) where "connect" is entirely postnatal, auditory "connect" happens **prenatally**. The hardware is online and receiving input from ~26 weeks gestation onward.

### §2.1 — Fetal cochlear function from 26 weeks gestation

🟢 **Evidence**: Fetal cochlear function emerges ~26 weeks gestation, as measured by:
- Fetal startle response to loud sounds (Leader 1987)
- Fetal heart rate changes to auditory stimuli (Kisilevsky et al. 1992, 2003)
- Preterm infant response to sound (Moore, Perazio & Braun 1995; Moore 1996)

Before 26 weeks, the cochlear hair cells and auditory brainstem pathways are not yet mature enough for sound transduction + neural response. After 26 weeks, **functional hearing exists**, though with different frequency response than adult ears (muffled, low-frequency biased due to amniotic fluid + maternal tissue filtering).

**Fetal auditory environment**:
- Maternal heartbeat: continuous rhythmic low-frequency signal
- Maternal voice: conducted through tissues + amniotic fluid, ~65 dB in utero, preserved vowel formants
- External voices: ~40-50 dB filtered through maternal abdomen
- Bowel sounds, vascular sounds: continuous background

**🟢 Implication**: The fetus has **~14 weeks of continuous auditory input exposure** (weeks 26-40) before birth. This provides the compile window for prenatal auditory chunks identified in §2.2-§2.4.

### §2.2 — Maternal voice chunk compiles prenatally

🟢 **Event (fetal)**: Fetal heart rate changes differentially to maternal voice vs stranger's voice in late gestation. (Kisilevsky et al. 2003 *Psychological Science* 14:220-224 — 60 late-pregnancy fetuses, maternal voice → HR acceleration, stranger voice → HR deceleration.)

🟢 **Event (newborn)**: Newborns (2-3 days old) preferentially activate non-nutritive sucking to hear maternal voice recording vs stranger voice recording. (DeCasper & Fifer 1980 *Science* 208:1174-1176.)

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- **Maternal voice chunk** — compiled from ~14 weeks of continuous in utero exposure
- Acoustic features chunked: mother's fundamental frequency, formant structure, prosodic rhythm, characteristic vocal patterns
- **Framework claim**: this is a **genuine compiled chunk**, not innate — it's mother-specific, not generic female voice. A different mother's voice does not trigger the same response.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢. Pattern-match fires on familiar acoustic pattern (non-nutritive sucking rate = behavioral marker of pattern recognition). No action involvement (sucking modulation is an attention/engagement marker, not goal-directed action).

**Why this is a load-bearing event for F1**: The maternal voice chunk at birth is **the first documented case of compiled chunk in human development**. It is:
- Specific (mother, not just "female voice")
- Compiled from exposure (not innate)
- Available at day 1 post-birth
- Demonstrable via looking-time / sucking-rate paradigms
- Replicated (DeCasper 1980, DeCasper & Spence 1986, DeCasper et al. 1994)

This falsifies any view claiming chunks begin compiling only after birth. It establishes the 14-week prenatal window as a **functional chunk compile period**.

### §2.3 — Auditory cortex + Heschl's gyrus maturation

🟢 Primary auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus, Brodmann area 41) is among the **earliest cortical areas** to reach adult-like structural organization:
- Myelination begins ~6 months gestation
- Reaches 75% of adult myelination by ~18 months postnatal (Kinney et al. 1988)
- Synaptic density peaks around 3 months postnatal (Huttenlocher 1979)

Auditory processing streams (ventral = "what" pathway, dorsal = "where" pathway) are detectable in infant fMRI studies from ~3 months (Dehaene-Lambertz et al. 2002, 2006 — landmark infant fMRI language processing studies).

**🟡 Framework note**: Just as with PFC in [01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md](../Foundation/01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md) §3, auditory cortex was never "offline" — it was hardware-ready prenatally and functionally active from the moment cochlear transduction became possible. The postnatal "maturation" observed is **content specialization** (chunks for specific phonemes, voices, words), not hardware coming online.

### §2.4 — Native language rhythm chunks compile prenatally

🟢 **Event**: Newborns (4 days old) prefer hearing their native language's rhythm pattern over foreign languages with different rhythm class. (Mehler, Jusczyk, Lambertz et al. 1988 *Cognition* 29:143-178, with 4-day-old Paris-born infants showing preference for French over Russian.)

🟢 **Related**: Newborns from Mandarin-speaking mothers show different language-rhythm preferences than newborns from English-speaking mothers. (Moon, Cooper & Fifer 1993 *Infant Behavior & Development* 16:495-500.)

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- **Native language rhythm chunk** — prosodic pattern at the rhythmic-class level (stress-timed vs syllable-timed vs mora-timed)
- Compiled from ~14 weeks prenatal exposure to maternal speech rhythm + ambient native-language speech
- Rhythm class, not phoneme detail: newborns do NOT yet show phoneme-level native bias (that emerges 6-12 months, §3.5)

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢. Pattern-match on rhythm class, behavioral marker via preference test.

**Framework importance**: Mehler 1988 is one of the most cited papers in developmental psycholinguistics. It establishes that **chunks of language-specific acoustic patterns** are compiled in utero. The chunks are coarse (rhythm-class level) not fine (phoneme level), but they are **language-specific learned chunks**, not generic acoustic detection.

### §2.5 — DeCasper & Spence 1986 — the compile evidence

🟢 **Seminal study**: DeCasper & Spence 1986 *Infant Behavior & Development* 9:133-150. "Prenatal maternal speech influences newborns' perception of speech sounds."

**Design**: 16 pregnant women in last 6 weeks of pregnancy read *The Cat in the Hat* (Dr. Seuss) aloud twice daily to their fetuses. After birth (2-3 days), newborns tested with non-nutritive sucking paradigm on: (a) recording of *Cat in the Hat* read by mother, (b) recording of a novel story (*The King, the Mice, and the Cheese*) read by the same mother.

**Result**: 🟢 Newborns **preferentially modulated sucking** to hear the *Cat in the Hat* recording (the familiar pre-birth story). The preference is **specific to the story pattern**, not just "maternal voice" — both recordings used mother's voice, differing only in the story content.

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- **Specific story rhythm/prosody chunk** for *The Cat in the Hat* — compiled from ~6 weeks × 2/day = ~84 prenatal exposures
- The chunk includes rhythmic-prosodic pattern of the specific text, not just word sounds (because story text is not understood by fetus, only its rhythmic signature)

**Why this study is load-bearing**:

1. **Exposure count is countable**: ~84 repetitions over 6 weeks produced a **detectable chunk**. This gives a rough estimate of **minimum compile threshold** for a chunk under ideal conditions (pre-birth, no interference, consistent source, emotional-neutral content).
2. **Chunk is specific**, not generic: newborns can distinguish two stories read by the same mother. The distinguishing features must be encoded in the chunk.
3. **Chunk persists across birth**: the chunk compiled in utero is still accessible 2-3 days after birth, despite the massive sensory cascade of birth.
4. **Replicates the repetition compile mechanism** ([Chunk.md](../../Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md) §2.1) — LTP-based compile via repeated exposure.

**DeCasper 1986 is thus not just "cute prenatal learning demo" — it is direct empirical evidence for one of F1's core claims: chunks compile via repetition exposure, and the compile window is active prenatally.**

### §2.6 — Summary of prenatal auditory chunks library

🟢🟡 **At birth, the newborn auditory system contains this compiled chunks library**:

| Chunk | Compile window | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Maternal heartbeat rhythm | 14+ weeks | Inferred from calming effect of heartbeat sounds |
| Maternal voice (specific) | ~14 weeks | DeCasper & Fifer 1980, Kisilevsky 2003 |
| Native language rhythm class | ~14 weeks | Mehler et al. 1988, Moon et al. 1993 |
| Specific story prosodic pattern | ~6 weeks (if repeated) | DeCasper & Spence 1986 |
| Ambient voices (overheard) | Variable | Indirect evidence |

**This library is the starting state for the postnatal auditory arc described in §3-§4.** Contrast with visual (03 §2) where the starting state is essentially empty (CONSPEC innate template only, no compiled chunks).

## §3 — Read Phase (Input Detection Chunks) — Postnatal Trajectory

> Postnatal auditory chunks build on the prenatal library. This section covers the expansion and specialization of auditory chunks from birth through ~24 months, culminating in the Convergence Point 1 gradient evidence in §6.

### §3.1 — Moro startle reflex (L0 reference)

🟢 **Event**: Newborns respond to sudden loud sounds (clap, door slam) with full-body Moro reflex — arms extending outward then returning inward, often with cry.

🟢 **Research**: Standard pediatric neurological exam; present at birth, typically disappears 3-6 months.

🟡 **Chunks required**: None. Innate brainstem reflex circuit (auditory → vestibular nucleus → motor).

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L0** 🟢. Pure reflex, no learned chunks involved.

**Inclusion here**: As a **reference event** for ladder calibration — Moro is the unambiguous L0 auditory event at birth. Anything higher on the ladder must be distinguishable from pure Moro-like reflex.

### §3.2 — Mother voice preference at birth (L2 inherited from prenatal)

Covered in §2.2. Carries over from prenatal. L2 🟢.

### §3.3 — Native language rhythm preference at birth (L2 inherited from prenatal)

Covered in §2.4. L2 🟢.

### §3.4 — Phoneme discrimination — universal at birth

🟢 **Event**: Newborns and young infants (0-6 months) discriminate phoneme contrasts from virtually **any** human language, including contrasts they have never heard.

🟢 **Research**:
- Eimas, Siqueland, Jusczyk & Vigorito 1971 *Science* 171:303-306 — seminal study showing 1-4 month olds discriminate /ba/ vs /pa/ (English voice-onset-time contrast)
- Trehub 1976 — infants discriminate Czech phoneme contrasts absent from English
- Werker & Tees 1984 *Infant Behavior & Development* 7:49-63 — infants under 6 months discriminate Hindi dental/retroflex contrast and Salish (Nthlakapmx) velar/uvular contrast, both ABSENT from English

**Core finding**: Before ~6 months, infants discriminate virtually any phoneme contrast from any language. They are "universal listeners" (Kuhl 2004 review phrase).

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- Acoustic feature detection chunks (voice onset time, formant transitions, spectral features) — innately tuned auditory system + early cortical chunk compile
- **No language-specific chunks required yet** — the universal discrimination reflects general acoustic sensitivity, not learned categories

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢. Pattern-match on acoustic-feature differences, behavioral marker via head-turn / habituation paradigm. No action involvement.

### §3.5 — Phoneme perceptual narrowing 6-12 months ⭐ gradient evidence

🟢 **Event**: Between 6 and 12 months, infants **lose** the ability to discriminate non-native phoneme contrasts while **maintaining or strengthening** native contrasts.

🟢 **Research — key longitudinal study**:
- Werker & Tees 1984 — tested English-learning infants at 3 age points (6-8mo, 8-10mo, 10-12mo) on non-native contrasts (Hindi, Salish):

| Age | Non-native Hindi contrast | Non-native Salish contrast |
|---|---|---|
| 6-8 months | ~90-100% discriminate | ~90-100% discriminate |
| 8-10 months | ~50% discriminate | ~60% discriminate |
| 10-12 months | ~10-20% discriminate | ~10% discriminate |

**This is the cleanest gradient evidence in developmental psychology.**

- Kuhl et al. 1992 *Science* 255:606-608 — "Linguistic experience alters phonetic perception in infants by 6 months of age" — demonstrated perceptual magnet effect (native-language phoneme prototypes attract perception of similar sounds, reducing discrimination near category centers)
- Kuhl 2004 *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 5:831-843 — review article integrating decade+ of findings; coined "native language magnet" theory

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required** (the core claim of this file):
- **Native phoneme category chunks** — strengthening over 6-12 months via exposure repetition
- **Non-native phoneme chunks** — compiling weakly or not at all, since zero exposure to non-native contrasts
- **Perceptual magnet effect** — as native category chunk strengthens, acoustic variations within the category get mapped TO the category prototype (attracted to it), reducing the perceptual distance between within-category variants

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢 throughout. Pattern-match on acoustic-category difference, behavioral marker via habituation / head-turn.

**⭐ Convergence Point 1 contribution**: The narrowing is **smooth and monotonic**, not discrete. From 6 months (universal listener) to 12 months (native-biased), the loss of discrimination happens **progressively month by month**, not as a single threshold event. This is incompatible with a discrete "chunk compiled / not compiled" binary view. Full verdict analysis in §6.

### §3.6 — Own name recognition at 4.5 months (Mandel, Jusczyk & Pisoni 1995) ⭐

🟢 **Event**: 4.5-month-old infants recognize their own name — they look longer toward a speaker producing their own name than toward a speaker producing a different name with matched stress pattern.

🟢 **Research**: Mandel, Jusczyk & Pisoni 1995 *Psychological Science* 6:314-317. "Infants' recognition of the sound patterns of their own names." 

**Design**: 24 infants (mean age 4.5 months). Head-turn preference paradigm: when looking toward speaker producing own name (e.g., "Jennifer"), infant listens longer than when speaker produces different name with matched stress (e.g., "Victoria"). Reliable preference demonstrated.

**Extensions**:
- Jusczyk & Aslin 1995 — earlier work on word segmentation precursors
- Bortfeld, Morgan, Golinkoff & Rathbun 2005 — 6-month-olds use own name as anchor for word segmentation
- Parise, Friederici & Striano 2010 — own name elicits distinctive ERP signature in 5-month-olds

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- **Own name auditory chunk** — compiled from ~4.5 months of hearing the name in various contexts (mother calling baby, family addressing baby, talk directed at baby)
- The chunk includes phonetic sequence + stress pattern + typical prosodic context
- The chunk is **name-specific**, not generic word detection (since the control stimulus is a matched-stress different name)

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢. Pattern-match on specific acoustic sequence, behavioral marker via looking-time / head-turn.

**⭐ Why this is the strongest L2 event in F1**:

1. **Earliest well-documented compiled-chunk pattern match for a named entity**. At 4.5 months, baby has compiled a chunk for "my name" and fires that chunk on hearing it.

2. **Falsifies the old "PFC offline" view**: at 4.5 months, a baby discriminates two stress-matched disyllabic strings — recognizing one as "mine" and one as not. This requires compiled chunk + pattern-match attention modulation. Under the old view, this kind of discrimination was typically attributed to later cortical maturation. Mandel 1995 pushes the well-documented capability 6+ months earlier.

3. **Supports the reframe committed in [01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md](../Foundation/01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md)** — the reframe says "PFC hardware is online, chunks are what's missing". Own name recognition at 4.5 months shows a specific chunk **compiled and operational** for a task the old view would have attributed to "PFC not ready". The chunks account wins cleanly.

4. **Receptive-productive gap preview** (Convergence Point 5): name recognition 4.5mo (receptive) vs first productive words 10-14mo (productive side) = 6-10 month gap. And for the baby's own productive first word being the baby's own name, it's typically even later. This receptive-productive gap will be drilled in §9 below and fully in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md).

### §3.7 — Word segmentation from continuous speech (Saffran, Aslin & Newport 1996) ⭐

🟢 **Event**: 8-month-old infants segment continuous speech streams into word-like units using **statistical learning** — tracking syllable co-occurrence probabilities.

🟢 **Research**: Saffran, Aslin & Newport 1996 *Science* 274:1926-1928. "Statistical learning by 8-month-old infants."

**Design**: Infants listened to 2 minutes of continuous nonsense syllables with no pauses, no prosodic cues, and no acoustic word boundaries. The only cue to "word-hood" was **transitional probability**: syllable pairs within "words" always co-occurred (probability 1.0), while syllable pairs spanning "word boundaries" co-occurred only probabilistically (probability 0.33). After the 2-minute exposure, infants discriminated:
- "Part-words" (sequences that spanned word boundaries in the exposure)
- "Words" (sequences that were fully within word boundaries)

**Result**: 🟢 Infants showed reliable discrimination, preferring part-words (novel) over words (familiar) in a head-turn paradigm. **2 minutes of exposure compiled detectable word-like chunks.**

**Extensions**:
- Saffran, Johnson, Aslin & Newport 1999 — statistical learning also works for tone sequences (not just language)
- Aslin, Saffran & Newport 1998 — longer exposure + cross-linguistic replication
- Pelucchi, Hay & Saffran 2009 — infants learn adjacent transitional probabilities from natural speech, not just lab stimuli

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- **Transitional probability tracking mechanism** — compiles co-occurrence statistics over time
- **Candidate word chunks** — high-probability syllable sequences emerge as chunks
- The compile mechanism is **repetition-based + statistical**, refining [Chunk.md](../../Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md) §2.1 repetition mechanism to include frequency-based weighting

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2-L3** 🟢. Pattern-match on statistical structure, behavioral marker via head-turn preference. Borders L3 because the chunks are used **actively** in subsequent discrimination tasks (prediction + matching).

**Why Saffran 1996 is load-bearing for F1**:

1. **Compile speed evidence**: 2 minutes of exposure produces a detectable chunk. This is faster than Mandel name (4.5 months accumulated exposure) or DeCasper story (6 weeks × 2/day). This gives an **upper bound on minimum compile time** for simple statistical chunks under ideal conditions.

2. **Mechanism specificity**: transitional probability tracking is a specific, testable compile mechanism that can be implemented and simulated. The implications for artificial learning systems are significant.

3. **Support for gradient view (Convergence Point 1)**: chunks emerge as statistical regularities strengthen over time — not as discrete thresholds. Consistent with phoneme narrowing §3.5.

### §3.8 — Common noun comprehension 6-9 months (Bergelson & Swingley 2012) ⭐

🟢 **Event**: 6-9 month olds **comprehend** common object nouns — they look toward the correct referent when named, in a looking-while-listening paradigm.

🟢 **Research**: Bergelson & Swingley 2012 *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 109:3253-3258. "At 6-9 months, human infants know the meanings of many common nouns."

**Design**: 6-9 month infants shown side-by-side images of two common objects (apple/banana, foot/hand, etc.) while a caregiver named one. Eye tracking measured which image the infant looked at when the word was spoken. Infants looked significantly at the named object — above chance.

**Key insight from study**: This was **earlier than widely assumed**. Pre-2012, developmental psycholinguistics textbooks typically placed first comprehension at ~12 months. Bergelson & Swingley showed 6 months earlier, with robust methodology and replication.

🟡 **Chunks plausibly required**:
- **Word-form chunks** — auditory sequences for each known word
- **Object-form chunks** — visual chunks for each known object
- **Binding chunks** — linking word to object (multi-modal bind, see §8)
- **Look-to-referent chunk** — output motor response

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L3** 🟢. Coordinated output (gaze shift to correct referent), pattern-match on word heard → binding to object → action. Low bar L3 because response is only gaze, not full motor.

**Framework importance**: Common noun comprehension at 6-9 months establishes that by mid-first-year, infants have:
- Dozens of word-form chunks compiled
- Corresponding object-form chunks compiled
- Word-to-object binding chunks compiled
- All well before first productive word (typically ~12 months)

This is another strong piece of **receptive-productive asymmetry evidence** — see §9.

### §3.9 — Speech prosody-to-meaning chunks (8-12 months)

🟢 **Event**: Infants 8-12 months discriminate emotional prosody (happy vs sad voice), question vs statement intonation, infant-directed vs adult-directed speech (preferring infant-directed). (Fernald 1993, Werker, Pegg & McLeod 1994.)

🟡 **Chunks required**: Prosodic pattern chunks + valence-binding chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢. Pattern-match on prosodic envelope.

**Framework note**: Prosody chunks compile earlier than word meaning chunks because prosodic features (pitch contour, intensity envelope) are coarser / more robust to acoustic noise.

### §3.10 — Reading side summary — what is compiled by 12 months

🟢🟡 **Auditory chunks library at 12 months** (contrast with birth in §2.6):

| Chunk class | Status at 12 months |
|---|---|
| Maternal voice | Stable, rich |
| Native language rhythm | Stable, strong |
| Native phoneme categories | Specialized (non-native discrimination weakened) |
| Own name | Compiled, stable from ~4.5mo |
| Common word forms | Dozens compiled (Bergelson 2012) |
| Word-object binds | Dozens compiled (Bergelson 2012) |
| Statistical word segmentation chunks | Operational (Saffran 1996) |
| Emotional prosody chunks | Compiled |
| Question vs statement prosody | Compiled |

This is a **rich, specialized chunks library** before first productive word. The receptive system is far ahead of the productive system — see §9.

## §4 — Control Phase (Output Chunks — Limited for Pure Auditory)

> Auditory Control phase is limited compared to visual (03 §4) because pure auditory motor output is sparse — mainly sound localization via head turn. Most action-involved auditory events are multi-modal (head turn + attention, vocalization + articulation).

### §4.1 — Head turn toward sound source (3-4 months)

🟢 **Event**: Infants turn head toward sound source with reliable accuracy by 3-4 months; earlier (0-2 months) head orientation is crude/inconsistent.

🟢 **Research**: Muir & Field 1979, Morrongiello 1988.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Sound localization chunks (interaural time difference, interaural level difference) + head motor chunks + binding chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2-L3** 🟡. Pattern-match on sound direction triggers head motor.

### §4.2 — Attention orienting to familiar voices

🟢 **Event**: Infants 2-4 months show increased attention (head orientation, gaze, quieting) when they hear mother's voice vs stranger's voice.

🟡 **Chunks required**: Mother voice chunk (§2.2) + attention modulation chunks.

**PFC-Inference Ladder**: **L2** 🟢.

## §6 — ⭐ Convergence Point 1 Primary Verdict: Proto-chunk gradient vs discrete

> **This is the section this file was drilled to deliver.** Convergence Point 1 asks: "Does 'proto-chunk' exist as an intermediate stage between raw firing and stable compiled chunk, or is chunk compilation a discrete threshold event?" (plan §0.6 Convergence Point 1.) This section commits the **primary verdict** using phoneme discrimination trajectory 0-12 months as keystone evidence.

### §6.1 — The question framed

**Two competing views**:

**Discrete view**: Chunk compilation is a threshold event. Before the threshold, the system has "no chunk" (just raw sensory firing). After the threshold, the chunk exists and fires reliably. There is no meaningful intermediate state — it's binary.

**Gradient view**: Chunk compilation is a continuum. Early in compilation, the chunk is weak — fires sometimes, generates weak pattern-match, may not yet modulate attention reliably. Mid-compilation, the chunk is moderate — fires more reliably, stronger pattern-match. Late-compilation, the chunk is stable — fires reliably, strong attention modulation, durable across time + noise. Proto-chunks are meaningful intermediate states.

**Why it matters for H1** (plan §0.6): The architectural commitment for H1 (chunk substrate cross-cutting hypothesis) depends on this answer. If chunks are discrete, we need different substrate primitives (hard thresholds + all-or-nothing firing). If chunks are gradient, the substrate primitive is strength-weighted associative networks with continuous tuning (closer to biological LTP / Hebbian learning).

### §6.2 — Phoneme discrimination trajectory — the keystone evidence

**Werker & Tees 1984 data** (reconstructed from the paper):

English-learning infants, tested on non-native Hindi retroflex/dental contrast:

| Age group | ~Discrimination rate |
|---|---|
| 6-8 months | ~93% of infants discriminate |
| 8-10 months | ~54% of infants discriminate |
| 10-12 months | ~12% of infants discriminate |

Same infants (longitudinal), tested on non-native Salish velar/uvular contrast:

| Age group | ~Discrimination rate |
|---|---|
| 6-8 months | ~100% of infants discriminate |
| 8-10 months | ~67% of infants discriminate |
| 10-12 months | ~10% of infants discriminate |

**Key feature of the data**: The decline from ~90-100% → ~10-12% across 4 months is **smooth and monotonic**. At 8-10 months, infants are in a **clearly intermediate state** — some discriminate the contrast, some don't, and individual infants may show partial discrimination (weaker effect than 6-8mo but stronger than 10-12mo).

**🟢 This is direct behavioral evidence of intermediate states** — neither "chunk absent" nor "chunk fully compiled" but something in between.

### §6.3 — Multiple lines converge on gradient view

Phoneme narrowing is not the only gradient evidence. Multiple lines from across F1 converge:

**From 03 Visual**:
- Object permanence sub-chunks (§3.8) compile at different rates (2.5mo trajectory, 3.5mo solidity, 4-6mo complex scenarios, 8-9mo action-based) — sub-chunks within "permanence" emerge at different rates, not all at once
- Face perceptual narrowing 6-9 months (§3.9) — parallel gradient across visual modality
- Feature integration 3-6 months (§3.5) — gradient emergence

**From 04 Auditory (this file)**:
- Phoneme discrimination trajectory 6-12 months — keystone cleanest gradient
- Word segmentation Saffran 1996 — 2-minute-exposure compile is fast but implies the chunk is initially weak and strengthens over subsequent hearings
- Kuhl perceptual magnet effect — category boundaries sharpen over time, not discrete
- Common noun comprehension Bergelson 2012 — word-object binds compile at different rates, with dozens compiled by 6-9mo

**From biological mechanism**:
- **Early-LTP vs late-LTP** (Bliss & Lømo 1973, Abraham & Williams 2008). Early-LTP is protein-synthesis-independent, lasting hours. Late-LTP is protein-synthesis-dependent, lasting days-weeks. The conversion from early-LTP to late-LTP is **itself a gradient** — synaptic weight changes occur continuously during the transition, not as a binary switch.
- Hebbian learning (Hebb 1949) — "neurons that fire together wire together" is an **incrementally-strengthening** process, not discrete.
- Infant fMRI (Dehaene-Lambertz 2002, 2006) shows graded neural response to speech features, consistent with graded chunks.

### §6.4 — Verdict committed: Gradient view ⭐

🟢 **Primary verdict**: **Chunk compilation is gradient, not discrete. Proto-chunks exist as meaningful intermediate states.**

**Confidence level**: 🟢 for the core claim (multiple independent empirical lines + biological mechanism consistency).

**Precision of claim**:

1. **Chunks have strength**. A chunk is not binary (exists / doesn't exist) but is at a **strength level** that determines firing reliability, pattern-match intensity, and attention modulation.

2. **Proto-chunks are weak chunks**. They fire sometimes, produce partial pattern-match, modulate attention inconsistently. They are the early-LTP / early-compile state before late-LTP / stable-chunk consolidation.

3. **Chunk strengthening is driven by**: (a) repetition, (b) emotional peak tagging, (c) consistency of input, (d) multi-modal reinforcement, (e) sleep consolidation (see plan §0.7 compile mechanisms). Each of these acts **incrementally** to strengthen chunks.

4. **Chunks can weaken too**. Unused chunks decay (phoneme non-native narrowing). Chunks can also be overwritten or merged with other chunks.

5. **The "discrete" intuition comes from thresholding at observation**. When a chunk crosses the threshold where its firing is reliable enough for behavioral detection (looking time change, head turn, etc.), it appears to "switch on". But the underlying strengthening has been gradient — the observation threshold is what makes it look discrete.

**What the discrete view might save**: In certain special cases, some chunks may **appear** discrete because of thresholding phenomena. Stereopsis (03 §2.5) emerges suddenly at 3-4 months due to binocular cell tuning + vergence crossing together at threshold. But even here, underlying substrate maturation is gradient — the discrete appearance is the observation moment when multiple gradient processes cross a shared threshold.

### §6.5 — Implications of the verdict

**For H1 (chunk substrate)**:

1. **Substrate primitives are strength-weighted associative networks**, not binary symbolic chunks. This is consistent with biological substrate (LTP/LTD, Hebbian learning, neural network models) and is the substrate architecture F1 commits to.

2. **Proto-chunks** are useful as a theoretical term for "chunks that are forming but not yet behaviorally detectable". They refer to real neural activity — the pre-threshold strengthening state.

3. **Chunk compile rate varies** by input saliency + repetition frequency + emotional tagging + multi-modal reinforcement. This explains why different body-input systems compile at different rates (visual fast, interoceptive slow) and why individual babies vary widely in milestone timing.

**For Convergence Point 2 (Precondition-2 Chunk-Substrate)**:

The gradient view also explains **why "chunks adequate threshold" is not a single event** but is content-specific and varies by body-input. Adequacy for a body state is reached when the chunks for that state have strengthened enough to fire reliably and drive downstream operations (attention, action, feeling).

**For the old "PFC offline" view**:

The gradient verdict **further falsifies** the old view. Under the old view, a baby "gets" object permanence at some discrete age (often cited as 8-9 months Piaget A-not-B). Reality shows permanence sub-chunks compiling gradually from 2.5 to 12+ months. The discrete view was **an artifact of action-based testing** (A-not-B) that set a high threshold for detection. When looking-time methods measure lower-threshold effects, the gradient is visible.

**For F1 methodology**:

Every F1 event classification on the PFC-Inference Ladder (L0-L4) must be understood as **graded within level**. An L2 event can be strong L2 (adult-level reliability) or weak L2 (partial, inconsistent). This is consistent with Property 4 of the ladder (plan §0.7.3) — plausibility-qualified.

### §6.6 — Open sub-questions on the verdict

🟡 **Remaining uncertainty**:

1. **Can strength be measured directly?** Currently, strength is inferred from behavioral outcomes (looking time, reaction intensity). Direct neural measurement of chunk strength in infants is not yet feasible. Future fNIRS + MEG + habituation paradigms may enable this.

2. **Is there a minimum strength for any chunk-like behavior?** Or is the strength continuum truly continuous to zero? This matters for whether "proto-chunks" have a lower bound.

3. **Do chunks ever harden into discrete states?** After sufficient consolidation, a well-compiled chunk may be effectively "discrete" for practical purposes. But this is **late-stage stability**, not the compile mechanism itself being discrete.

4. **Inter-chunk relationships**: if chunks A and B are both graded, their binding (chunk A ↔ B) may have its own gradient. How do composite chunks relate to sub-chunks under the gradient view? Deferred to [09-Event-Chunks-Inference-Matrix.md](../09-Event-Chunks-Inference-Matrix.md).

## §7 — Compile Mechanisms Mapping for Auditory Chunks

> Same 4 compile mechanisms from plan §0.7 + [Chunk.md](../../Body-Base/Schema/Chunk.md) §2. Applied to auditory.

### §7.1 — Repetition (LTP-based)

**Dominant for**: Phoneme categories, word forms, native rhythm, maternal voice, prosodic patterns.

**Evidence**: Every chunks event in §3 is repetition-driven. Native phoneme chunks strengthen with ~months of exposure. Word forms compile with dozens-to-hundreds of exposures. DeCasper 1986 showed ~84 story readings compile a chunk. Saffran 1996 showed 2 minutes of focused exposure compile word-segmentation chunks.

**Framework claim**: Repetition is the **primary compile mechanism for auditory chunks**. Auditory input is naturally dense (continuous stream from birth) and repetition rates are high.

### §7.2 — Emotional peak tagging

**Dominant for**: Mother voice chunk (associated with feeding comfort), first-name chunk (associated with positive attention), fear chunks (associated with loud startling sounds).

**Evidence**: Chunks tagged with high-valence states compile faster and are more durable. Mother voice is prototype case — feeding episodes provide repeated positive-valence context while mother is speaking.

### §7.3 — Multi-modal binding

**Dominant for**: Word-object binds (§3.8), face-voice binds (social smile E12 in 03 §6.1), prosody-emotion binds.

**Evidence**: Bergelson & Swingley 2012 demonstrates rich word-object binds at 6-9mo. The multi-modal binding mechanism creates cross-modality chunks that enable subsequent meaning grounding for language.

### §7.4 — Sleep consolidation

**Dominant for**: Overnight phoneme category reorganization + word form consolidation.

**Evidence**:
- Gomez, Bootzin & Nadel 2006 *Psychological Science* 17:670-674 — infant word learning benefits from nap
- Friedrich, Wilhelm, Born & Friederici 2015 *Nature Communications* 6:6004 — infant memory consolidation during sleep
- Walker 2017 *Why We Sleep* on sleep + memory consolidation

**Framework claim**: Sleep is critical for auditory chunks consolidation because auditory input is dense and needs offline processing to organize. Infant sleep (16 hours/day, REM-dense) is likely optimal for this.

### §7.5 — Interaction of mechanisms

The four mechanisms **interact synergistically** in auditory chunks compilation:
- Repetition (daily phoneme exposure)
- + Emotional tagging (mother speaking while feeding → positive peak)
- + Multi-modal (face + voice + touch binding)
- + Sleep consolidation (nightly reorganization)

= rapid and durable compile of auditory chunks. This is why auditory chunks compile faster than interoceptive chunks (which have lower exposure rate, less multi-modal binding, less emotional peak unless dysregulated). See contrast in [06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md).

## §8 — Multi-modal Binding (Auditory Component) — Convergence Point 3 partial

> Full Convergence Point 3 verdict is in [07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) §6. This file covers auditory-side evidence.

### §8.1 — Auditory + visual (face-voice binding)

Covered in 03 §6.1. Social smile E12 is the first large-scale multi-modal bind. From the auditory side:
- Voice chunks (from prenatal) bind with visual face chunks (compiling postnatally)
- By 6-8 weeks, the face + voice combination is reliably integrated
- Mother voice + mother face → identity chunk (5 months, E15)

🟡 **Framework claim**: Auditory chunks are typically the **first available** for cross-modal binding (since they exist prenatally), making them the anchoring modality for many early multi-modal chunks. Visual chunks then catch up and add the visual dimension.

### §8.2 — Auditory + motor (head turn, articulation)

- Sound localization → head turn (§4.1) is audio-motor bind
- Vocal imitation (8-12 months canonical babbling of native phonemes) is audio-articulatory motor bind
- Name-recognition → turn toward speaker is audio-motor bind via attention

### §8.3 — Auditory + interoceptive (voice-comfort binding)

- Mother voice → calming effect (interoceptive regulation)
- This is one of the earliest multi-modal chunks — already operational at birth (mother voice soothes newborn) because of prenatal compile
- See [06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md](06b-Interoceptive-Other-States.md) for interoceptive-side treatment

### §8.4 — Auditory-as-anchor claim

🟡 **Framework observation**: In typical development, auditory is the **temporally earliest** binding anchor because of prenatal compile. This is an interesting asymmetry vs blind infants (where auditory is still available) or deaf infants (where the anchoring is different). In deaf infants, visual-tactile chunks take the anchoring role, with parallel developmental milestones but different modality timing.

## §9 — Receptive-Productive Asymmetry Preview (Convergence Point 5 receptive side)

> Full formalization of H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md). This section documents the **receptive side evidence** from the auditory arc.

### §9.1 — Receptive milestones (from §3)

By ~12 months, the infant has compiled:
- Own name chunk (from 4.5 months, Mandel 1995)
- Native phoneme categories (by 12 months, Werker & Tees 1984)
- Dozens of word forms + word-object binds (by 6-9 months, Bergelson 2012)
- Word segmentation chunks (from 7.5-8 months, Saffran 1996)
- Prosodic emotional chunks (by 8-9 months)
- Native rhythm preference (from birth, Mehler 1988)

### §9.2 — Productive milestones

By contrast, productive milestones are substantially delayed:

| Receptive event | Age | Productive equivalent | Age | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Own name recognition (Mandel) | 4.5 months | Child produces own name | ~18-24 months | ~15-18 months |
| Common noun comprehension | 6-9 months | First productive words | 10-14 months | ~4-6 months |
| Full phoneme inventory receptive | 12 months | Full phoneme inventory productive | 4-7 years | ~3-6 years |
| Grammar receptive | 18-24 months | Grammar productive | 2-3 years | ~months |
| Understanding "need to pee" (interoceptive) | 14-18mo (L2 "face-freeze") | Produces "need to pee" verbal | 22 months (E23) | ~4-8 months |

### §9.3 — The 13.5 month name gap (Mandel 4.5mo to productive name)

🟢 Own name recognition at 4.5 months (Mandel 1995) vs first productive use of own name ~18 months (widely documented, e.g., Bloom 1973): **13.5 month gap**.

🟡 **Framework interpretation**: The receptive chunk (own name auditory pattern) is compiled at 4.5 months and is operationally sufficient for recognition. The productive side requires:
- Articulatory motor chunks (which for the baby's own disyllabic name at 18mo is substantial motor coordination)
- Self-reference chunks (understanding that "name" refers to self)
- Communicative intention chunks (willingness + ability to use the label socially)
- Matching production-to-reference (producing the name correctly tagged to self-referential context)

The asymmetry is because **production requires additional chunks** beyond receptive. Receptive is already hard (need phonetic + pattern + attention chunks). Production is harder (add motor + self + reference + social chunks). This is H11 in brief — full drill in 08.

### §9.4 — Framework implication (preview)

The auditory arc provides the strongest evidence that **receptive chunks compile substantially before productive chunks** — in some cases (phoneme categories) by years. H11 is not just a language phenomenon — it extends to other body-input systems (interoceptive, motor) as described in 06a + 05 + 08.

**Preview verdict statement**: H11 Receptive-Productive Asymmetry is **supported** by auditory arc evidence. Formal verdict + framework implications in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §5 + [10-F1-Synthesis.md](../10-F1-Synthesis.md).

## §11 — Open Questions

1. **How long is the auditory chunks memory of prenatal exposure?** DeCasper 1986 shows 2-3 days post-birth recall. What about 2 weeks? 2 months? 6 months? Does the chunk fade without reinforcement, or persist?

2. **What is the minimum exposure for a phoneme category to "strengthen enough"?** We know 12 months of exposure is sufficient, but is 6 months? 3 months? Is there a critical period closure, or is it reversible with intensive exposure?

3. **Bilingual infants — how do they track two sets of phoneme categories?** Werker et al. 2009 work on bilingual infants suggests they maintain both sets longer than monolingual infants do for non-native contrasts. This is consistent with the gradient view (both sets compile, neither weakens because both receive exposure).

4. **The Saffran 1996 statistical learning chunks — how durable?** 2 minutes of exposure produces a chunk, but does it last hours? Days? Weeks? Or does it require re-exposure to stay compiled?

5. **Prenatal chunks and adoption**: what happens to maternal voice chunks when an adopted baby is exposed to a different caregiver from birth? Presumably the prenatal chunk fades without reinforcement and is replaced by chunks for the adoptive caregiver's voice. This has not been directly studied.

6. **Own name recognition 4.5 months — individual variation**: do babies with more frequent use of their name show earlier recognition? The compile threshold is presumably exposure-dependent. Individual variation around 4.5 months (some earlier, some later) would support the rate-dependent view.

7. **Deaf infants without cochlear implant**: how does the absence of auditory chunks affect other chunk systems? Feral-child cases (rare, non-experimental) suggest broad downstream effects. Modern early cochlear implantation provides some natural experiments. What does F1 framework predict about the cascading consequences?
