## §0 — Abstract

🟢 **Claim**: The four other interoceptive body states (hunger, pain, thermal, emotional) each traverse variants of the L0→L4 PFC-Inference Ladder at state-specific rates, with **non-uniform progression** across states but a **shared underlying mechanism** (4-channel compile per [06a §6.3](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§6.3)). The non-uniformity is explained by variation in exposure × saliency × caregiver contingency × peak valence parameters per state, not by different architectures.

🟡 **Framework contribution**: Cross-body-state synthesis reveals that (a) bladder is **not uniquely slow** — all interoceptive states lag visual/auditory consistently, (b) each state has its own **critical period for cortisol tagging** with state-specific empirical anchors (pain-trauma, feeding-eating disorders, thermal-SIDS edge case, emotional-attachment dynamics), (c) **thermal arc is truncated** — rarely reaches L4 within F1 window, which is itself diagnostic of compile mechanism limits (low exposure × low salience × caregiver-managed rather than baby-managed), (d) emotional L4 largely falls **outside F1 window** (2-3yr+) — meta-emotional verbalization requires chunks that compile after the F1 time boundary.

## §2 — Hunger arc

### §2.1 — Connect phase (hardware)

**Peripheral and central pathway**:
- **Visceral afferents**: vagal (X) nerve from upper GI + splanchnic (spinal) from lower GI
- **Hormonal signals**: ghrelin (stomach, signals empty stomach), leptin (adipose, signals satiety), insulin, blood glucose levels
- **Hypothalamic integration**: arcuate nucleus (POMC vs AgRP neurons), lateral hypothalamus (feeding drive), paraventricular nucleus
- **Ascending to insula**: hunger signals reach posterior insula via NTS (nucleus of the solitary tract) → thalamus → cortex
- **PFC involvement**: per [01 §4](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md#§4) 5 pillars, PFC can receive hunger signals from birth; what develops is chunks to interpret and act on them

**Developmental status at birth**: Hardware functional. Newborns show the full hunger-to-feeding behavioral program (rooting, sucking) within minutes of birth. Hunger is arguably the **body state with the strongest innate action coupling** at birth — rooting reflex + sucking reflex + cry are pre-wired responses to hunger signals.

**Relative to bladder**: Hunger has **more robust reflex motor output at birth** than bladder. Bladder has only reflex voiding (no approach behavior toward food). Hunger has reflex rooting → sucking → swallowing — a compiled action sequence. This shifts hunger to a slightly different compile trajectory than bladder.

### §2.2 — Stage A: L0-L1 newborn (E1, 0-3 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E1](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e1) 2 weeks):

> 2-week-old newborn, 3 hours since last feeding. Blood sugar dropping, stomach empty, hunger hormone (ghrelin) rising. Baby begins crying — a cry with no distinct enough acoustic features to differentiate from other types of distress.

**Ladder classification**: **L1** (post-hoc cry) + **L0** (reflex rooting-sucking when breast/bottle approaches).

**Key framework observation from E1 Notes**: "Wolff (1969) called this the 'basic cry pattern'. The fact that parents CANNOT differentiate hunger/pain/wet from acoustic alone is evidence that the baby's PFC doesn't yet have chunks to output a differentiated signal."

At this stage:
- Hunger signal ascends to insula; PFC can receive per 5 pillars
- No compiled chunk differentiates hunger-specific pattern from generic distress
- Output is undifferentiated cry (same cry as for wet diaper or thermal discomfort)
- Sub-cortical motor pathway produces acoustic variance across distress types (Wasz-Höckert 1968 — slight pitch/rhythm differences) but this is NOT a PFC chunk, it is reflex motor program variance (see §3.2 parallel for pain)

**Critical distinction**: The acoustic signature variance across distress types at Stage 0 is **evidence that body output is not homogeneous**, but is **NOT evidence that baby has Self-Pattern Match for the distress type**. The variance comes from sub-cortical motor program differences driven by different afferent pathways (metabolic hunger ≠ nociceptive pain at the brainstem level), not from PFC-mediated categorization.

### §2.3 — Differentiated cry emerging (E11 ~3-5 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E11](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e11) 5 months):

> Parents can now distinguish 3-4 types of cry by acoustic alone, without context. Hunger cry: rhythmic, starts soft then builds, has pauses for breath. Pain cry: high intensity from the start, sudden onset, no pause. Tired cry: whiny, drawn-out, lower intensity.

**Ladder classification**: **L1 differentiated** (post-hoc, not yet predictive) + weak **L2** emerging (some babies may show pre-cry anticipatory activation on seeing feeding cues — e.g., quieting on seeing bottle preparation).

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present**: weak differentiation chunks for each distress type ("hunger pattern → cry pattern A", "pain pattern → cry pattern B")
- **Compiling**: weak pre-emptive chunks (sight of bottle → anticipatory quiet) — these are **L2 markers** for hunger
- **Absent**: verbal labels, planned action chunks

**Key research anchor**: Gustafson & Harris 1990 — parental accuracy in identifying distress type from acoustic alone increases from ~30% at newborn to 50-70% at 3-6 months, reaching ~80% with context. **This increase reflects two parallel processes**:
1. Parent learns pattern recognition on their side (compile of parent's chunks for baby's acoustic patterns)
2. Baby's output becomes more differentiated due to baby's chunks emerging

Both processes run in parallel and are hard to separate empirically. Framework-level: the mechanism is **bidirectional compile**, and the observable outcome (parental accuracy improvement) is a joint product.

### §2.4 — L2 pre-emptive feeding response (L2 marker for hunger)

**Observable event**: Baby 4-6 months sees caregiver preparing bottle or approaching breast — baby quiets, turns toward, shows mouth-open anticipation, may stop fussing if previously fussing.

**Ladder classification**: **L2 pattern-match activation** — environmental cues (bottle preparation, caregiver approach in feeding posture) fire the compiled associative chunk before any feeding has occurred.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present**: environmental pattern chunk (visual + motor cues from caregiver → feeding incoming)
- **Present (firing)**: hunger chunk that now has an antecedent prediction ("food is coming")
- **Absent**: verbal labels

**Why this is an L2 marker parallel to bladder face-freeze**: Both are pattern-match events on antecedent signal without full action chunks. The difference is valence — hunger L2 is **positive** (anticipatory quieting, orienting), bladder face-freeze is **neutral-to-negative** (freeze, no action). The framework claim is that the L2 ladder level is **valence-independent** — it is about the ladder structure (recognition without full execution), not the valence of the recognition.

**Distinction from L4**: At Stage L2 the baby cannot request feeding. The baby can recognize "feeding is coming" when cues appear, but cannot initiate feeding through communication. When feeding does not appear, the baby reverts to L1 distress cry.

### §2.5 — L3 crude reach for food (8-12 months)

**Observable event**: Baby 8-12 months reaches toward food on the table, makes "gimme" gestures, may grab at caregiver's food.

**Ladder classification**: **L3 crude response** — action chunks for procuring food are compiling, motor coordination improving (reaching per [05 §2.5](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§2.5) develops 4-6mo, hand-to-mouth by 5-6mo, grabbing by 8-10mo).

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present**: hunger chunk + environmental predictor + motor action chunk (reach/grab)
- **Present**: partial social chunk (caregiver may provide food if requested via gesture)
- **Absent**: verbal labels + coordinated planning

### §2.6 — L4 verbal "hungry" (E21, 18 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E21](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e21) 18 months):

> 18-month baby is playing with a toy. Hunger sensation increases, stomach growling. Baby stops playing, walks to mother and says "hungry" or "eat eat", may point to mouth or belly.

**Ladder classification**: **L4 coordinated execution** — verbal label + planned social action + compound motor response.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢 (strongest confidence for L4 events):
- **Present**: hunger chunk with stable firing
- **Present**: predictive-temporal chunk ("I'm hungry, need to eat")
- **Present**: verbal label chunk ("hungry" / "eat eat")
- **Present**: social communication chunk (caregiver can be addressed)
- **Present**: planned action chunk (go to caregiver, gesture, wait)
- **Present**: verbal articulation motor chunk (see [05 §2.11](05-Motor-Output-Arc.md#§2.11))

Một khi có handle (label 'đói'), bé có thể: 1. Nhớ lại chunk khi không đang trải nghiệm (anticipation), 2. Communicate với người khác về chunk, 3. Plan dựa trên chunk, 4. >
> *English:* "Very important for the framework: language DOES NOT CREATE chunks (chunks already exist from Stage 0 newborn). But language CREATES HANDLES for chunks. Once there is a handle (the label 'hungry'), the baby can: 1. Recall the chunk when not currently experiencing it (anticipation), 2. Communicate with others about the chunk, 3. This is the **Convergence Point 6 primary content** (verbal-label-as-handle mechanism). Chunks exist before labels. Labels are **handles** that enable retrieval, communication, planning, compositionality, and reflection. The distinction is adopted as framework commitment and will be fully formalized in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) §5.

### §2.7 — Hunger H11 gap

**Receptive**: Hunger chunk fires reliably at ~3-5 months (differentiated cry E11) → pre-emptive feeding response 4-6mo → by ~6mo baby reliably "recognizes" hunger state internally
**Productive**: Verbal "hungry" at ~18 months (E21)

**Gap**: ~12-15 months (from reliable receptive ~5mo to productive ~18mo).

This is parallel to [06a](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md) bladder gap (6-10mo) and [04 §9](04-Auditory-System-Arc.md#§9) name gap (13.5mo). The H11 ratio holds: productive chunks bundle is ~3-4× larger than receptive chunks bundle, compile time scales roughly with bundle size, gap ≈ 2-3× receptive compile time.

### §2.8 — Hunger body state at compile: 4-threshold gradient

> **Scope correction (N+4c2-REV1)**: Earlier draft used binary "supportive (low cortisol) / punitive (high cortisol)" framing. Replaced with the framework-correct model from [06a §7.0](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0) — applied to hunger specifically. This section is shorter than 06a §7 because 06a drilled the upstream commitments; 06b applies them.

**Framework inheritance from [06a §7.0](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0)**: (1) Novelty cortisol vs Threat cortisol distinction; (2) Schema = trigger, hormone = sustain; (3) Neural wear mechanism in developing PFC; (4) 3 mechanism × 3 origin taxonomy; (5) Imposed parent as connection + threat dual source; (6) 4 threshold levels.

**Feeding-specific amplification**: Feeding is simultaneously (a) hunger chunk compile window, (b) primary L2 Connection interaction window, (c) primary oxytocin-release context. This means the body state at feeding compile is **especially** dominated by the caregiver-connection profile. The caregiver's body state transfers directly through the feeding interaction via oxytocin, gaze, vocal tone, and physical contact. Hunger chunks compile bundled with this connection context — they are some of the first chunks where interoceptive content + social content are inseparable.

#### §2.8.1 — Level 1: Light (responsive-warm feeding, novelty-dominant)

**Caregiver pattern**: Feed on demand when possible, read infant signals, physical contact during feeding (holding, skin contact), warm vocal tone, no rushing, no forced finishing. Weaning gradual on infant signals.

**Body state at compile**: Novelty cortisol (infant exploring new food textures, tastes — mild cortisol sustaining exploration) + dopamine (salience alert: food approaching — approach signal, NOT reward itself) + opioid (actual reward: comfort from caregiver warmth + satiety) + oxytocin surge (caregiver connection). Schema fire: "hunger signal → caregiver attention → food + warmth → satiation".

**Chunks association compiled**: Hunger chunks bonded with **opioid + oxytocin** — body-internally-cooperative. Adult retrieval: smooth interoceptive awareness of hunger, body responds cooperatively to hunger signal.

**Adult outcome**: Normal relationship to eating, intuitive interoceptive-guided eating, no predisposition to eating disorders.

✅ **Framework verdict**: Level 1 is optimal. This is the state the infant feeding literature (🟡 Satter 1990, 🟡 Hughes & Frazier-Wood 2016 responsive feeding) recommends.

#### §2.8.2 — Level 2: Moderate (mild schedule, novelty-mixed)

**Caregiver pattern**: Some schedule-based feeding (every 3-4 hours regardless of infant signal), occasional mild pressure to finish a portion, mostly calm interactions. No shaming, no forced feeding, no caregiver emotional withdrawal during feeding.

**Body state at compile**: Mostly novelty cortisol but with mild threat-mechanism activation when infant signals are overridden (Anticipation mechanism: "if I don't eat now, I don't eat until next scheduled time" — but this is mild). Mild Social mechanism when caregiver shows mild disapproval. Oxytocin mostly preserved.

**Chunks association compiled**: Primarily opioid-novelty + mild cortisol residue. Adult retrieval: mostly normal, occasional mild disconnect between hunger signal and eating behavior ("I should eat now because it's time" vs "I feel hungry").

**Adult outcome**: Largely normal eating patterns. Some individuals show mild interoceptive-signal dismissal ("I eat on schedule") but no clinical impairment.

✅ **Framework verdict**: Acceptable when Level 1 is impractical (work schedules, daycare, caregiver disability). Not optimal.

#### §2.8.3 — Level 3: Heavy (force feeding, schedule rigidity, shame-based)

**Caregiver pattern**: Strict schedule regardless of infant signals. Force feeding (physical coercion to eat when infant refuses). Shaming appetite ("pig! fat!"). Body-size commentary during feeding. Emotional withdrawal during feeding conflicts. Treating infant refusal as defiance rather than signal.

**Body state at compile**: **Threat cortisol dominant** — Social mechanism (shame + disapproval from primary connection source) + Anticipation mechanism (infant imagines next meal → more conflict → more shame). **Oxytocin drops** — the feeding interaction, which should be the primary L2 connection compile event, becomes a threat context. Schema fire: "hunger signal → feeding conflict → shame + withdrawal".

**Chunks association compiled**: Hunger chunks bonded with **threat cortisol + broken oxytocin signal**. Critically, the same chunks that should later be the adult's reliable hunger-signal interpreter are instead body-state-coded as aversive. The adult literally cannot trust their own hunger signal because the compile context made hunger signaling unsafe.

**Adult outcome** (🟡 correlation evidence, mechanism plausible):
- **Alexithymic eating patterns** — cannot distinguish hunger from other emotional states (Bird & Cook 2013 — alexithymia and eating disorder comorbidity)
- **Emotional eating** — hunger signal co-activated with threat chunks → stress triggers eating response rather than hunger does
- **Eating disorders** — anorexia (hunger suppression learned from feeding-conflict context), binge eating (compensatory response to long-term hunger-signal dismissal)
- **Food anxiety** — orthorexia, picky eating persisting to adulthood, food-context avoidance

**Empirical anchors**:
- 🟡 **Birch 1999** — development of food acceptance patterns, child feeding experience and later eating behavior
- 🟡 **Bird & Cook 2013** — alexithymia-eating disorder comorbidity traceable to interoceptive compile gaps

⚠️ **Framework verdict**: Level 3 produces structural chunk-association damage compounded by compromised L2 connection compile. Recovery requires both chunk re-association and attachment repair. Parents at Level 3 should shift toward Level 1-2.

#### §2.8.4 — Level 4: Extreme (feeding trauma, severe neglect, all mechanisms fire)

**Caregiver pattern**: Severe food scarcity due to neglect, withholding food as punishment, feeding-time violence, force feeding with physical restraint, or feeding in actively fearful context (domestic violence during feeding). Also: medical feeding trauma (NG tube distress, surgical interventions in infancy without comfort).

**Body state at compile**: All 3 threat mechanisms fire (Physical from hunger pain or forced feeding; Social from caregiver context; Anticipation from learned threat pattern). Stage 1 cortisol overload episodes + Stage 2 accumulated wear on developing PFC (per [06a §7.0.3](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0.3)). Amygdala maximally reinforced around feeding context.

**Chunks association compiled**: Hunger chunks are trauma-bound. The adult cannot access normal hunger-signal → eating pathways without re-activating the compiled trauma state.

**Adult outcome**: Severe eating disorders with trauma-linked presentation. Avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder (ARFID). Somatic dysregulation around eating. Update requires trauma-informed professional intervention.

❌ **Framework verdict**: Level 4 requires emergency intervention and long-term therapeutic support.

#### §2.8.5 — Critical period window for hunger

🟡 **Framework hypothesis**: 0-24 months with peak plasticity 0-12 months. Hunger chunks compile earlier than bladder chunks because hunger events occur more frequently (6-10/day vs 10-15 bladder events) and have stronger peak valence (hunger distress is more acute than bladder fullness). Earlier chunks compile during the 0-12 month window when the caregiver-connection component is maximally interleaved with the interoceptive component.

**Why earlier than bladder**: Hunger compile begins Day 1 (E1 hungry cry); bladder compile begins ~6 months. Hunger has 5-6 extra months of compile opportunity before the bladder critical window even opens.

## §3 — Pain arc

### §3.1 — Connect phase (hardware)

**Peripheral pathway**:
- **Nociceptors**: C-fibers (unmyelinated, slow, diffuse burning pain) + A-delta fibers (myelinated, fast, sharp localized pain) in skin, muscle, viscera
- **Spinal integration**: dorsal horn → spinothalamic tract → thalamus → somatosensory cortex (S1/S2) + **insula** + **ACC**
- **Dual pathway**: lateral (sensory-discriminative, localization) + medial (affective-motivational, via ACC)
- **Descending modulation**: PAG (periaqueductal gray) → RVM → spinal cord (pain-inhibiting + facilitating)

**Developmental status at birth**: Per Fitzgerald 2015 and infant pain research, newborn pain pathways are functional but **hyperresponsive** (higher pain sensitivity than adults), with weaker descending inhibition. Myelination + descending control mature over months-years. Cortical pain response (fMRI) observable in infants (Goksan et al. 2015).

**Framework note**: Pain pathways are **less developmentally plastic** than cognitive chunks — the underlying nociceptive machinery is hardware-present at birth. What develops is the **chunks that label + localize + predict** pain, not the pain signal itself.

### §3.2 — Stage A: L0-L1 newborn (E2, 0-3 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E2](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e2) 6 weeks vaccination):

> 6-week-old baby goes for vaccination. Needle breaks skin → nociceptive signal fires up through spinothalamic tract. Baby cries intensely and immediately — high-intensity cry, sudden onset, no "warm-up".

**Ladder classification**: **L0/L1** — reflex pain response (withdrawal + cry).

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- **Present**: innate nociceptor → spinal reflex arc + brainstem cry motor program
- **Absent**: compiled pain chunks (no concept of "pain as category", no localization chunks, no pain-memory chunks, no verbal)

**Key framework observation from E2 Notes**: "The pain cry DOES have a somewhat distinct acoustic signature — higher pitch, sudden onset, no 'warm-up' like a hunger cry. Important framework distinction: acoustic difference ≠ Self-Pattern Match. PFC cannot yet receive it, but body output is already non-uniform."

This parallels §2.2 hunger observation — output differentiation at Stage 0 does NOT imply chunks differentiation. It implies **sub-cortical motor program differences driven by different afferent pathways**.

### §3.3 — L2 marker: Vaccination pre-cry (Pierce & Rodrigues 1996)

**Observable event**: Baby 9-18 months, repeat vaccination or pediatric visit. Baby begins crying **upon entering the clinic** or **seeing the medical provider** — BEFORE any needle touches the skin. In some cases, upon seeing the white coat alone. The cry is not a general anxiety cry but a specific distress that parents report as "she remembered last time".

**Ladder classification**: **L2 pattern-match activation** — environmental chunks (clinic sight + provider uniform + smell of alcohol + position on exam table) fire the compiled associative chunk ("this context predicts pain") before the pain stimulus occurs.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present**: environmental pattern chunk (multi-modal: visual + olfactory + tactile/postural)
- **Present (firing)**: prior pain-memory chunk (indexed to environmental antecedent)
- **Present**: affective valence tag (high, negative — peak pain from prior visit compiled with peak cortisol tagging)
- **Absent**: explicit verbal anticipation + action plan

**Why this is a clean L2 marker** (similar to bladder face-freeze in ladder position but different in affect profile):
1. Known antecedent: specific environmental context
2. Observable response: distress cry before stimulus
3. Chunks-missing signature: no escape attempt (L3 would involve walking away, scrambling out of arms, etc.)
4. PFC online requirement: a chunk is firing on antecedent — requires cortical substrate

**Research anchor**: Pierce & Rodrigues 1996 — one of the earliest documentation of conditioned distress in infants to medical context. Widely confirmed in pediatric pain literature since.

**Difference from bladder face-freeze**: Vaccination pre-cry has active distress (crying) while face-freeze has passive freeze. Why? Because pain chunks are tagged with **peak negative valence** (vaccination is a high-cortisol event). The peak valence tag drives a full distress motor program even though no action chunk is available. Bladder face-freeze has **neutral-to-mild-negative valence** (wet diaper discomfort is unpleasant but not peak), producing the passive freeze instead of active distress.

**Framework note**: The L2 ladder level can produce **different body-level manifestations** depending on affective valence of the firing chunk. Passive stillness (bladder face-freeze, peek-a-boo anticipation, stranger anxiety face pause) at neutral-to-moderate valence; active distress (vaccination pre-cry) at peak negative valence; active excitement (peek-a-boo positive anticipation) at peak positive valence. All are L2 (recognition without full execution) but the body-level trace varies.

### §3.4 — L4 "leg hurts" (E22, 20 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E22](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e22) 20 months):

> 20-month baby trips and falls, knee hits the floor. Baby cries, then (a few seconds later) says "hurt" and points to the knee.

**Ladder classification**: **L4 coordinated execution with body schema integration**.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- **Present**: pain chunk + verbal label ("hurt")
- **Present**: **body schema chunks** — the baby "knows" the pain is at the knee, not somewhere else. This requires spatial self-mapping integration.
- **Present**: pointing action chunk (linked to body schema)
- **Present**: caregiver-communication chunk

**Key framework observation**: E22 requires a **compound chunks stack** that is larger than E21 hunger "hungry" — because pain has a **spatial localization** component that hunger does not. Hunger is a body-general state; pain is a body-specific state. This compound chunks requirement is visible in the ~2-month gap between E21 "hungry" (18mo) and E22 "leg hurts" (20mo).

**Body schema development** is its own arc (not drilled separately in F1 — falls into interoceptive + proprioceptive + visual integration). Research on alexithymia notes that alexithymics often show **deficit in body location identification** — interoceptive accuracy is lower. Body schema chunks compile throughout early childhood and the quality of compile depends on caregiver labeling (naming body parts, body-part songs, physical touch labeling, etc.).

**Language-chunk interaction (Convergence Point 6 preview continued)**: E22 Notes observation:

> "Language rich in body-part terms (Vietnamese has many detailed body part words — knee, elbow, nape, bridge of nose...) creates conditions for deeper body-pattern chunks compared to more generic language. Framework implication: language structure shapes chunks structure. Not because babies 'develop differently biologically', but because handles available in the language determine what can be 'grasped explicitly'."

This is a refinement of the §2.6 "language doesn't create chunks, language creates handles" claim: **language richness determines the granularity of handles available**, which shapes which chunks become explicitly accessible for reflection and communication. A culture with 10 words for specific body parts gives babies 10 handles for 10 chunks that would otherwise remain implicit. This is NOT biological determinism on language (Whorfian strong) but handle-granularity determinism on explicit-access (Whorfian moderate).

### §3.5 — Pain H11 gap

**Receptive**: Pain chunks fire reliably from birth (pain cry E2 at 6 weeks) → environmental pain anticipation 9-18mo (vaccination pre-cry) → pain localization and recognition 18mo+
**Productive**: Verbal "hurt" (generic) ~18mo, with body location "leg hurts" ~20mo

**Gap for pain generic**: Similar to hunger, ~13-14mo from reliable receptive (~5mo differentiation per E11) to productive generic (~18mo).
**Gap for pain localized**: ~15-16mo, adding body schema chunks compile time.

This is consistent with H11 ratio prediction from [06a §8.4](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§8.4) — productive bundle for pain-localized (~5-7 chunks: pain + verbal label + articulation + body schema + spatial map + pointing + caregiver communication) is 3-4× the receptive bundle (~2 chunks: pain detection + environmental context).

### §3.6 — Pain body state at compile: 4-threshold gradient

> **Scope correction (N+4c2-REV1)**: Earlier binary "supportive/punitive" framing replaced with framework-correct model per [06a §7.0](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0). Framework inheritance same as §2.8.

**Pain-specific distinction**: Pain is the one interoceptive state where the **Physical mechanism** (§7.0.4) is primary — nociceptor activation is hardwired and bypasses chunks-level processing for the first few hundred milliseconds. This means pain events have endorphin buffer built in (descending analgesia + opioid release at nociceptor level). The endorphin partially counters whatever cortisol the event produces.

**Pain compile architecture**: Unlike hunger (continuous chunk compile from many small events) or bladder (~15 events/day), pain compile happens in **rare peak events** (immunization, injuries, medical procedures). Each pain event has very high peak valence (Physical mechanism strongest of the 3) but low frequency. This is a **flashbulb compile pattern** — few events compile very strongly per-event, dominated by the body state at the moment of the pain event.

#### §3.6.1 — Level 1: Light (buffered pain, caregiver-mediated)

**Caregiver pattern**: When pain event occurs (fall, injury, medical procedure), caregiver responds quickly with physical comfort (holding, rocking), labels the event ("Does it hurt? I know, that hurts a lot"), validates the expression, does not minimize or shame, does not amplify into caregiver panic. Medical procedures are prepared for (explanation at child's level, comfort hold position, distraction, reassurance).

**Body state at compile**: Pain peaks → endorphin surge (built-in buffer) + amygdala activation + cortisol sustain. Then immediately: caregiver warmth → oxytocin + mild opioid → partial resolution. Schema fire: "pain signal → caregiver arrives → comfort comes → pain resolves". The compile is simultaneously "pain hurts" and "pain is recoverable with help".

**Chunks association compiled**: Pain chunks bonded with **resolvable + connection-supported**. Adult retrieval: normal pain sensitivity, appropriate help-seeking when injured, no persistent pain-related anxiety, no pain catastrophizing.

✅ **Framework verdict**: Level 1 is optimal for pain chunks. This model is supported by 🟢 Cohen et al. 2008 systematic reviews of pediatric procedural pain management — caregiver-mediated comfort during procedures predicts normal adult pain response.

#### §3.6.2 — Level 2: Moderate (routine care, some dismissal)

**Caregiver pattern**: Caregiver responds to pain but with mild dismissal ("it's just a scratch, you're ok"), rushes through comfort, minimal labeling. Medical procedures handled with minimal preparation. No shaming but limited active comfort.

**Body state at compile**: Pain chunks get mixed signal — Physical mechanism buffered by endorphin as usual, but the Social mechanism component (caregiver response) is weak. Schema fire: "pain signal → minimal support → resolve on my own".

**Chunks association compiled**: Primarily neutral-pain chunks with mild association to self-resolution pattern rather than help-seeking.

**Adult outcome**: Often presents as stoic pain tolerance with occasional help-seeking gaps. May underreport pain to doctors. Not clinically impaired but not optimal.

✅ **Framework verdict**: Acceptable, though Level 1 is preferable.

#### §3.6.3 — Level 3: Heavy (dismissal + shaming)

**Caregiver pattern**: Active dismissal ("don't cry, it's nothing"), shaming ("big babies don't cry for that", "stop being dramatic"), or neglect (pain events not responded to at all). Medical procedures handled without comfort, without preparation, caregiver absent or emotionally distant during procedure.

**Body state at compile**: Physical mechanism still fires (endorphin present) but the Social mechanism adds a **shame overlay** — the pain itself is the problem AND the pain expression is the problem. Anticipation mechanism compounds: "next pain event → same dismissal → I am alone with this". Oxytocin drops (connection source refuses to be connection during pain). Schema fire: "pain signal → I am alone + I am bad for hurting".

**Chunks association compiled**: Pain chunks compile with **two separate association loads**:
1. **Normal pain interoception** — still compiled via Physical mechanism
2. **Layer of "pain expression = shame"** — compiled via Social mechanism

These two layers produce the adult pattern of simultaneous pain-experience + pain-suppression-of-expression. The adult knows they are in pain (Layer 1 intact) but cannot express it (Layer 2 interferes).

**Adult outcome** (🟡 correlation evidence, mechanism plausible):
- **Alexithymic pain** — cannot verbalize pain, goes to doctor with vague somatic complaints
- **Pain dismissal tendency** — adult dismisses own pain (learned self-dismissal from early other-dismissal)
- **Chronic pain** susceptibility — accumulated undischarged pain events + suppressed expression contribute to chronic pain syndromes (🟢 Anand & Craig 1996 show neonatal pain handling predicts later pain response patterns)
- **Somatic symptom disorders** — body signals interpreted as threat + inability to express → symptoms present as physical complaints without clear medical cause

⚠️ **Framework verdict**: Level 3 produces a specific alexithymic-pain adult profile that is hard to identify because Layer 1 is intact — the person reports pain accurately but cannot describe it well.

#### §3.6.4 — Level 4: Extreme (medical trauma, repeated procedures without comfort)

**Caregiver pattern**: NICU graduates with prolonged painful procedures without consistent comfort; chronic illness in infancy with repeated surgeries/interventions; birth trauma with sustained distress; circumcision or other painful procedures without anesthesia (🟡 Taddio et al. 1997 — NEJM study: boys circumcised without anesthesia showed elevated pain response to later vaccinations compared to anesthetized controls, suggesting pain memory compiles with the original threat context). Also: physical abuse creating repeated pain events in a threat context.

**Body state at compile**: Pain chunks compile during peak Physical + Social + Anticipation activation simultaneously. The endorphin buffer is overwhelmed by repeated events. Developing pain-modulation systems (descending inhibition, periaqueductal gray) may be shaped by this chronic compile context. Stage 2 accumulated PFC wear on the regulatory circuits that would later modulate pain perception.

**Chunks association compiled**: Pain chunks bound with trauma context. Pain hypersensitization possible (chronic pain amplification) or dissociative numbing possible (depends on individual and event profile).

**Adult outcome**: Chronic pain syndromes with high comorbidity to PTSD. Complex Regional Pain Syndrome risk. Fibromyalgia-type presentations. Somatic symptom disorders. Recovery requires trauma-informed pain management (not just analgesia).

**Empirical anchors**:
- 🟢 **Anand & Craig 1996** — neonatal pain response and later development
- 🟢 **Fitzgerald 2015** — infant pain neurophysiology
- 🟢 **Goksan et al. 2015** — fMRI cortical pain processing in infants
- 🟡 **Taddio et al. 1997** — NEJM circumcision study (pain memory compile)

❌ **Framework verdict**: Level 4 produces structural pain-modulation system damage requiring specialized trauma-informed intervention.

#### §3.6.5 — Critical period window for pain

🟡 **Framework hypothesis**: Pain critical period is **broader and less concentrated** than hunger or bladder because peak pain events occur sporadically throughout childhood, not just in a narrow compile window. The 0-24mo window has the strongest foundational effect because the pain-modulation systems are still compiling, but subsequent painful childhood events continue to contribute to the compile. This gives pain a **dose-response profile** where cumulative pain experience + cumulative response quality over 0-5yr matters more than any single window.

**Framework distinction from bladder/hunger**: Bladder has a tight 12-30mo window because bladder chunks compile only after Stage B threshold. Pain has no equivalent threshold — pain chunks can compile from Day 1 and continue indefinitely. This makes pain chunks more dose-dependent and less critical-period-dependent than the other states.

## §4 — Thermal arc (truncated)

### §4.1 — Connect phase (hardware)

**Peripheral pathway**:
- **Thermoreceptors**: cold-sensitive (TRPM8) + warm-sensitive (TRPV1/TRPV3/TRPV4) in skin and some viscera
- **Core thermoreception**: hypothalamic preoptic area (POA) neurons sensitive to core temperature
- **Ascending pathway**: spinothalamic → thalamus → somatosensory cortex + **insula** (thermal interoception hub per Craig 2002)
- **Output pathways**: POA → autonomic (vasodilation, sweating, shivering) + behavioral (seeking warmer/cooler environment)

**Developmental status at birth**: Newborns are **thermoregulatory-challenged** due to high surface-to-volume ratio, immature autonomic control, and limited behavioral options. They rely heavily on caregiver thermoregulation (swaddling, clothing, ambient temperature management).

### §4.2 — Stage A: L0-L1 newborn (E4)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E4](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e4) 1 month thermal cry):

> 1-month-old baby, room temperature 32°C, covered with a thick blanket. Body temperature rising slightly, sweating at neck and head, whole-body thermoregulatory discomfort.

**Ladder classification**: **L0/L1** — autonomic response (sweating) + crude distress signal (crying, fussiness).

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present**: hardwired thermoregulatory autonomic response
- **Absent**: compiled thermal chunks (no concept of "cold"/"hot" as category)
- **Output**: generic fussiness or cry, acoustically similar to other discomfort states (per §2.2 E1 observation pattern)

### §4.3 — **Critical framework observation: SIDS as edge case**

From [Feel-Example E4 Notes](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e4) (verbatim, framework-critical):

> "This is a **dangerous edge case** worth noting for the framework. SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome) is strongly associated with overheating (Mitchell et al. 1993; Ponsonby et al. 1993). The baby does NOT 'know' to wake up and escape thermal stress, because there is no Self-Pattern Match for temperature yet. The distress signal may not be strong enough to overcome sleep state. This is a concrete illustration of the cost of lacking Self-Pattern Match — the body can sustain damage that the PFC cannot receive."

**Framework-critical implication**: Thermal is a case where **lack of Precondition-2 adequacy has mortal consequences**. The hardware is intact (thermoreceptors fire, POA signals distress), the PFC is online (per 5 pillars), but without a compiled chunk tying "thermal distress → wake up → escape action", the baby cannot act on the signal. In the specific case of overheating during sleep, this Precondition-2 inadequacy can be fatal.

**Contrast with adult**: Adult has compiled chunks "I feel too hot → wake up → adjust blankets or temperature". This chunks stack is absent in newborns, present by Stage C toddlers (~14-18mo when thermal-discomfort wriggling emerges), but during the gap window newborns are dependent on caregiver thermoregulation.

**This is NOT "PFC offline"** (the old reframe-rejected view). It IS "PFC online but chunks for thermal-escape action absent". Which is why the framework commitment of [01](01-PFC-Hardware-Reframe.md) hardware-vs-content distinction matters practically — SIDS prevention guidelines (back-to-sleep, no heavy blankets, cool room) are effectively **external scaffolding for missing thermal-escape chunks**, not substitutes for a "developing PFC".

### §4.4 — L3 partial: wriggling / postural adjustment (12-18 months)

**Observable event**: Baby 14-18 months pulls off blankets, kicks off socks when hot, curls up when cold, may seek warmer/cooler spots on own (crawling away from vent, toward parent's body).

**Ladder classification**: **L3 crude response** — motor action chunks for thermal regulation compiling, caregiver still managing primary thermoregulation.

**Chunks state inference** 🟡:
- **Present**: thermal distress chunk + crude action chunks (pull off, seek warmth)
- **Absent**: verbal labels for most children at this age
- **Absent**: explicit anticipation (baby doesn't "predict" that outside cold will require jacket)

### §4.5 — L4 partial within F1 window: limited

**Observable event**: Some babies at 18-24 months may say "cold" or "hot" in clear contexts but thermal verbalization is **less prioritized** than bladder/hunger/pain verbalization. A baby who says "hungry" and "need to pee" may not yet say "cold" at the same age.

**Framework claim**: Thermal **rarely reaches full L4 within the F1 window (0-24mo)**. When it does, it is partial (simple labels "cold"/"hot") without the planning-action sophistication of E21 "hungry" or E23 "need to pee".

### §4.6 — Why thermal ladder is truncated in early years

**Mechanism explanation** via the 4-channel compile + exposure × saliency × contingency × valence formula from [06a §6.3](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§6.3):

1. **Exposure**: Moderate. Thermal events occur several times per day (diaper change, clothes change, going outside). Moderate rate.
2. **Saliency per event**: LOW. Most thermal events are sub-threshold (minor cold from diaper change, minor warm from blanket). Only extreme thermal events (overheating, cold exposure) produce high-salience signals.
3. **Caregiver contingency**: LOW. Caregivers manage thermal PROACTIVELY (dressing, ambient temperature control) — so baby rarely gets the antecedent-consequence pairing that other states provide. Caregiver handles the thermal before baby's signal fires; baby rarely experiences "signal → distress → caregiver response" because caregiver intervention pre-empts the signal.
4. **Peak valence**: LOW to moderate. Thermal discomfort is unpleasant but rarely peak (bladder discomfort is worse; pain is much worse).

All four parameters are weaker for thermal than for bladder, hunger, or pain. The 4-channel compile mechanism produces slower chunks accumulation, and the ladder traversal is correspondingly slower. By 24 months, thermal is typically at L3 (crude response) without reaching L4 coordinated.

**Framework diagnostic**: The thermal truncation is NOT a counter-example to the compile mechanism — it is **predicted by the mechanism**. The formula says compile rate depends on exposure × saliency × contingency × valence. Lower parameters → slower compile → truncated ladder traversal within a given time window. Thermal is the cleanest case where all four parameters are reduced.

### §4.7 — Thermal body state at compile (truncated compile, edge-case dominant)

> **Scope correction (N+4c2-REV1)**: Earlier "thermal cortisol tagging" framing replaced per [06a §7.0](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0).

**Thermal distinguishing feature**: Per §4.6, thermal compile is **truncated** in the F1 window — most babies do not reach L4 by 24mo because compile-rate parameters (exposure × saliency × contingency × peak valence) are all low. This means the 4-threshold gradient applied to thermal is **dominated by caregiver-managed defaults** (Level 1 by default for most babies) with rare edge cases where Level 3/4 applies.

**Default case (most babies)**: Caregivers manage thermal environment (swaddling, clothing, ambient temperature, response to fussiness). Thermal chunks compile under mostly Level 1 body state with novelty cortisol from normal thermal events (feeling cool at bath time, feeling warm under blanket). The chunks remain at L2-L3 within F1 window (full L4 compile happens 2-5yr).

**Edge case 1 — NICU graduates and medical thermal events** (Level 3-4 profile):
Prolonged incubator context with repeated thermal management procedures + medical trauma combined. Body state: Physical mechanism (thermal distress without caregiver body contact buffer) + Anticipation (infant learns "thermal signal → procedure → distress"). Thermal chunks compile bound with medical-trauma body state.

Adult outcome (🟡 clinical observation): NICU graduates show higher rates of thermal-sensation anxiety, tactile defensiveness including thermal component, and sometimes phobic responses to medical thermal contexts.

**Edge case 2 — Severe neglect or homelessness** (Level 4 profile):
Persistent cold exposure without resolution (severe neglect contexts, refugee/displaced family situations, homelessness with inadequate shelter for infants). Body state: Physical mechanism (sustained thermal distress) + absence of caregiver resolution + Anticipation (no expectation of relief). Thermal chunks compile with **no resolution template** — the pattern "thermal signal → resolved" does not form.

Adult outcome (🟡 clinical observation): Trauma-linked thermal hypervigilance, persistent cold-weather anxiety disproportionate to actual thermal stress, possible dissociative responses to cold contexts.

**Edge case 3 — SIDS mortal consequence** (per §4.3):

This is the **framework-critical edge case**. SIDS (🟢 Mitchell et al. 1993, Ponsonby et al. 1993) correlates with overheating during sleep. The mechanism is not that cortisol tags thermal chunks — it is that thermal distress does not compile into actionable chunks in the first place (compile rate too low for Stage B threshold), so the infant lacks any self-signal to wake up and escape thermal stress. This is **chunks-absent failure**, not **chunks-mistagged failure** — a distinct mechanism from the other edge cases.

**Framework verdict**: Most thermal compile is at Level 1-2 (caregiver-managed default) with thermal chunks not reaching L4 within F1 window. Edge cases at Level 3-4 produce trauma-linked thermal chunks. The SIDS edge case is structurally different and is the primary framework-critical observation from the thermal arc.

### §4.8 — Thermal summary table

| Stage | Age | Event | Ladder | Chunks state |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | 0-3mo | E4 newborn thermal cry | L0/L1 | Reflex + autonomic |
| B | 3-12mo | Minor fussiness at thermal events | L1 | Weak differentiation |
| (B→C transition) | 12-18mo | Wriggling, pull blanket off | L3 | Partial action chunks |
| C-partial | 18-24mo | Some verbal "cold"/"hot" in context | Partial L4 | Simple label, not planning |

## §5 — Emotional arc

### §5.1 — Scope note

Emotional interoception is **multi-channel by definition** — limbic + amygdala + insula + mPFC + ACC all contribute. Unlike bladder/hunger/pain/thermal which have clean peripheral afferent pathways, emotional states arise from complex network activity. This makes the "interoceptive" framing only partial — emotion is interoceptive-and-more.

This section covers emotional chunks **from the interoceptive angle**: how baby's own emotional states become observable (Feel-Consciousification+) and labeled (Feel-Labeling–Feel-Explanation). Social-side of emotional chunks (recognizing caregiver emotion, social smile, cry contagion, empathy giving) is covered in [07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) — 06b crosses with 07 at E12 (social smile) and E26 (egocentric empathy).

### §5.2 — Stage A: L0-L1 newborn emotional states

**E5 quiet alert state** ([Feel-Example E5](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e5) 2 weeks):

> 2-week-old baby, just finished feeding, clean diaper, appropriate temperature, being held against mother's chest. Eyes open, pupils moderately dilated, tracking mother's face with short latency.

**Ladder classification**: **L0** (absence of distress, not positive felt sense).

**Critical framework observation from E5 Notes**:

> "Chunk(s): There is no 'comfort' chunk in the sense of positive observation. This state is defined by the ABSENCE of distress, not yet by a felt sense of well-being. In other words: the baby does not yet 'feel comfortable' — the baby 'does not feel uncomfortable', a small but important distinction for the framework."

This is a **framework-critical distinction**: at Stage 0, positive emotional states are **absence of negative signals**, not compiled positive chunks. The first positive emotional chunks compile later, via multi-modal binding during positive caregiver interaction (E12 social smile onwards).

**Brazelton 1973 significance**: "quiet alert state" is the **window for seed chunks formation** — PFC receives visual input (mother's face) in low-noise context. Quality of caregiving is quantified partially by quiet-alert-time percentage. Normal babies ~10% of wake time; colicky babies <2%; NICU babies even lower. This is the **opportunity rate** for positive emotional chunks to compile.

### §5.3 — E11 differentiated emotional states (3-5 months)

**Observable event**: Baby 3-5 months shows differentiated facial expressions for distress vs interest vs disgust vs mild positive. Cry differentiation (covered in §2.3 for hunger) parallels emotional differentiation.

**Ladder classification**: **L1 differentiated** emotional states.

**Chunks state inference**: Early emotional categorization chunks compiling ("this body state → this motor expression"). Still largely sub-cortical motor program variance (per E1/E2 observation pattern). PFC reception increasing but without verbal access.

### §5.4 — E12 social smile as L2 emotional+social binding (6-8 weeks)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E12](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e12) 7 weeks):

> 7-week-old baby looking at mother's face from ~30cm distance. Mother has a warm expression, speaking gently. Baby begins smiling — a genuine social smile, outward-directed, with engagement.

**Ladder classification**: **L2** (pattern-match on caregiver face + voice context → smile motor output).

**Framework significance**: E12 is the **first observable positive multi-modal chunk** in F1 timeline. It is the **primary anchor for Convergence Point 3 (multi-modal binding mechanism)** — full drill in [07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) §3.3.

**Why social smile is emotional-interoceptive and not purely social**: The smile chunk fires **only when baby is in calm state baseline** (no overwhelming interoceptive distress). A hungry, wet, cold, or over-tired baby does not produce social smile to the same face + voice stimulus. The **interoceptive calm baseline is a precondition** for the social smile chunk to fire. This is direct evidence that E12 couples emotional-interoceptive state with social-visual-auditory pattern-match.

**Cross-reference**: See [07 §3.3](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md#§3.3) for full multi-modal binding mechanism analysis. This section notes only the interoceptive-coupling aspect.

### §5.5 — E26 egocentric empathy (18 months)

**Observable event** ([Feel-Example E26](../../Body-Base/Feeling/Feel-Example-Draft.md#e26) 18 months):

> 18-month baby is playing near mother. Mother begins crying. Baby stops playing, walks to her own room, fetches her own stuffed animal or comfort blanket, brings it over and offers it to mother.

**Ladder classification**: **L4 compound chunks** — self-emotional chunks used as template for social action.

**Chunks state inference** 🟢:
- **Present**: own distress chunk (fired via Resonance from mother's distress — Martin & Clark 1982 cry contagion → Hoffman 2000 egocentric empathy stage)
- **Present**: own comfort template ("when I'm distressed, my bear helps me")
- **Present**: cross-agent application chunk (applying own template to another)
- **Present**: motor action chunks (retrieve bear, carry to mother)
- **Present**: caregiver-recognition chunk (this is mother; mother is distressed)
- **Absent (critical)**: theory-of-mind sophistication — baby does NOT yet recognize that mother's needs may differ from own needs. Mother doesn't actually need a child's bear; baby applies own template.

**Framework significance** (E26 Notes):

> "Hoffman (2000) calls this 'egocentric empathy' — the baby uses her OWN comfort template and applies it to mother, without yet realizing that mother may need something different."

**Why this is interoceptive**: The chain begins with baby's own emotional-interoceptive chunk (own distress state when comforted by bear). The chunk's CONTENT is internal body state. The social application is the direction of output, but the chunk being applied is an interoceptive chunk.

This is the clearest F1-window evidence of **self-emotional chunks as computational substrates** — they can be used not just for own-state recognition but also for **simulating other agents' states** (proto-theory-of-mind). The 18-month timing is consistent with the progression from Stage 0 quiet alert (own absence of distress) → Stage L2 social smile (own positive state bound to social stimulus) → Stage L4 egocentric empathy (own emotional chunks used as simulation templates for others).

### §5.6 — Verbal emotional labels mostly post-F1

**Observable pattern**: Verbal productive emotional labels ("happy", "sad", "scared", "angry") emerge largely **after 2 years**, often 2-3 years for clear production with correct contextual use. Within F1 window (0-24mo), verbal emotional vocabulary is typically **limited to 1-2 labels** ("happy" or "scared" in simple contexts).

**Framework explanation**: Emotional chunks are compile-rich but **verbal-label-attachment is slower** than for bladder/hunger/pain because:
1. Caregivers label emotional states **less explicitly** than physical states ("are you tired?" is more common than "are you sad right now?")
2. Emotional states have **lower objective markers** — parents often cannot identify which emotion a baby is experiencing, reducing contingent labeling
3. Emotional vocabulary is **more abstract** — "happy" has less concrete referent than "hungry"

Full emotional verbal development extends into F3 Chunk-External-Development (language acquisition) and is primarily covered in [08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md](08-Verbal-Production-Arc.md) + F3 later.

### §5.7 — Emotional H11 parallel

**Receptive-side** (reading caregiver emotion): Present very early. Martin & Clark 1982 cry contagion at newborn age — baby's own distress chunks fire in response to other baby's cry. Social referencing 9-12 months — baby checks mother's face for emotional cue about novel object. Still-face paradigm from 6 months — baby detects caregiver emotional absence.

**Productive side**: Verbal emotional labels mostly post-F1 (2-3 years). Non-verbal emotional expressions (facial, vocal, motor) compile earlier and are continuous from E12 (6-8wk).

**Gap**: Receptive reading of caregiver emotion appears at ~weeks old (cry contagion newborn); productive verbal emotional labels at ~2-3 years. **Gap ≈ 24-30 months** — the **largest H11 gap** among all F1 body states.

**Why so large**: Emotional chunks bundle for production is very large:
1. Emotional state chunk
2. Emotional discrimination chunk (distinguishing between own states)
3. Verbal label chunk (abstract, lower-concrete-referent than physical)
4. Articulation motor chunk
5. Social-context chunk (when to report emotion, to whom)
6. Meta-emotional chunk (recognizing own state while in it, not after)

vs receptive bundle:
1. Emotional pattern recognition chunk
2. Optional: context chunk (who is expressing)

Ratio ~3-4 × matches the H11 prediction, consistent with the overall H11 mechanism claim.

### §5.8 — Emotional body state at compile — ⭐ the primary case for imposed-parent dual-source mechanism

> **Scope correction + expansion (N+4c2-REV1)**: Earlier "cortisol tagging" framing replaced. More importantly, §5.8 is the section where the framework's **most critical insight** — imposed parent = connection source + threat source simultaneously, from [06a §7.0.6](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0.6) — applies most cleanly. The attachment literature is empirically well-developed precisely because this mechanism produces the strongest and most persistent developmental effects.

**Why emotional is the uniquely strong case**: Bladder/hunger/pain/thermal all have a separable body-input component (mechanical, nociceptive, thermoreceptive) that exists independently of the caregiver. Emotional interoception is different — the infant's emotional regulation substrate is **built via interaction with the caregiver's regulation**. There is no pre-caregiver baseline for emotional chunks to compile against; the caregiver **is** the regulation infrastructure during the compile window.

This makes the compile body state almost entirely determined by caregiver response patterns during the 0-12mo window. Unlike bladder training where the bladder chunks exist on their own substrate and parents shape the tagging, emotional chunks **do not have an independent substrate** — the compile is co-regulatory from the start.

#### §5.8.1 — Level 1: Secure attachment (Bowlby/Ainsworth Type B)

**Caregiver pattern**: Contingent response to distress. Mirroring + labeling ("I know you're sad, mom is here"). Physical presence during comfort. Consistent availability. "Secure base" (Ainsworth) — caregiver is reliable return point from exploration. Fonagy's mentalizing stance: caregiver treats baby as having a mind with intentions and feelings.

**Body state at compile**: Distress arises → amygdala + limbic activation → infant signals → caregiver arrives → co-regulation begins (physical contact + oxytocin release + vocal prosody mirroring) → distress resolves with caregiver co-present. Cortisol spikes but resolves cleanly because resolution is reliable. Schema fire during compile: "emotional signal → caregiver comes → co-regulation → resolution".

**Chunks association compiled**: Emotional chunks bonded with **secure-base template**. Key architectural features of compiled chunks:
- **Distress is recoverable** — there is a reliable path from dysregulation to regulation
- **Connection seeks dissolve distress** — help-seeking is an effective strategy
- **Own emotions are legible** — another mind treats them as understandable
- **Self can be regulated** — the infant experiences successful co-regulation repeatedly, compiling into the expectation of regulation

**Adult outcome** (🟢 strong longitudinal evidence): Normal emotional regulation capacity, capacity to tolerate negative emotions without dissociation or acting out, capacity to seek comfort from others without shame, capacity to form secure adult attachments, lower rates of all mental health disorders.

**Empirical anchor**: 🟢 Minnesota longitudinal study (Sroufe, Egeland, Carlson & Collins 2005) — infant attachment classification at 12-18mo predicts broad patterns of adult relational and emotional functioning 30 years later.

✅ **Framework verdict**: Level 1 is the optimal emotional compile context. This is the strongest evidence base in all of F1 for the framework's claim that body state at compile matters more than any cognitive teaching later.

#### §5.8.2 — Level 2: Insecure-avoidant attachment (Type A)

**Caregiver pattern**: Caregiver present but emotionally distant. Responds to physical needs but minimizes emotional expressions ("don't cry about that", "you're fine"). Mild emotional withdrawal during distress. No outright shame or anger. Often a culturally-learned pattern where caregivers genuinely believe they are teaching independence by not responding to distress.

**Body state at compile**: Distress arises → infant signals → caregiver response is partial (physical only, no co-regulation) → infant experiences unresolved distress → learns to suppress the signal because signaling doesn't produce resolution. Social mechanism activates mildly (rejection of expression) without outright threat. Oxytocin compiles weakly.

**Chunks association compiled**: Emotional chunks bonded with **suppress-and-self-soothe template**. Key features:
- **Distress is not effectively expressible** — expression doesn't produce caregiver co-regulation
- **Solo regulation is the default path** — infant compiles self-soothing strategies without external reference
- **Connection and emotional expression are decoupled** — connection can happen but must avoid emotional content

**Adult outcome** (🟢 evidence): Avoidant attachment style in adult relationships. **Alexithymia correlation** — difficulty identifying own emotions because the compile context did not include external mirror labels. Tendency toward emotional self-reliance that can read as strength but limits intimacy. Higher rates of somatization (emotions that cannot be expressed express through body symptoms).

**Framework verdict**: ⚠️ Level 2 produces functional but emotionally-constricted adult pattern. Adult repair is possible (therapy provides the missing mirroring + labeling component) but slow because the foundational architecture was compiled without it.

#### §5.8.3 — Level 3: Insecure-ambivalent / anxious attachment (Type C)

**Caregiver pattern**: Inconsistent response — sometimes warm and responsive, sometimes withdrawn or overwhelmed. The inconsistency is often not the caregiver's choice (maternal depression, economic stress, unprocessed caregiver trauma, illness) but the effect on the infant is the same. Caregiver emotional availability is unpredictable.

**Body state at compile**: Distress arises → uncertainty about whether signaling will produce help → infant escalates signal to increase chance of response → caregiver response depends on caregiver state, not infant signal → partial or unreliable resolution. Cortisol spikes and often does not resolve cleanly (because resolution was not reliable). The compile context has **chronic partial cortisol** + **hypervigilance for caregiver state**.

**Chunks association compiled**: Emotional chunks bonded with **hypervigilant-uncertain template**. Key features:
- **Distress is sometimes recoverable, sometimes not** — infant cannot predict which
- **Hypervigilance for caregiver state** is the survival strategy — infant monitors caregiver emotion more than own emotion
- **Own emotional signals become escalated** — high-amplitude signaling compiles because reliable low-amplitude signaling doesn't work
- **Oxytocin association is intermittent** — connection happens sometimes but is not a reliable regulator

**Adult outcome** (🟢 evidence): Anxious attachment style. **Hypervigilance for others' emotions** (well-developed via compile context) combined with **low tolerance for own emotions** (they are escalated signals compiled during unreliable co-regulation). Higher rates of anxiety disorders, relationship anxiety, rumination, emotional escalation cycles.

**Framework verdict**: ⚠️ Level 3 produces an adult with specific strengths (interpersonal emotion-reading) alongside specific vulnerabilities (own-emotion regulation). Adult repair is possible via consistent therapeutic relationship providing the missing reliability component.

#### §5.8.4 — Level 4: Disorganized attachment (Type D)

**Caregiver pattern**: Caregiver is **simultaneously source of comfort and source of fear**. This happens when the caregiver is actively threatening (abuse, violence) or when the caregiver is frightened or dissociated themselves (caregiver trauma, severe mental illness, active substance abuse during caregiving). The infant faces an **impossible situation** — the attachment system demands approach toward caregiver for comfort, but the threat system demands withdrawal from caregiver for safety.

**Body state at compile**: This is the primary case for [06a §7.0.6](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0.6) "imposed parent = connection source + threat source simultaneously". The attachment system and threat system fire in direct conflict with no resolution pathway. Oxytocin and cortisol activate in opposition. Physical + Social + Anticipation mechanisms all fire (child experiences physical danger if abusive caregiver, social shame/disconnect, and Anticipation of next episode). Cortisol stays chronically elevated because no resolution is available.

**Chunks association compiled**: The emotional chunks compile with **contradictory tagging**. The same caregiver-presence signal is bound to both comfort-seeking AND threat-avoidance chunks simultaneously. This is structurally different from Level 1-3 because the chunks themselves are internally incoherent, not just negatively tagged.

**Plus neural wear dimension** ([06a §7.0.3](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.0.3)): Chronic high cortisol + no resolution opportunity + developing PFC substrate = maximal Stage 2 accumulated wear during the window when the emotional regulation circuits are being compiled. The PFC regulatory circuits that would normally develop during this window are actively shaped by ongoing threat context.

**Adult outcome** (🟢 strong evidence, clinically significant):
- **Disorganized attachment pattern** in adult relationships — simultaneous desire for and terror of closeness
- **Dissociative responses** during emotional events (the internal contradiction resolved via dissociation)
- **Complex PTSD** symptoms — persistent hyperarousal + relational instability + identity disturbance + affect dysregulation
- **Highest rates** of severe mental health outcomes in the attachment literature: borderline personality patterns, severe depression, substance abuse, suicidality
- **Somatic manifestations** extensively documented (🟢 van der Kolk 2014 *The Body Keeps the Score* — trauma-chunks persistence into adult physiology)

**Empirical anchors**:
- 🟢 **Bowlby** (1969, 1973, 1980) — foundational attachment framework
- 🟢 **Ainsworth et al. 1978** Strange Situation — three attachment patterns + disorganized added later (Main & Solomon 1986)
- 🟢 **Sroufe, Egeland, Carlson & Collins 2005** — Minnesota longitudinal study
- 🟢 **Fonagy et al. 2002** — mentalizing and attachment
- 🟢 **van der Kolk 2014** — trauma persistence in body
- 🟢 **Schore 2003** — right-brain development and attachment

❌ **Framework verdict**: Level 4 produces the most severe developmental compile damage in the framework. Recovery requires trauma-informed professional intervention, often over years. The chunks themselves are internally conflicted, not just negatively tagged — repair involves not just re-associating chunks but reintegrating contradictory structures.

#### §5.8.5 — Critical period window: 0-12 months, with lifelong plasticity reduced after

🟡 **Framework hypothesis**: The 0-12mo window is **uniquely critical** for emotional chunks because:

1. **The substrate is being compiled** — not just the chunks. Limbic-PFC regulatory circuits are developing their baseline architecture during this window (🟢 Schore 2003).
2. **The caregiver is not just a source of events to compile against — the caregiver IS the regulation infrastructure**. There is no alternative substrate for emotional compile until the infant develops the capacity to mentalize and self-regulate, which itself depends on this compile window.
3. **The neural wear dimension is maximally active**. Chronic Level 4 compile context during 0-12mo damages the very regulatory circuits that would later be used to update the compiled chunks.

**Adult repair is possible but bounded**: Subsequent secure relationships (adult therapy, long-term partners, close friendships with consistent co-regulation) can provide the missing compile context. Research on "earned secure" attachment (🟡 Roisman et al. 2002) shows that adults can move from insecure infant classification to secure adult functioning, but the earlier work was done with more recoverable Level 2-3 cases. Level 4 recovery requires much longer and more intensive intervention.

**Cross-reference**: [07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md](07-Social-Recognition-Arc.md) §7 expands emotional compile into full social-emotional binding including Resonance mechanisms. 07 covers the social-perception side; 06b covers the interoceptive side. Together they cover the full emotional compile arc.

## §6 — Cross-body-state synthesis

### §6.1 — Rate variance table

The 4 body states covered in 06b + bladder from 06a show **non-uniform ladder progression** that is **predicted by the compile mechanism formula** (4-channel × exposure × saliency × contingency × valence).

| Body state | Exposure | Saliency | Contingency | Peak valence | L4 onset (verbal) | Framework rank (compile rate) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bladder (06a) | Med (~10-15/day) | Moderate | Low→Med (toilet training age) | Moderate | ~22mo (E23) | Medium |
| Hunger (§2) | Med-high (~6-8/day with snacks) | High | High (contingent feeding) | High | ~18mo (E21) | Medium-fast |
| Pain (§3) | Low (peaks rare) | Very High | High (contingent comfort) | Very High | ~18-20mo (E22) | Fast-per-event |
| Thermal (§4) | Med | Low | Low (caregiver-managed) | Low | rarely L4 by 24mo | Slowest |
| Emotional (§5) | Continuous | Variable | Variable | Very Variable | 2-3yr+ verbal | Complex |

**Observations from the table**:

1. **Hunger reaches L4 earliest** (~18mo E21) among interoceptive states. Parameters: high contingency (parents respond quickly to hunger signals), high valence (hunger peaks are strongly unpleasant), moderate exposure (several events per day). The contingency × valence product is highest among the 5 states.

2. **Bladder reaches L4 slightly later** (~22mo E23). Parameters: moderate valence, lower contingency (caregivers manage via schedule primarily in early months, with higher contingency only at toilet training start ~18mo). The lower contingency explains the 4-month lag behind hunger.

3. **Pain reaches L4 around 18-20mo** with a wide range depending on body schema chunks compile rate. Pain has the **highest peak valence** but **lowest exposure** (peak events are rare) — so peak-per-event compile is fast but total compile time is gated by event rarity.

4. **Thermal does not reach L4 within F1 window**. Lowest contingency + lowest saliency combination. Predicts thermal L4 emergence ~2-4 years (outside F1 window).

5. **Emotional has the largest spread**. Different sub-states compile at different rates. Social-emotional (E12) at L2 at ~7 weeks (fastest L2 emergence) but verbal emotional productive is slowest (~2-3 years).

**Framework claim**: The rate variance is **not random**. It follows the 4-parameter formula predicted by the compile mechanism. High contingency + high valence + moderate-high exposure → fast ladder traversal. Low contingency + low valence → truncated traversal. This is the **strongest available empirical validation** of the Precondition-2 compile mechanism claim (Convergence Point 2 from [06a §6.3](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§6.3)).

#### §6.3.2 — Three cross-cutting observations

**Observation 1 — Imposed parent threat is amplified for earlier-compile states**

The earlier a state's compile window, the more the caregiver relationship serves as compile infrastructure rather than compile context. Emotional (0-12mo) compiles most entangled with caregiver; hunger (0-12mo) is second-most entangled because feeding events are connection events; bladder (12-30mo) is later and more separable. This creates a **gradient of imposed-parent amplification**:

```
                       Amplification of imposed-parent damage ↑
Emotional (0-12mo)     ████████████████  (maximum — substrate itself)
Hunger (0-12mo)        ████████          (high — feeding is connection)
Pain (0-24mo sporadic) █████              (moderate — caregiver mediates)
Bladder (12-30mo)      ████               (moderate — separable)
Thermal (Level 1 default) ██              (low — mostly not applicable)
```

This gradient predicts why the attachment literature finds the largest and most persistent effects (emotional) while the toilet-training literature finds moderate effects (bladder) — it is not that the mechanism is different but that the **compile-window overlap with L2 connection infrastructure** is different.

**Observation 2 — Same caregiver pattern produces different damage profiles across states**

A caregiver who operates at Level 3 (Heavy) applies similar behavior across multiple states (strict, shaming, emotionally-withdrawn during distress). But the **damage profile differs by state** because each state has different critical period + mechanism mix:

| State | Level 3 caregiver → this state's compile damage |
|---|---|
| Bladder | Specific chunk-association: bathroom anxiety, paruresis |
| Hunger | Interoceptive signal dismissal: alexithymic eating, eating disorders |
| Pain | Expression suppression: stoic pain reporting, somatic disorders |
| Thermal | Usually not engaged (caregiver manages thermal by default) |
| Emotional ⭐ | **Attachment-level damage affecting ALL other states** |

The emotional-state damage **amplifies the other states' damage** because emotional regulation is the substrate that would later help the adult update the other chunks. A Level 3 caregiver produces specific per-state damage AND compromises the emotional-regulation infrastructure that would later allow recovery.

**Observation 3 — Neural wear dimension compounds with per-state damage**

Per [06a §7.0.3 + §7.5](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§7.5), chronic threat cortisol in developing PFC produces Stage 2 accumulated wear. This wear is **additional to** the state-specific chunk association damage and **multiplicative with it**.

A child raised in chronic Level 3 context across multiple states receives:
- State-specific chunk association damage (per §6.3.1)
- Plus cumulative neural wear on developing PFC
- Plus degraded emotional regulation infrastructure (emotional Level 3)
- Plus compromised adult capacity to later update the damaged chunks (the PFC that would do the updating is itself compromised)

This is why ACE studies (🟢 Felitti et al. 1998) find dose-response relationships with cumulative childhood adversity rather than single-state specific effects — the damage compounds across states and across mechanisms.

## §10 — Open questions

### §10.1 — State-specific empirical questions

1. **Does differentiated cry (E11) onset timing correlate with subsequent L4 onset timing?** If the differentiation chunk compile rate predicts the full L4 compile rate, differentiated-cry-onset measurements could serve as early indicators for developmental compile rate variation.

2. **Can thermal L4 be accelerated by intentional caregiver practice?** The 4-parameter formula predicts that if caregivers deliberately increased contingency (e.g., labeling "are you feeling cold?" more explicitly, waiting for baby signals before adjusting temperature), thermal chunks should compile faster. Testable with intervention studies.

3. **Why does emotional verbal onset lag so much (2-3yr) compared to physical states (18-22mo)?** The framework attribution is "abstract labels + low contingency + lower-concrete-referent". Is there a mechanism to partially close this gap? Cultures with explicit emotional-mirroring practices (some Scandinavian, some Japanese attachment traditions) may show earlier emotional verbal onset — testable.

4. **Is body schema compile rate (required for E22 localized pain) independently observable?** If yes, body schema development could be tracked as its own substrate whose compile trajectory determines when pain chunks + body schema chunks + verbal labels can integrate.

### §10.2 — Cross-state framework questions

5. **Why does contingency dominate valence for some states (bladder) but valence dominates for others (pain)?** The 4-parameter formula is currently additive/multiplicative in aggregate but the relative weights may differ by state. A more refined formula would have state-specific weights derivable from observed compile rates.

6. **Is the H11 1:3 ratio really universal or are there outliers?** 5 states in 06a+06b all fit the ratio. 8+ states across human cultures might reveal exceptions. The ratio is proposed as framework claim with falsification test (find an outlier).

7. **Do chunks templates transfer across body states?** [06a §4.3](06a-Interoceptive-Bladder-Keystone.md#§4.3) documented E23 → E24 template transfer (bladder → bowel). Does the "hungry" template transfer to "thirsty" in similar fashion? Does "leg hurts" transfer to "arm hurts"? Systematic cross-state transfer study would illuminate chunk compositionality mechanism.

### §10.3 — Clinical / developmental application questions

8. **Can 06b cross-state analysis inform clinical assessment of developmental delays?** If a 24-month-old has L4 bladder + hunger but no L2 pain marker, what does that profile predict? Framework suggests it could indicate pain-specific chunks compile disruption (which has clinical relevance for pain management or alexithymia screening).

9. **Attachment-informed parenting frameworks** often emphasize emotional mirroring and "noting states". Does 06b framework provide a substrate-level explanation for why these practices work? (Answer: yes, by increasing contingency parameter in the 4-channel compile formula for emotional chunks). R-F1-7 update recommendation for Body-Parenting-Optimization.md should include cross-state compile rate optimization.
